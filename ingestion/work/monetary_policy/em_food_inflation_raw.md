EM Food Inflation: Upside Pressures Globally, But Heterogeneous Local Exposure
Food price shocks have historically been frequent and large across emerging markets
(EMs), more so than in developed markets (DMs). These shocks tend to be driven by a
combination of rising energy costs and restrictive trade policies (2007-2008 food
shock), exacerbated by severe weather events in major exporting regions (2010-2012
price spikes) or geopolitical fragmentation and supply chain bottlenecks (Russia’s
full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022).
Following the start of the Iran war, the sharp increase in oil prices and the accompanying
shortage of physical oil supply have rapidly passed through into diesel and fertilizer
markets – both of which are essential raw inputs in food production, with prices up by
around 50-60%yoy. Additionally, climate experts are warning of El Niño weather
patterns later this year, which are likely to translate into adverse conditions for
agriculture, therefore implying regional supply risks that may extend into 2027. The El
Niño effects this year are expected to be particularly severe given meteorological
predictions for a larger-than-usual increase in Pacific Ocean sea surface temperatures,
on top of an already record-warm planet. As a result, concerns around EM food inflation
have re-emerged.
In this EM Macro Themes, we examine the principal drivers of EM food inflation,
distinguishing between and statistically identifying global, regional and local factors
across countries. Using our modeling framework, we link movements in soft
commodities, fertilizer and diesel prices, and the US Dollar to globally driven food
inflation. We then present our view on where food inflation is likely headed and provide
a framework for identifying vulnerabilities across individual countries and regions. While
a detailed analysis of El Niño is outside the scope of this piece, we nonetheless present
some evidence from recent episodes on EM food inflation (see also our Commodities
Team’s recent commentary).
A Framework To Address the Challenges of Modeling Food Inflation
Food inflation is a key factor influencing inflation trends across countries, but the
complex interplay of global, regional and local drivers makes it difficult to model
effectively. Many essential foodstuffs – such as grains, oilseeds, dairy, meat, and sugar –
are traded on international markets, meaning that their prices are shaped by global
demand and supply factors, including worldwide weather patterns and other major
shocks. Conversely, a significant portion of foodstuffs – particularly seasonal produce –
is supplied domestically or regionally, making their prices more sensitive to local
conditions.
Moreover, net food trade positions vary meaningfully across both DMs and EMs (Exhibit
1). Accounting for regional factors is therefore important, as countries can drive a
meaningful share of the food price variation observed within their respective regions
through trade linkages with neighbouring economies. Further, at the local level,
exchange rate pass-through, domestic subsidies and price controls, VAT changes,
transport bottlenecks and idiosyncratic harvest outcomes can dominate the headline
print in any given quarter. Another layer of complexity is added by the seasonal variation
in the share of food that is locally sourced (e.g., northern hemisphere advanced
economies tend to rely on local supplies during summer but increase imports during the

EM Food Inflation Historically More Exposed to Surges
Historically, food inflation has been materially higher in EMs than in DMs, a pattern
driven by both structural factors and by idiosyncratic country-specific shocks (Exhibit 2).
In addition to this structural sensitivity, food carries a significantly larger weight in the
consumer inflation basket of EM economies than it does in DM economies (Exhibit 3,
27.9% of the CPI basket in EM vs. 14.5% in DM economies). Together, these two features
make EM headline inflation structurally more exposed to swings in food prices and, by
extension, to the global and regional shocks that move them.

The higher exposure of EM headline inflation to food price swings reflects several
reinforcing factors. First, EM consumers spend a much larger share of their disposable
income on food, mechanically raising food’s weight in the CPI basket – a pattern
consistent with Engel’s law, where lower-income households allocate a greater
proportion of spending to staples. Second, EM food supply chains tend to be less
diversified and more reliant on a narrower set of domestic producers, leaving them more
vulnerable to localized weather shocks and livestock diseases. Third, EM currencies are
typically more volatile against the USD, and since many key food inputs (fertilizers,
diesel, grains) are sourced internationally and USD-invoiced, exchange-rate
pass-through amplifies imported food inflation.

the marginal driver of food price changes is overwhelmingly the supply side: weather,

Soft Commodities, Diesel, and Fertilizers As the Main Global Drivers
Generally, the UN FAO soft commodities index has served as a relevant leading indicator
of global food inflation, given that grains, oilseeds, dairy, meat, and sugar collectively
capture the bulk of tradable food prices (Exhibit 4). However, in the 2022 inflation
episode, realized food CPI inflation across both DM and EM economies spiked by
considerably more than the FAO index would have predicted, reflecting in part the
global nature of the inflation shock at the time that extended to demand-side drivers as
well as to supply factors outside of commodity prices.

episodes when food inflation moves synchronously across regions – such as the 2008
commodity spike, the 2010-11 surge, and most notably the 2022 post-pandemic and

However, the explanatory power of the regional factors varies meaningfully across areas.
Our regional factors explain less than the global one does, partially due to the global

supply chains, the Euro area’s common currency, and limited reliance on administered

profile consistent with a small, open, food-importing region whose cycle tracks the
world but carries a recognisable local overlay from the 2022 energy and fertilizer shock

somewhat larger contribution from regional factors.
In Asia and Latin America, global and regional forces have roughly equal importance, yet
the local component remains most informative as it is driven by both weather-relevant
events and country-specific patterns.

Having extracted the global factor, we then regress it on a small set of observable global
price processes – the FAO soft commodities index, fertilizer and diesel prices, and the
broad USD – to identify which transmission channels best explain the common
component of global food inflation (details in Box 2). The dominant contributor is the
effective exchange rate, reflecting the Dollar-invoicing channel: a stronger Dollar
mechanically raises the local-currency cost of food and energy imports. Further, we find
that an increase in world soft-commodity prices today raises global food inflation two
quarters later, consistent with the six-month pass-through from world wheat, maize,
vegetable oil and dairy quotations to retail food CPI. Fertilizer prices matter, albeit with
a longer, three-quarter lag, as fertilizer feeds into food prices indirectly through the next
planting cycle. Finally, diesel also exerts inflationary pressure on the global factor, but
with a lower contribution and with a longer lag.
