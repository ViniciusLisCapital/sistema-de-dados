## Executive Summary

Food prices represent a major source of inflation volatility in emerging market (EM) economies, particularly in lower-income countries, where food accounts for 28% of the CPI basket in EMs versus 15% in developed markets (DMs). Food inflation not only comprises a large share of the CPI basket for EM economies but also exhibits higher volatility, with three major global shocks in the past two decades (2007, 2010, 2022) and risks of renewed shock in 2026 from ongoing supply disruptions resulting from the Iran war and El Niño-related concerns.

Food prices tend to be more supply-driven than other inflation components, given their sensitivity to agricultural conditions (often weather-related), global supply chains, and generally lower demand elasticity. Using a statistical approach, we identify and distinguish between global, regional, and local drivers of food inflation across 125 countries in our sample.

Our analysis suggests:
- Higher sensitivity to global factors in Europe (including CEE) and North America
- Roughly equal sensitivity to regional and global factors in Asia and Latin America
- Highest local exposure in Africa
- Mixed results in the Middle East

A simple framework mapping key inputs—soft commodity, diesel, and fertilizer prices, and the US Dollar—into the global factor suggests upward pressure of around 2 percentage points on global food inflation in H2 2026. However, the read-through is likely heterogeneous across countries, with local and regional supply factors as well as currency dynamics dominating in certain markets.

High exposure to the global factor may amplify food inflation risks in EMs versus DMs, with Ethiopia, Pakistan, Senegal, Malaysia, Ghana, and Morocco screening among the more exposed countries.

## Introduction

Food price shocks have historically been frequent and large across emerging markets (EMs), more so than in developed markets (DMs). These shocks tend to be driven by a combination of rising energy costs and restrictive trade policies (2007–2008 food shock), exacerbated by severe weather events in major exporting regions (2010–2012 price spikes), or geopolitical fragmentation and supply chain bottlenecks (Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022).

Following the start of the Iran war, the sharp increase in oil prices and the accompanying shortage of physical oil supply have rapidly passed through into diesel and fertilizer markets—both essential raw inputs in food production, with prices up by around 50–60% year-over-year. Additionally, climate experts are warning of El Niño weather patterns later this year, which are likely to translate into adverse conditions for agriculture, therefore implying regional supply risks that may extend into 2027. The El Niño effects this year are expected to be particularly severe given meteorological predictions for a larger-than-usual increase in Pacific Ocean sea surface temperatures, on top of an already record-warm planet. As a result, concerns around EM food inflation have re-emerged.

In this EM Macro Themes report, we examine the principal drivers of EM food inflation, distinguishing between and statistically identifying global, regional, and local factors across countries. Using our modeling framework, we link movements in soft commodities, fertilizer and diesel prices, and the US Dollar to globally driven food inflation. We then present our view on where food inflation is likely headed and provide a framework for identifying vulnerabilities across individual countries and regions. While a detailed analysis of El Niño is outside the scope of this piece, we nonetheless present some evidence from recent episodes on EM food inflation.

## A Framework To Address the Challenges of Modeling Food Inflation

Food inflation is a key factor influencing inflation trends across countries, but the complex interplay of global, regional, and local drivers makes it difficult to model effectively. Many essential foodstuffs—such as grains, oilseeds, dairy, meat, and sugar—are traded on international markets, meaning that their prices are shaped by global demand and supply factors, including worldwide weather patterns and other major shocks. Conversely, a significant portion of foodstuffs—particularly seasonal produce—is supplied domestically or regionally, making their prices more sensitive to local conditions.

Moreover, net food trade positions vary meaningfully across both DMs and EMs (Exhibit 1). Accounting for regional factors is therefore important, as countries can drive a meaningful share of the food price variation observed within their respective regions through trade linkages with neighboring economies. Further, at the local level, exchange rate pass-through, domestic subsidies and price controls, VAT changes, transport bottlenecks, and idiosyncratic harvest outcomes can dominate the headline print in any given quarter. Another layer of complexity is added by the seasonal variation in the share of food that is locally sourced (e.g., northern hemisphere advanced economies tend to rely on local supplies during summer but increase imports during winter months). The impact of local weather shocks on prices may, thus, in these instances be more short-lived than that of global shocks.

### Exhibit 1: Net Food Exporters Are Roughly Evenly Distributed Between EMs and DMs

![Exhibit 1 shows a chart displaying the distribution of net food exporters and importers, with values ranging from approximately -6 to +10 percent of GDP for various countries, roughly balanced between EMs and DMs]

To separate these layers cleanly, we estimate a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) factor model on a panel of food inflation rates across roughly 125 developed and emerging economies. We first isolate a single common series from the full panel—the one underlying pattern that captures as much of the shared movement as possible and best summarizes worldwide swings in food prices; this is our global factor. We then remove this global factor from each country's inflation series and divide countries into regional blocs—Western Europe, Central and Eastern Europe (CEE), Middle East, Asia, North America, South America, and Africa. Following the same logic, we obtain a second common pattern that captures the co-movement that is specific to a region but separate from the global cycle, the regional factor. What is left once both layers are stripped away is, by construction, the country-specific or local component.

This decomposition has two practical advantages. First, it is agnostic: we do not need to pre-commit to a particular basket of commodity prices or to a specific transmission channel, because the layers are drawn directly from the inflation data themselves. Second, the three components do not overlap, which makes the resulting variance decomposition—how much of each country's food inflation is global, regional, or local—clean and intuitive.

One challenge and modeling choice relates to regional classification, where we posit that geography—economic and physical—shapes some aspects of supply chain integration as well as common exposure to localized weather-related shocks. However, regions are disparate in terms of their geographic size as well as economic and supply chain integration. Our regional classification therefore has a significant bearing on the distinction between "regional" and "local" shocks in our framework. For tractability, we limit the regions that we define to the following seven: Europe, CEE, Africa, Middle East, North and South America, Asia. We acknowledge the limitations of this modeling choice and the fact that important sub-regional weather-related and other variation likely exists across some of these large blocs (notably, Africa and Asia).

## EM Food Inflation Historically More Exposed to Surges

Historically, food inflation has been materially higher in EMs than in DMs, a pattern driven by both structural factors and by idiosyncratic country-specific shocks (Exhibit 2). In addition to this structural sensitivity, food carries a significantly larger weight in the consumer inflation basket of EM economies than it does in DM economies (Exhibit 3, 27.9% of the CPI basket in EM vs. 14.5% in DM economies). Together, these two features make EM headline inflation structurally more exposed to swings in food prices and, by extension, to the global and regional shocks that move them.

### Exhibit 2: Historically Food Inflation in EM Has Been More Volatile Than in DM, But It Has Converged In The Last Decade

![Line chart showing median food inflation for EM and DM from 1983 to 2025, with EM showing higher volatility and peaks around 2008-2012, with convergence in recent years]

The higher exposure of EM headline inflation to food price swings reflects several reinforcing factors:

1. **Income effect**: EM consumers spend a much larger share of their disposable income on food, mechanically raising food's weight in the CPI basket—a pattern consistent with Engel's law, where lower-income households allocate a greater proportion of spending to staples.

2. **Supply chain diversification**: EM food supply chains tend to be less diversified and more reliant on a narrower set of domestic producers, leaving them more vulnerable to localized weather shocks and livestock diseases.

3. **Currency volatility**: EM currencies are typically more volatile against the USD, and since many key food inputs (fertilizers, diesel, grains) are sourced internationally and USD-invoiced, exchange-rate pass-through amplifies imported food inflation.

### Exhibit 3: At Low Levels of GDP per Capita, The Share Of Food in the CPI Basket is Very High

![Scatter plot showing relationship between GDP per capita (x-axis) and food CPI weight percentage (y-axis), with EM and DM countries marked separately, showing clear inverse relationship]

This supply-side taxonomy is particularly well-suited to food inflation because, unlike core services or non-essential components of CPI, food demand is highly inelastic—households cannot easily substitute away from staples even as prices rise. As a result, the marginal driver of food price changes is overwhelmingly the supply side: weather, input costs, geopolitical disruption, and currency-driven import costs. This is more the case in EMs than in DMs, given the comparatively lower services component and share of value added embedded in food prices, as well as the higher share of staple goods.

## Soft Commodities, Diesel, and Fertilizers As the Main Global Drivers

Generally, the UN FAO soft commodities index has served as a relevant leading indicator of global food inflation, given that grains, oilseeds, dairy, meat, and sugar collectively capture the bulk of tradable food prices (Exhibit 4). However, in the 2022 inflation episode, realized food CPI inflation across both DM and EM economies spiked by considerably more than the FAO index would have predicted, reflecting in part the global nature of the inflation shock at the time that extended to demand-side drivers as well as to supply factors outside of commodity prices.

### Exhibit 4: Lagged FAO Food Index Movements Lead Global Food Inflation, But Underpredicted the 2022 Surge Relative to Prior Shocks

![Dual-axis chart showing FAO Food Commodity Prices (LHS) and Global Food Inflation Median (RHS) from 2000 to 2026, with annotations showing prior episodes accounted for by soft commodity prices and the 2022 surge exceeding predictions]

The gap reflected the fact that 2022 was not only a soft-commodity shock but also—and arguably more importantly—a fertilizer and energy input cost shock, triggered by Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine and the resultant disruption to natural gas flows to Europe. Drawing on the lessons of the 2022 inflation surge and longer-term historical patterns, soft commodities, diesel, and fertilizers stand out as key global drivers of food inflation.

The current geopolitical backdrop highlights the importance of this channel. According to our Commodities team, roughly a quarter of global nitrogen fertilizer trade and about 20% of LNG—the primary feedstock for nitrogen production—typically transit the Strait of Hormuz. Consequently, any disruption to this shipping route simultaneously constrains global fertilizer availability and raises fertilizer production costs in alternative supply regions. As Exhibit 5 shows, diesel and fertilizer prices have quickly reacted to the Iran war, increasing inflationary pressure at the global level.

### Exhibit 5: Diesel and Fertilizers Also Lead Food Inflation

![Dual-axis chart showing Diesel (LHS), Fertilizer (LHS), and Global Food Inflation Median (RHS) from 2000 to 2027, with annotations indicating recent increases and leading effects]

That said, increases in fertilizer and diesel prices do not always pass through fully to food prices—particularly when soft commodities remain stable or when the price swings are less pronounced. To disentangle these effects, we model this relationship systematically later in the piece.

## Global and Local Factors Emerge As The Main Drivers

Having extracted the global factor using our PCA decomposition framework (described in detail in Box 1), we find that it performs strongly in tracking aggregate global food inflation dynamics (Exhibit 6), particularly during episodes when food inflation moves synchronously across regions—such as the 2008 commodity spike, the 2010–11 surge, and most notably the 2022 post-pandemic and Russia-Ukraine war shock.

### Exhibit 6: Our Global Food Factor Tracks Aggregate Global Food Inflation Well

![Line chart showing the Global Food Factor and Median Global Food Inflation from 2000 to 2026, demonstrating close alignment particularly during major shock episodes]

However, the explanatory power of the regional factors varies meaningfully across areas. Our regional factors explain less than the global one does, partially due to the global nature of agricultural trade and to the fact that countries' specific idiosyncrasies would be picked up by the local component. Nevertheless, some interesting patterns emerge.

### Exhibit 7: DMs and CEE are More Globally Synchronised, While Asia, Africa, Middle East and Latin America Are More Idiosyncratic

![Stacked bar chart showing variance decomposition across regions, with percentages attributed to global, regional, and local factors for Western Europe, CEE, North America, Asia, Latin America, Africa, and Middle East]

We find that Western Europe is the most globally synchronized bloc: more than half of food inflation variance is attributable to the global factor (Exhibit 7) and the global factor alone explains 81% of the regional aggregate—consistent with deeply integrated supply chains, the Euro area's common currency, and limited reliance on administered prices. Similarly, the CEE region's dynamics are dominated primarily by global trends—a profile consistent with a small, open, food-importing region whose cycle tracks the world but carries a recognizable local overlay from the 2022 energy and fertilizer shock and EUR pass-through. A similar profile emerges for North America, albeit with a somewhat larger contribution from regional factors.

In Asia and Latin America, global and regional forces have roughly equal importance, yet the local component remains most informative as it is driven by both weather-relevant events and country-specific patterns.

Similarly, in Africa, the largest share of the variation across regions is explained by local factors. This is consistent with the dominant role of domestic harvest cycles, sharp FX moves, conflict-related supply disruptions, and pervasive price administration, which together break the link with global commodity prices that the framework picks up elsewhere.

Finally, the Middle East evidence is more mixed (Exhibit 8). This reflects the region's structural peculiarities: GCC staples are heavily regulated and subsidized in country-specific ways (e.g., Saudi flour and bread subsidies, UAE strategic food reserves), while Israel has price controls on a basket of staples, making pass-through slow, partial, and politically timed in idiosyncratic ways. Further, intra-regional food trade is negligible, with virtually all wheat, rice, and edible oils sourced from the Black Sea, EU, North America, or Asia, leaving no channel for a regional cycle to form. Adding to this the heterogenous fiscal space between hydrocarbon exporters and net importers and different consumption baskets shaped by water scarcity and dietary differences, the result is a region where the global and local factors are the only systematic drivers.

## Expected Global Food Inflation Pressure to Build Up in 2026 H2

Having extracted the global factor, we then regress it on a small set of observable global price processes—the FAO soft commodities index, fertilizer and diesel prices, and the broad USD—to identify which transmission channels best explain the common component of global food inflation (details in Box 2). The dominant contributor is the effective exchange rate, reflecting the Dollar-invoicing channel: a stronger Dollar mechanically raises the local-currency cost of food and energy imports. Further, we find that an increase in world soft-commodity prices today raises global food inflation two quarters later, consistent with the six-month pass-through from world wheat, maize, vegetable oil, and dairy quotations to retail food CPI. Fertilizer prices matter, albeit with a longer, three-quarter lag, as fertilizer feeds into food prices indirectly through the next planting cycle. Finally, diesel also exerts inflationary pressure on the global factor, but with a lower contribution and with a longer lag.

### Exhibit 9: We Expect the Consequences of the Iran War To Increase Global Food Inflationary Pressure

![Dual-axis chart showing contributions from FAO Soft Commodity Prices, Fertilizer, Diesel, Broad USD, Residual, and fitted values/projections for the global food factor from 2001 to 2025]

We then use these estimates to trace the contribution of these variables to building food inflationary pressures. Assuming a gradual normalization of the prices of fertilizer and diesel over time, and given the elasticities estimated from our global-factor regression, the combination of higher fertilizer, soft commodities, and diesel prices, and a strong USD is consistent with a meaningful build-up in global food inflationary pressure over the coming quarters (Exhibit 9).

In our assessment, countries that exhibit both a high sensitivity to the global inflation factor and where food makes up a significant proportion of the consumer basket (Exhibit 10)—such as Ethiopia, Pakistan, Senegal, Malaysia, Ghana, and Morocco—are likely to experience the most pronounced pass-through to domestic inflation. By contrast, our analysis suggests lower risk for countries that exhibit a lower sensitivity to global factors.

### Exhibit 10: We Expect Economies With Higher Food Inflation Variance Correlated to the Global Factor to Be the Most Exposed to Inflationary Pressure

![Scatter plot showing relationship between percentage of variance explained by global factor (x-axis) and food CPI weight (y-axis), with EM and DM countries labeled, highlighting high-risk countries like Ethiopia, Nigeria, Bangladesh, and others]

## El Niño Presents Localized Rather than Global Upside Food Inflation Risks

As previously noted, additional upside pressure on food prices could also materialize as a result of El Niño-related effects. This climate phenomenon is characterized by sea surface temperatures in the tropical eastern and central Pacific Ocean rising 0.5°C above the long-term average, typically bringing warmer conditions and reduced precipitation (in India, South East Asia, Australia, Southern Africa, and northern parts of South America) that weigh directly on agricultural production. Climate experts are warning of El Niño weather patterns later this year, which would likely translate into adverse growing conditions and imply supply-side risks extending into 2027.

### Exhibit 11: In Aggregate There Is Little Correlation Between El Niño And Higher EM Food Inflation

![Chart showing EM median food inflation and shaded areas representing El Niño years from historical data, demonstrating lack of clear aggregate correlation]

### Exhibit 12: However, Countries in Southern Africa And South Asia Appear To Have Been More Exposed Historically

![Bar chart showing average food inflation relative to headline inflation during El Niño episodes (2015–2016 and 2023–2024) for selected countries, with Southern Africa and South Asian countries showing larger effects]

Historically, the 2015–2016 and 2023–2024 episodes stand out as particularly significant, given their sizeable impact on global temperatures and, by extension, on agricultural conditions and output. While in-depth analysis of the potential cross-country impact of El Niño on EM food inflation is outside of the scope of this piece, we nonetheless make two high-level points:

1. **Aggregate effect**: There is no clear evidence of the impact of El Niño episodes on aggregate EM food inflation (Exhibit 11). This reflects the fact that El Niño reduces precipitations in some regions but increases rainfalls elsewhere, and the effects on global production likely offset each other.

2. **Localized vulnerability**: Judging by the last two major episodes, the countries that experienced the largest effects on food inflation (in excess of headline) were predominantly in southern and eastern Africa and parts of Asia (Exhibit 12). Indeed, at the country level, the effects of El Niño on food prices can be considerably more pronounced there.

The most exposed economies fall into two broad categories: those geographically vulnerable to El Niño-driven drier weather, and those whose consumption baskets carry a heavier weight on soft commodities that have historically been disrupted during such episodes. Against this backdrop, the risk to food inflation in these vulnerable economies is firmly skewed to the upside, with the potential for sharp, localized price spikes should a strong El Niño materialize and compound existing supply pressures.

## Box 1: PCA-Based Decomposition of Food Inflation

We decompose quarterly year-on-year food CPI inflation across a broad cross-section of countries (developed and emerging) into three orthogonal components—a global factor common to all countries, a regional factor common to countries within the same geographic region, and a local (country-specific) residual. The procedure is a sequential two-stage Principal Component Analysis (PCA) followed by country-level OLS projection, in the spirit of dynamic factor models of Forni, Hallin, Lippi and Reichlin (2000), Bai and Ng (2002), and Stock and Watson (2002).

### Methodology

The input is food CPI levels for around 125 countries. A small list of countries with patently unreliable or hyper-inflationary series is excluded ex ante.

We extract the global component by applying PCA to a balanced panel of food inflation from 2000-Q1 onwards. We then regress each country's food inflation on a constant and the global component G<sub>t</sub>.

The residuals are by construction orthogonal to G and capture inflation variation that the global component does not explain. We then group countries by region and apply PCA on the within-region residual panel to extract a regional component (Exhibit 13).

Given G and the relevant R for country i's region, we run a final two-regressor OLS per country.

The country-specific loadings measure the sensitivity of country i's food inflation to the global and regional shocks, while the local component absorbs idiosyncratic developments—domestic supply shocks, exchange-rate pass-through unique to the country, weather, policy, etc.

### Rescaling Factors

A practical inconvenience of the PCA decomposition is that the extracted components are latent objects with no natural unit: they are simply principal-component scores, normalized to have zero mean and unit (or unit-relative) variance. To restore an inflation interpretation, we project the factors back onto observable inflation aggregates using a simple OLS regression. Specifically, to get the final global and regional factors, we estimate a regression where the dependent variable is either the cross-country mean of food inflation at the global level or regional level. The respective fitted values represent the global and regional factors in inflation equivalent terms.

## Box 2: Explaining the Global Factor

Having extracted, in a first stage, a single common factor G<sub>t</sub> from the cross-section of country-level food CPI inflation, we then investigate what observable global price processes account for the movement in this latent common factor.

### Transmission Channels

These four variables span the principal transmission channels emphasized in the agricultural-economics literature:

1. **World soft-commodity prices (FAO)**: Captures prices of key traded foodstuffs
2. **Input costs through energy-intensive fertilizer (Urea/DAP)**: Reflects production cost shocks
3. **Transport and processing costs (diesel)**: Captures logistics and energy costs
4. **Dollar-invoicing channel (USD)**: Mechanically affects local-currency import prices of food and fuel

### Lag Selection and Elasticities

The lag is chosen by AIC and BIC criteria, recognizing that pass-through from world prices to a cross-country common factor in CPI food inflation is neither instantaneous nor uniform across channels—fertilizers, in particular, are typically pre-bought one to two seasons ahead.

The coefficient on each driver should be interpreted as the elasticity of the common component of food inflation (an unobserved factor, not a single country's CPI) with respect to a one-percent year-over-year change in that driver, holding the others fixed at their respective optimal lags (Exhibit 14).
