n Fair value is an equilibrium concept, so some analysis is required before
determining how to incorporate these developments into spot FX views. It takes
roughly three years for fair-value-based FX forecasts to outperform a random
walk and roughly five years for spot to converge to fair value on average. This is
consistent with our “rule of thumb” that the real exchange rate tends to
converge to the underlying trend at a rate of roughly 20% per year. Beyond five
years, spot tends to overshoot fair value, although there is even more
uncertainty at this horizon.
n Looking across the G10, GSDEER forecasts perform best for AUD, EUR, CAD,
and NZD, and the model provides a “firm anchor” for GBP, JPY, and CHF. This
means that spot is less likely to have meaningful deviations from fair value, even
if exact spot dynamics are harder to forecast. We find that the inflation
component of GSDEER works well for EUR, GBP, and CAD, which is logical
because these are the countries with the strongest linkages with the US, and so
where the law of one price is most applicable. The terms of trade component is
naturally important for commodity exporters, although NOK is a notable (but
understandable) outlier.
n We see three key implications of our results. First, investors should take
seriously the stronger fair value estimate for AUD because it is a currency where
GSDEER forecasts spot well, especially when terms of trade are driving fair
value estimates. Second, weaker fair value estimates for EUR and GBP suggest
that we should not expect too much of a valuation-driven boost for these
currencies. Finally, it is advisable to discount the stronger NOK fair value
because this is driven by the terms of trade component, which is a poor
forecaster of medium-term FX moves for this cross.

GSDEER—A User’s Manual
There have been large swings in our G10 GSDEER fair value estimates over the last
three years. Big changes in commodity prices and inflation differentials have a direct
impact on the fundamental value of currencies (changes in productivity would also have
an impact, but this has not been as central of an issue lately). The surge in energy prices
has boosted Terms of Trade for commodity exporters, and in turn raised those
currencies’ ‘fair value’. This is especially true for NOK and AUD, where GSDEER has
strengthened by over 10% (Exhibit 1). And while inflation differentials have not been too
large yet given the common nature of the shock for most countries, there is scope for
differentials across countries and sectors to accumulate in coming quarters as central
banks face different trade-offs.

Take the Long Way Home
However, moving fair value estimates may not translate into spot dynamics for a while.
Even in the case of a currency such as Sterling, where fair value models work relatively
well, there can occasionally be large and persistent mis-valuations (Exhibit 2). In the
short-run, broad global macro variables and country-specific news will be more
important for FX and dominate the signal from equilibrium fair value concepts.

Pick the Best Driver
GSDEER performs differently across countries for two main reasons.2 First, data quality
is not the same everywhere and we have already highlighted the difference that inflation
measurement can make. Second, the model is also conceptually more appealing for
certain crosses. This is because GSDEER is essentially a PPP model adjusted for terms
of trade among G10 crosses where productivity differentials are not too large (Exhibit
12). As a result, GSDEER is not best equipped to capture fair FX value for countries
where international trade is relatively small and where limited linkages means that the
law of one price does not hold.

towards ‘fair’ with market forces.

Navigate the Next Turns
We see three key implications of our results.

Appendix: Explaining how we assess the performance of the GSDEER
model
We assess the forecasting performance of our GSDEER models using an out-of-sample
approach. The left panel below explains the methodology taking the case of evaluation
as of January 1995. We start by constructing the GSDEER time series at this date. Only
using the in-sample data, we estimate a regression to calculate the estimated change in
spot at various future horizons based on the level of mis-valuation at a given point in
time. We then use this relationship to estimate the change in spot at each horizon after
1995 (up to 10 years). We then repeat this process for each quarter, using an expanding
window for the in-sample data. Finally, we calculate the RMSE or R-squared using these
forecasts and the realized out-of-sample values. In contrast, the right panel shows how
an in-sample approach would differ. Here, we would regress the entire latest GSDEER
(2023Q2) on the history of realized data and then evaluate the predicted values. This
gives the GSDEER an advantage because, by construction, both series have the same
average value3.
