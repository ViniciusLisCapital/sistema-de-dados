A Primer on our GSDEER and GSFEER Models
GSDEER and GSFEER are our two formal FX valuation models, rooted in separate
academic theories of exchange rate fair value. In this FX in Focus, we provide a
refresher on the methodological background behind these two models, highlight
their different use cases and current signals, and update our analysis on their
historical performance. In our recently introduced 10-year-ahead FX forecasts, we
rely specifically on our GSDEER model because, as we outline here, it outperforms
GSFEER over longer horizons.

GSDEER fair value estimates are slower moving than GSFEER estimates as inputs do not
change significantly from quarter to quarter. Still, over a longer horizon, these fair values
can trend stronger or weaker depending on the underlying trends of the inputs. For
example, if inflation averages 3% in a country versus 2% in the US, that 1pp differential
will accumulate and have a compounded impact on the fair value over a longer horizon.
Consequently, we find that inflation differentials have had the largest contributions to
changes in our fair value estimates over the last 10 years (see Exhibit 2 here). For
example, our EUR/CHF fair value estimate has moved from close to 1.25 in 2014 to 1.02
now (and we expect it to fall further), mostly because of structurally lower inflation in
Switzerland relative to the Euro Area (Exhibit 2). On the other side, higher inflation rates
in many emerging markets have pushed fair value estimates weaker. For instance, our
USD/MXN fair value estimate has moved from below 14 to around 20 currently partly on
account of higher Mexican inflation vis-à-vis the United States (the Bank of Mexico has a
3% inflation target versus the Fed’s 2% target) (Exhibit 3).

The main implementation challenge for this type of valuation model is the choice of
inflation measure used to deflate the nominal exchange rate. There is no perfect
measure of FX-relevant inflation and different measures have their advantages and
drawbacks, as we have discussed extensively before. We also analysed this in the context
of our 10-year forecasts: higher inflation slippage historically is typically positively
correlated with how much inflation risk-premium is embedded in FX forwards.
While the two other inputs (productivity and terms of trade) have generally had smaller
contribution to fair value changes over the last 10 years, there are some important
cross-country differences. Productivity differentials have pushed most fair values
weaker versus the Dollar as US productivity growth has outperformed that of other
economies, but CNY is a notable exception, where relative productivity growth has been
exceptionally strong in recent years and is predicted to extend. Finally, terms of trade
have a greater contribution to fair values in commodity-exporter economies like AUD,
BRL or CLP.

Our GSFEER model is more cyclical than GSDEER: instead of modelling the empirical
relationship between real exchange rates and long-run drivers such as productivity and
terms of trade, it links the currency to its economy’s external and internal imbalances. In
particular, it estimates the likely outcome for the exchange rate given the gap between a
country’s current account and its ‘norm’, whilst also accounting for the country’s output
gap and for past FX moves. For example, if a country’s current account balance is too
low, the currency is likely to depreciate to facilitate the strengthening of the current
account back to its norm. The other two inputs — the output gap and past FX moves —
capture other macroeconomic shifts that can help or challenge this process (for
example, if a currency has already depreciated, it would usually take time before the
trade volumes reflect that; or shifts in the output gap can also help to bring the current
account back into equilibrium in addition to exchange rate shifts)1.
In these models, the current account ‘imbalance’ is measured as its deviation from a
theoretical ‘norm’ — where the norm is defined as the current account that would be
consistent with the country’s long-term savings and investment fundamentals. There is

different scenarios and over different time horizons. We see GSDEER as a
slower-moving, longer-run anchor whereas GSFEER estimates fluctuate more frequently.
Our GSFEER framework is especially useful when an economy is undergoing a structural
shift in its macroeconomic fundamentals which GSDEER is not able to capture (for
instance, GBP following the Brexit referendum, or many EMs in the aftermath of the
taper tantrum in 2013).
Finally, our near-term tactical views rely more heavily on our GSBEER model — which
relates currencies to other market variables — and the direction of risk sentiment or
monetary policy, whereas our more structural models become more relevant beyond the
one-year horizon.

The Roadmap — Slow and Steady Wins the Race
Our fair value estimates define the landing zone for exchange rates, but empirical
estimates of the pace of convergence are essential for determining the path of our
forecasts. We carry out two exercises on how our GSDEER and GSFEER fair value
estimates translate to a path for exchange rates. First, we update our prior work on how
to optimally weigh the two models to reach a ‘unified’ fair value for each cross. Second,
we estimate out-of-sample convergence rates across currencies for each model.
We think the key methodological differences between the two models that we discussed
in the previous section imply that the two sets of fair values can be complementary for
FX forecasting. We showed in the past that an in-sample empirical assessment of the
two models’ performance argued for a weighted-average approach, with a 60% weight
assigned to GSDEER, and a 40% weight to GSFEER. Repeating that same exercise today
in an out-of-sample setting, we find that an average loading on the two models has
shifted slightly, with a 70-30 split on GSDEER and GSFEER respectively, based on
multivariate regression results for 1-year real exchange rate returns (Exhibit 5).

While these results make a strong argument for attaching some weight to the GSFEER
model for 1-year ahead returns, that argument becomes slightly less clear for returns
over longer horizons. Shifting from a 1-year to a 10-year forecasting horizon, the implied
relative weight attached to GSFEER drops from 31% to 21% (Exhibit 6). We think this
drop-off in relative performance at longer horizons can be well-explained by the
differences between the two models we discussed earlier. In particular, GSFEER’s
reliance on more cyclical inputs such as current accounts and output gaps makes it less
applicable to horizons that typically span multiple cycles.

Across currencies though, these convergence rates can differ significantly, for three key
reasons. First, the quality of the data inputs can differ substantially, and we have
demonstrated in the past how different inflation inputs can significantly alter fair value
and convergence results. Second, economic openness, and other factors that may
impact the effectiveness of the PPP channel – such as the elasticity and complexity of a
country’s export basket – differ widely across countries. Across currencies, we find that
both models tend to act as better long-term FX anchors for countries with smaller and
more open economies. The equilibrating mechanism in both cases – the PPP-type
channel in GSDEER, and the FX move required to bring the current account in line with

Who’s ahead, who’s behind?
Even for currencies with relatively high convergence rates, we view fair value anchors as
gradually applying pressure on currencies, and as often being overwhelmed by cyclical
factors. Exchange rates can sustain and even extend large misvaluations for multiple
years, making a ‘valuation argument’ for directional FX expressions reliant on other
drivers pointing in the same direction. We believe this is why the Dollar has sustained its
high valuation for much of the last decade on a GSDEER basis, with the GSFEER signal
flipping to overvaluation in 2020. However, with the US economy no longer
outperforming to the same degree as before, we expect the Dollar’s overvaluation to
continue to erode. These models can provide a helpful signal on where there is greater
