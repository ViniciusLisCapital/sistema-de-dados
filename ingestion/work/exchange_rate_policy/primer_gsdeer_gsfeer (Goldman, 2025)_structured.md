## Executive Summary

GSDEER and GSFEER are our two formal FX valuation models, rooted in separate academic theories of exchange rate fair value. This FX in Focus provides a refresher on the methodological background behind these two models, highlights their different use cases and current signals, and updates our analysis on their historical performance.

**Key findings:**

- Strong evidence supports a weighted-average approach across the two models when applying fair value signals to 1-year real returns. However, over longer horizons, GSDEER increasingly outperforms, consistent with GSFEER being a more cyclical model.

- Annual convergence rates to GSDEER are close to the 20%/year 'rule-of-thumb' in the literature, but with significant variation across currencies, and generally higher in G10 than EM.

- More managed currencies feature lower convergence rates, including CNY, PEN, or CZK. These differences across models and convergence rates inform our relative preference for CZK over PLN in the CEE region.

- As we expect the Dollar's overvaluation to erode, these models can provide a helpful signal on where there is greater scope for Dollar depreciation and how fast that convergence could take place. AUD and KRW stand out as undervalued versus the Dollar in our models while historically having above-average convergence rates.

## Two Roads to Fair — The Tortoise and the Hare

### The GSDEER Model

Our GSDEER model is essentially an augmented purchasing power parity (PPP) model. It postulates that real exchange rates tend to mean-revert over time, but that differences in productivity and terms of trade will influence that underlying mean. The model is estimated through a regression approach with inflation, terms of trade and productivity differentials versus the US as the inputs.

**Key features:**

- The inflation differential has a one-to-one passthrough to fair value
- Coefficients for terms of trade and productivity differentials are smaller (0.35 and 0.28, respectively)
- With a regression approach, deviations from fair value will average to zero in-sample
- The country-specific constant is equivalent to the average exchange rate over the estimation sample
- Regime breaks are included in some EM currencies to account for shifts in currency regimes including peg-breaks

**Current valuation (2025Q4):** The Dollar appears around 15% overvalued. JPY, NOK and CNY have the largest undervaluation signal. CZK, GBP, and MXN are among the most overvalued (see Exhibit 1).

### Drivers of Fair Value Changes

GSDEER fair value estimates are slower moving than GSFEER estimates as inputs do not change significantly from quarter to quarter. Still, over a longer horizon, these fair values can trend stronger or weaker depending on underlying trends. For example, if inflation averages 3% in a country versus 2% in the US, that 1pp differential will accumulate and have a compounded impact on fair value over a longer horizon.

Inflation differentials have had the largest contributions to changes in fair value estimates over the last 10 years. For example, our EUR/CHF fair value estimate has moved from close to 1.25 in 2014 to 1.02 now (and we expect it to fall further), mostly because of structurally lower inflation in Switzerland relative to the Euro Area (see Exhibit 2). On the other side, higher inflation rates in many emerging markets have pushed fair value estimates weaker. For instance, our USD/MXN fair value estimate has moved from below 14 to around 20 currently, partly on account of higher Mexican inflation vis-à-vis the United States (the Bank of Mexico has a 3% inflation target versus the Fed's 2% target) (see Exhibit 3).

### Implementation Challenges

The main implementation challenge for this type of valuation model is the choice of inflation measure used to deflate the nominal exchange rate. There is no perfect measure of FX-relevant inflation and different measures have their advantages and drawbacks. We have also analysed this in the context of our 10-year forecasts: higher inflation slippage historically is typically positively correlated with how much inflation risk-premium is embedded in FX forwards.

While the two other inputs (productivity and terms of trade) have generally had smaller contribution to fair value changes over the last 10 years, there are important cross-country differences. Productivity differentials have pushed most fair values weaker versus the Dollar as US productivity growth has outperformed that of other economies, but CNY is a notable exception, where relative productivity growth has been exceptionally strong in recent years and is predicted to extend. Finally, terms of trade have a greater contribution to fair values in commodity-exporter economies like AUD, BRL or CLP.

### The GSFEER Model

Our GSFEER model is more cyclical than GSDEER: instead of modelling the empirical relationship between real exchange rates and long-run drivers such as productivity and terms of trade, it links the currency to its economy's external and internal imbalances.

**Key features:**

- Estimates the likely outcome for the exchange rate given the gap between a country's current account and its 'norm'
- Accounts for the country's output gap and past FX moves
- If a country's current account balance is too low, the currency is likely to depreciate to facilitate the strengthening of the current account back to its norm
- Output gap and past FX moves capture other macroeconomic shifts that can help or challenge the rebalancing process

**Defining the Current Account Norm:** In these models, the current account 'imbalance' is measured as its deviation from a theoretical 'norm' — where the norm is defined as the current account that would be consistent with the country's long-term savings and investment fundamentals. There is no universally accepted approach to estimating this norm. So just like with the inflation adjustment in GSDEER, underlying assumptions here will influence the results.

**Data challenges:** Current account data has been more volatile this year due to tariff effects and front-loading efforts. While we use a smoothing filter, there could be relatively large swings in these valuations from quarter to quarter.

### Comparing GSDEER and GSFEER Signals

The output of the GSFEER model is a trade-weighted real FX adjustment, which we can compare to the trade-weighted valuations under our GSDEER model (Exhibit 4). The valuation signal is consistent across both models for most currencies, including for the broad Dollar and for the significantly undervalued CNY, NOK and JPY. However, there are important distinctions:

- While BRL and CAD screen as undervalued under our GSDEER model, below-norm current accounts argue for some real trade-weighted depreciation under our GSFEER model
- The degree of undervaluation in SEK, ZAR, and CLP is substantially smaller in our GSFEER model

### Using Both Models

We use both models in shaping our FX views: each approach has its own merits in different scenarios and over different time horizons. We see GSDEER as a slower-moving, longer-run anchor whereas GSFEER estimates fluctuate more frequently. Our GSFEER framework is especially useful when an economy is undergoing a structural shift in its macroeconomic fundamentals which GSDEER is not able to capture (for instance, GBP following the Brexit referendum, or many EMs in the aftermath of the taper tantrum in 2013).

Finally, our near-term tactical views rely more heavily on our GSBEER model — which relates currencies to other market variables — and the direction of risk sentiment or monetary policy, whereas our more structural models become more relevant beyond the one-year horizon.

## The Roadmap — Slow and Steady Wins the Race

### Optimal Model Weighting

Our fair value estimates define the landing zone for exchange rates, but empirical estimates of the pace of convergence are essential for determining the path of our forecasts. We carry out two exercises on how our GSDEER and GSFEER fair value estimates translate to a path for exchange rates.

We think the key methodological differences between the two models imply that the two sets of fair values can be complementary for FX forecasting. We showed in the past that an in-sample empirical assessment of the two models' performance argued for a weighted-average approach, with a 60% weight assigned to GSDEER, and a 40% weight to GSFEER.

Repeating that same exercise today in an out-of-sample setting, we find that an average loading on the two models has shifted slightly, with a 70-30 split on GSDEER and GSFEER respectively, based on multivariate regression results for 1-year real exchange rate returns (Exhibit 5).

### Performance Over Longer Horizons

While the results in Exhibit 5 make a strong argument for attaching some weight to the GSFEER model for 1-year ahead returns, that argument becomes slightly less clear for returns over longer horizons. Shifting from a 1-year to a 10-year forecasting horizon, the implied relative weight attached to GSFEER drops from 31% to 21% (Exhibit 6).

We think this drop-off in relative performance at longer horizons can be well-explained by the differences between the two models. In particular, GSFEER's reliance on more cyclical inputs such as current accounts and output gaps makes it less applicable to horizons that typically span multiple cycles.

On account of its higher forecasting value over longer horizons, we developed our 10-year-ahead FX forecasts using our GSDEER framework specifically, and did not incorporate our GSFEER approach.

## Convergence Rates to Fair Value

### Overview

We present the 1-year real exchange rate convergence results of our GSDEER and GSFEER models in full in Exhibit 7. We believe these results support our long-standing convergence rule-of-thumb of 20% per year – particularly for G10 economies – that we developed in our prior work on the mean reversion properties of real exchange rates.

### Variation Across Currencies

Across currencies, convergence rates can differ significantly, for three key reasons:

1. **Data quality:** The quality of the data inputs can differ substantially. We have demonstrated how different inflation inputs can significantly alter fair value and convergence results.

2. **Economic openness:** Economic openness, and other factors that may impact the effectiveness of the PPP channel – such as the elasticity and complexity of a country's export basket – differ widely across countries. We find that both models tend to act as better long-term FX anchors for countries with smaller and more open economies. The equilibrating mechanism in both cases – the PPP-type channel in GSDEER, and the FX move required to bring the current account in line with its norm in GSFEER – operate more effectively for more open economies.

3. **Managed exchange rates:** Managed exchange rates can see limited or no convergence given the typical market forces driving convergence no longer apply, or at least not to the same extent as for free-floating currencies. For instance, CZK, CHF and PEN feature some of the lowest convergence coefficients.

## Who's Ahead, Who's Behind?

Even for currencies with relatively high convergence rates, we view fair value anchors as gradually applying pressure on currencies, and as often being overwhelmed by cyclical factors. Exchange rates can sustain and even extend large misvaluations for multiple years, making a 'valuation argument' for directional FX expressions reliant on other drivers pointing in the same direction.

We believe this is why the Dollar has sustained its high valuation for much of the last decade on a GSDEER basis, with the GSFEER signal flipping to overvaluation in 2020. However, with the US economy no longer outperforming to the same degree as before, we expect the Dollar's overvaluation to continue to erode. These models can provide a helpful signal on where there is greater scope for Dollar depreciation and how fast that convergence could take place.

### Currency-Specific Observations

Combining current valuation signals with our convergence analysis, we would note the following:

**AUD and KRW:** Two clear examples of currencies that are currently both deeply undervalued versus the Dollar in our models, and have also historically converged to fair relatively quickly.

**CNY:** It is not surprising that CNY features low convergence rates across our exercises as the typical market forces that drive convergence do not apply as strongly in the case of managed currencies. Still, the consistent undervaluation signal across the two models leaves us confident in our view of further Yuan appreciation from here.

**CZK vs PLN:** For similar reasons, we also discount the high overvaluation from the GSDEER model for CZK, where we find a near-zero convergence rate. The GSFEER model points to a smaller overvaluation signal here, including versus PLN, which also has higher convergence rates to fair. These differences inform our relative preference for CZK over PLN in the CEE region.

**BRL:** The GSFEER and GSDEER models provide directionally different signals for the currency, however historical convergence rates are quite low, leaving other factors like high carry or commodity terms of trade shifts to play a greater role.
