# Fiscal Policy — Bibliography Candidates

**Purpose:** first-pass candidate literature list for the fiscal policy agent, built following `BIBLIOGRAPHY_METHODOLOGY.md`. Nothing here has been acquired yet — this is the pre-acquisition step. Once a source is sourced into `repository/fiscal_policy/` (naming convention: `topic_description (Author, Year).pdf`), process it into a new `fiscal_policy_conceptual_map.md`, one source at a time, following the same pattern used for `exchange_rate_conceptual_map.md`. `repository/fiscal_policy/` already exists (created earlier, currently empty).

**Scope — deliberately does not re-acquire fiscal-monetary interaction theory already covered elsewhere.** Two other files already own significant fiscal-adjacent ground:
- `inflation_bibliography_candidates.md` cluster 7 (`#fiscal_theory_of_the_price_level`) owns the Fiscal Theory of the Price Level itself — Sargent & Wallace (1981), Leeper (1991), Woodford (2001).
- `monetary_policy_bibliography_candidates.md` cluster 7 (`#brazil_monetary_policy`) owns the applied Brazil fiscal-dominance pair — Blanchard (2004) and Favero & Giavazzi (2004).

This file owns fiscal policy **on its own terms**: debt sustainability arithmetic, fiscal multipliers, fiscal rules and institutions, pension/social-security economics, and — because it is a first-order feature of the Brazilian state — fiscal federalism and subnational debt. Where this file's own lens on fiscal dominance differs from the two above (Reinhart-Rogoff-Savastano's debt-intolerance framing, cluster 4), it's included because the angle is fiscal *capacity*, not monetary/price-level *determination*.

**Scope boundary with `economic_activity_bibliography_candidates.md`:** fiscal multipliers (cluster 2) are the one place this file and business-cycle theory sit closest together — multiplier size is state-dependent on the output gap (Auerbach & Gorodnichenko 2012) — but this file owns the fiscal-shock-identification side, while economic activity owns the output-gap measurement the multiplier size depends on.

**Foundational score:** 1-5, defined in `BIBLIOGRAPHY_METHODOLOGY.md` §c.

31 literature candidates across 8 clusters. As with every other topic in this series, Brazil-specific institutional material is unusually load-bearing — cluster 3 (fiscal rules) and cluster 6 (federalism/subnational debt) are both built around primary legal sources and applied assessments rather than imported theory, since Brazil's fiscal institutions (the Lei de Responsabilidade Fiscal, the spending cap and its 2023 replacement, the historically fraught state-level bailout problem) are distinctive enough to need direct documentation, not just a theoretical gloss. Cluster 8 widens the lens from Brazil alone to the region — added after a follow-up request — so Brazil's own fiscal behavior can be read against a recurring Latin American pattern (procyclicality) rather than in isolation.

## 1. `#public_debt_sustainability_and_dynamics` — the arithmetic of whether debt is sustainable, and when it stops mattering

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Why | Suggested filename |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Domar, E. (1944), "The 'Burden of the Debt' and the National Income," *American Economic Review* | Paper | 5 / 1944 | The founding debt-sustainability arithmetic — shows that whether debt-to-GDP stabilizes or explodes depends on the relationship between the interest rate, the growth rate, and the primary balance (the "r vs. g" comparison every later debt-sustainability discussion, including Blanchard 2019 below, still uses) | `burden_of_the_debt (Domar, 1944).pdf` |
| Bohn, H. (1998), "The Behavior of U.S. Public Debt and Deficits," *Quarterly Journal of Economics* | Paper | 5 / 1998 | The standard empirical test for fiscal sustainability — does the primary balance respond positively to the debt level (a "fiscal reaction function")? This test, or a close variant, is the workhorse approach for asking "is a given country's fiscal stance sustainable" empirically rather than just via the r-vs-g arithmetic | `behavior_us_public_debt_deficits (Bohn, 1998).pdf` |
| Blanchard, O. (2019), "Public Debt and Low Interest Rates," *American Economic Review* 109(4): 1197-1229 (AEA Presidential Address) | Paper | 5 / 2019 | The most influential recent re-litigation of whether high public debt is dangerous — argues that when safe rates run persistently below growth (r < g), debt rollover without future tax increases is feasible and may carry no fiscal cost, while still flagging welfare costs from reduced capital accumulation and multiple-equilibria risk (investors *believing* debt is risky can make it so). Essential background for reading any "is Brazil's debt sustainable" argument that invokes r vs. g | `public_debt_low_interest_rates (Blanchard, 2019).pdf` |

## 2. `#fiscal_multipliers` — how much a real of spending or a real of tax cut actually moves output

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Why | Suggested filename |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Blanchard, O. & Perotti, R. (2002), "An Empirical Characterization of the Dynamic Effects of Changes in Government Spending and Taxes on Output," *Quarterly Journal of Economics* | Paper | 5 / 2002 | The standard modern empirical identification strategy for fiscal shocks (SVAR with institutional/timing restrictions) — the direct methodological ancestor of almost every later fiscal multiplier estimate, including for Brazil | `empirical_characterization_fiscal_shocks (Blanchard & Perotti, 2002).pdf` |
| Ramey, V. (2011), "Can Government Purchases Stimulate the Economy?," *Journal of Economic Literature* | Paper | 5 / 2011 | The definitive survey of the multiplier debate post-2008 — surveys the wide range of multiplier estimates across methodologies and explains why they differ so much, essential context before citing any single multiplier number | `can_government_purchases_stimulate_economy (Ramey, 2011).pdf` |

## 4. `#fiscal_capacity_debt_intolerance_and_dominance` — why EM sovereigns hit fiscal limits at debt levels DM sovereigns don't

**Distinct from, not a duplicate of,** the FTPL/fiscal-dominance theory already in `inflation_bibliography_candidates.md` cluster 7 and the applied Brazil pair in `monetary_policy_bibliography_candidates.md` cluster 7 — this cluster's lens is fiscal *capacity itself*: why the same debt-to-GDP ratio that's unremarkable for a reserve-currency issuer can trigger a crisis for an emerging market.

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Why | Suggested filename |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Reinhart, C., Rogoff, K. & Savastano, M. (2003), "Debt Intolerance," *Brookings Papers on Economic Activity* (also NBER Working Paper 9908) | Paper | 5 / 2003 | The founding statement of "debt intolerance" — many emerging markets face default risk and loss of market access at debt-to-GDP levels far below what advanced economies routinely sustain, because of a history of default and weaker institutional credibility, not the debt ratio alone. Essential framing for any argument about "how much debt can Brazil actually carry" that isn't just Domar/Blanchard arithmetic transplanted from a reserve-currency context | `debt_intolerance (Reinhart, Rogoff & Savastano, 2003).pdf` |

**Open gap, not yet filled:** the historical companion literature on Brazil's own specific debt-intolerance/fiscal-dominance episodes (the 2002 election-driven confidence crisis already covered from the exchange-rate/credibility angle in `monetary_policy_bibliography_candidates.md` cluster 7's Minella et al. 2003, and the 2015-16 crisis, cluster 7 below) is the applied complement this theoretical paper needs — flagged here rather than duplicated.

## Suggested acquisition order

1. **Domar (1944)** and **Blanchard (2019)** — the debt-sustainability-arithmetic pair; read together since Blanchard's whole argument is a modern re-litigation of exactly the r-vs-g comparison Domar first formalized.
2. **Lei Complementar 101/2000 (LRF)**, **EC 95/2016 (Teto de Gastos)**, and **LC 200/2023 (Novo Arcabouço Fiscal)** — read as a sequence; Brazil's fiscal-rule history only makes sense read chronologically as one rule replacing the last.
3. **Reinhart, Rogoff & Savastano (2003)** — cheap, short (relative to a full book), and the single most important corrective to naively applying Blanchard (2019)'s DM-focused r-vs-g comfort to an EM context.
4. **Blanchard & Perotti (2002)** — closes the fiscal-multiplier methodology gap before either Ramey's survey or Auerbach-Gorodnichenko's state-dependence result will make full sense.
5. **Gavin & Perotti (1997)** — read alongside Reinhart-Rogoff-Savastano (2003) above; procyclicality and debt intolerance are the two empirical regularities most responsible for why EM/Latin American fiscal policy doesn't behave like the Domar/Blanchard (2019) arithmetic, calibrated against reserve-currency issuers, would predict.
6. Everything else, roughly in cluster order — the Barr & Diamond (2009), Oates (1972)/Rodden (2006), and Klemm (n.d.) book/chapter items are lower urgency, useful depth rather than foundational; the four flagged open gaps (Novo Arcabouço's peer-reviewed assessment, EC 103/2019's ex-post fiscal impact, Brazil's specific 2015-16/subnational crisis narratives, and a cleanly-citable Latin America subnational federalism paper) need a dedicated search pass before anything can be acquired against them.
