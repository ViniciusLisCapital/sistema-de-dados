# Inflation — Bibliography Candidates

**Purpose:** first-pass candidate literature list for the inflation agent, built following `BIBLIOGRAPHY_METHODOLOGY.md`. Nothing here has been acquired yet — this is the pre-acquisition step. Once a source is sourced into `repository/inflation/` (naming convention: `topic_description (Author, Year).pdf`), process it into a new `inflation_conceptual_map.md`, one source at a time, following the same pattern used for `exchange_rate_conceptual_map.md`.

**Scope and boundary with `monetary_policy_bibliography_candidates.md`:** the two agents split the same broad territory by lens, not by keyword — inflation targeting *as an institutional regime* (rules, credibility, central bank design, the NK 3-equation transmission mechanism) stays in monetary policy; **this file covers the inflation process itself** — what actually drives the price level and its rate of change, how expectations form, how shocks propagate and get measured, and how disinflation has actually happened (or failed to) in Brazil and elsewhere. Where a classic source is genuinely foundational to both (Friedman 1968 was acquired first under monetary policy), it is cross-referenced here rather than re-acquired — see the note under cluster 1.

**Foundational score:** 1-5, defined in `BIBLIOGRAPHY_METHODOLOGY.md` §c. Shown next to publication year so canonicity and recency stay distinct.

37 literature candidates across 8 clusters, seven of them confirmed Banco Central do Brasil Working Papers (added 2026-07 after a verification pass against the BCB's actual publication list, rather than from memory). Brazil-specific material is unusually load-bearing for this topic — clusters 4 and 5 exist specifically because Brazil's own inertial-inflation theory and Real Plan disinflation are original contributions to the global literature, not just local applications of imported theory (per methodology §a's discretion note); clusters 1, 2, and 6 now each carry at least one BCB working paper applying the international theory directly to Brazilian data. Cluster 1 also now covers the full 2008-2020 "low-flation" decade as its own arc (global-slack explanation, missing disinflation, missing inflation, and the eventual "flat but not dead" verdict) between the 2008 GFC and the 2021-23 surge in cluster 8, rather than jumping straight from the flattening observation to the pandemic.

## 1. `#phillips_curve_and_expectations_augmentation` — the core theoretical relationship between slack and inflation

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Why | Suggested filename |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Phillips, A.W. (1958), "The Relation between Unemployment and the Rate of Change of Money Wage Rates in the United Kingdom, 1861–1957," *Economica* | Paper | 5 / 1958 | The original empirical curve — starting point for the entire literature, including its own eventual refutation | `original_phillips_curve (Phillips, 1958).pdf` |

| Phelps, E. (1967), "Phillips Curves, Expectations of Inflation and Optimal Unemployment over Time," *Economica* | Paper | 5 / 1967 | Co-discoverer (with Friedman) of the expectations-augmented Phillips curve and the natural rate concept — the theoretical rupture that made the original curve's apparent policy trade-off illusory | `expectations_augmented_phillips_phelps (Phelps, 1967).pdf` |

| Lucas, R. (1976), "Econometric Policy Evaluation: A Critique," *Carnegie-Rochester Conference Series on Public Policy* | Paper | 5 / 1976 | The Lucas critique, developed largely through the Phillips curve example — explains *why* the naive statistical trade-off breaks down when policy exploits it, essential background for reading any later Phillips curve work critically | `lucas_critique (Lucas, 1976).pdf` |

| Gali, J. & Gertler, M. (1999), "Inflation Dynamics: A Structural Econometric Analysis," *Journal of Monetary Economics* | Paper | 5 / 1999 | The empirical New Keynesian Phillips curve (NKPC) paper — estimates the hybrid backward/forward-looking specification that remains the workhorse for inflation dynamics research; distinct from Clarida-Galí-Gertler (1999, in monetary policy), which surveys the policy-rule side rather than estimating the inflation equation itself | `nkpc_structural_estimation (Gali & Gertler, 1999).pdf` |

| Blanchard, O. (2016), "The Phillips Curve: Back to the '60s," *American Economic Review Papers and Proceedings* | Paper | 4 / 2016 | Documents the post-2008 flattening of the curve and argues the relationship has reverted toward its pre-1970s low-slope, low-persistence form — the direct setup for the "missing disinflation" and "missing inflation" puzzles below | `phillips_curve_back_to_60s (Blanchard, 2016).pdf` |

| Borio, C. & Filardo, A. (2007), "Globalisation and Inflation: New Cross-Country Evidence on the Global Determinants of Domestic Inflation," BIS Working Paper No. 227 | Paper | 4 / 2007 | The structural explanation underlying much of the 2010s low-inflation commentary: argues *global* slack (import competition, global value chains), not domestic slack, is what disciplines prices — a leading candidate answer for why the curve looks flat from a purely domestic-variables perspective | `globalisation_and_inflation (Borio & Filardo, 2007).pdf` |

| International Monetary Fund (2013), "The Dog That Didn't Bark: Has Inflation Been Muzzled or Was It Just Sleeping?," *World Economic Outlook*, April 2013, Chapter 3 | Paper (institutional report chapter) | 4 / 2013 | The earliest, most influential institutional statement of the missing-disinflation puzzle — argues anchored expectations explain why inflation didn't fall as much as pre-2008 models predicted despite the Great Recession's huge output gaps; predates and complements Coibion & Gorodnichenko (2015) below | `dog_that_didnt_bark (IMF WEO, 2013).pdf` |

| Hooper, P., Mishkin, F. & Sufi, A. (2019), "Prospects for Inflation in a High Pressure Economy: Is the Phillips Curve Dead or Is It Just Hibernating?," NBER Working Paper 25792 | Paper | 5 / 2019 | The most comprehensive later verdict on the full 2008-2019 flattening episode — covers both the 2009-11 "missing disinflation" and the mirror-image 2015-19 "missing inflation" (unemployment at multi-decade lows with no inflation acceleration), concluding the curve is very flat but not dead. The single best entry point if only one source from this stretch is read | `phillips_curve_dead_or_hibernating (Hooper, Mishkin & Sufi, 2019).pdf` |

| Coibion, O. & Gorodnichenko, Y. (2015), "Is the Phillips Curve Alive and Well After All? Inflation Expectations and the Missing Disinflation," *American Economic Journal: Macroeconomics* | Paper | 4 / 2015 | Resolves the 2009-11 "missing disinflation" puzzle by showing standard Phillips curve estimates use the wrong expectations measure — directly bridges to cluster 2 | `missing_disinflation (Coibion & Gorodnichenko, 2015).pdf` |

| Alves, S.A.L. & Areosa, W.D. (2005), "Targets and Inflation Dynamics," Banco Central do Brasil Working Paper No. 100 | Paper | 4 / 2005 | Derives a Brazil-specific NKPC with indexation to both past inflation *and* the inflation target itself, generalizing Woodford's (2003) hybrid curve — the direct Brazilian counterpart to Gali & Gertler (1999) above, built for a regime (explicit inflation targets) the original NKPC wasn't designed around | `targets_and_inflation_dynamics (Alves & Areosa, 2005).pdf` |

| da Gama Machado, V. & Portugal, M.S. (2014), "Phillips Curve in Brazil: An Unobserved Components Approach," Banco Central do Brasil Working Paper No. 354 | Paper | 3 / 2014 | A later, methodologically distinct (unobserved-components) Brazilian Phillips curve estimate — useful as a robustness comparison against Alves & Areosa's structural NKPC approach | `phillips_curve_brazil_unobserved_components (da Gama Machado & Portugal, 2014).pdf` |

**Cross-reference, not re-acquired:** Friedman, M. (1968), "The Role of Monetary Policy," *American Economic Review* — already acquired under `monetary_policy/` (the natural rate hypothesis is this cluster's other founding half alongside Phelps 1967); treat it as shared infrastructure rather than duplicating the PDF.

## 4. `#brazilian_inertial_inflation_theory` — Brazil's own original contribution to inflation theory

**Why this cluster exists on its own:** unlike most of this bibliography, inertial inflation theory was developed by Brazilian economists to explain a phenomenon (chronic indexation-driven inflation with no clear demand or supply trigger) that mainstream imported theory did not handle well — it is one of the few cases where Brazilian applied macro produced genuinely original theory rather than a local application of a foreign model.

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Why | Suggested filename |
|---|---|---|---|---|

| Arida, P. & Resende, A.L. (1985), "Inertial Inflation and Monetary Reform: Brazil," in Williamson, J. (ed.), *Inflation and Indexation: Argentina, Brazil, and Israel*, Institute for International Economics | Paper (book chapter) | 5 / 1985 | THE founding statement of inertial inflation theory and the "monetary reform" (moeda indexada) proposal that directly inspired the Real Plan's URV mechanism a decade later | `inertial_inflation_monetary_reform (Arida & Resende, 1985).pdf` |

| Modiano, E. (1988), "A Ópera dos Três Cruzados: 1985-1989," in Abreu, M.P. (ed.), *A Ordem do Progresso: Cem Anos de Política Econômica Republicana*, Editora Campus | Paper (book chapter) | 4 / 1988 | The standard retrospective synthesis of the indexation era and the three failed stabilization plans (Cruzado, Bresser, Verão) that preceded the Real Plan — necessary context for why the Real Plan's designers built the URV the way they did | `opera_tres_cruzados (Modiano, 1988).pdf` |

| Simonsen, M.H. (1970), "Inflação: Gradualismo x Tratamento de Choque," APEC (also in later *Revista Brasileira de Economia* reprints) | Paper | 3 / 1970 | Earlier, pre-inertial-theory statement of the gradualism-vs-shock debate that inertial theory would later reframe — useful for tracing the intellectual lineage rather than as a standalone reference | `gradualismo_tratamento_choque (Simonsen, 1970).pdf` |

## 7. `#fiscal_theory_of_the_price_level` — inflation as a fiscal, not only monetary, phenomenon

**Bridges to, but is theoretically distinct from,** monetary policy's `#brazil_monetary_policy` cluster (Blanchard 2004, Favero & Giavazzi 2004 on fiscal dominance) — those are applied Brazil pieces; this cluster is the underlying formal theory of price-level determination under fiscal constraints.

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Why | Suggested filename |
|---|---|---|---|---|

| Sargent, T. & Wallace, N. (1981), "Some Unpleasant Monetarist Arithmetic," Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis Quarterly Review | Paper | 5 / 1981 | The founding result: under a binding fiscal constraint, tighter money today can mean *more* money creation (and inflation) later — the direct theoretical ancestor of the fiscal dominance concept used throughout Brazil-specific monetary policy analysis | `unpleasant_monetarist_arithmetic (Sargent & Wallace, 1981).pdf` |

| Leeper, E. (1991), "Equilibria under 'Active' and 'Passive' Monetary and Fiscal Policy," *Journal of Monetary Economics* | Paper | 5 / 1991 | Formalizes the active/passive policy-mix taxonomy that determines whether the price level is pinned down by monetary or fiscal policy — the standard modern vocabulary (still used in BCB/market commentary on "fiscal dominance risk") comes from this paper | `active_passive_policy_mix (Leeper, 1991).pdf` |

| Woodford, M. (2001), "Fiscal Requirements for Price Stability," *Journal of Money, Credit and Banking* | Paper | 4 / 2001 | Accessible synthesis and defense of the Fiscal Theory of the Price Level (FTPL) from monetary policy's leading NK theorist — useful bridge between the FTPL literature and the NK apparatus already in `monetary_policy_bibliography_candidates.md` | `fiscal_requirements_price_stability (Woodford, 2001).pdf` |

## Suggested acquisition order

1. **Arida & Resende (1985)** and **Franco (1995)** — the two-paper core of Brazil's original theoretical contribution and its main practical application; nothing else in this bibliography is as distinctively Brazil-specific or as directly load-bearing for explaining the Real Plan to a reader.
2. **da Gama Machado (2024)** and **Bryan & Cecchetti (1994)** — closes the measurement-methodology gap with both the general theory and BCB's own current, operative núcleo methodology; read together since the 2024 paper only makes sense against the trimmed-mean logic the 1994 paper establishes.
3. **Gali & Gertler (1999)** and **Coibion & Gorodnichenko (2015)**, then **Alves & Areosa (2005)** and **da Gama Machado & Portugal (2014)** — the international NKPC pair followed immediately by their two Brazilian empirical counterparts, so the general theory is fresh when reading the local estimates.
4. **Ball, Leigh & Mishkin (2022)** and **Bernanke & Blanchard (2023)** — most likely to be immediately useful for current LIS analysis given the recency of the episode they cover.
5. **Bevilaqua, Mesquita & Minella (2007)** and **Gaglianone (2017)** — the two Brazil-specific expectations-anchoring references, read after cluster 2's international theory (Mankiw & Reis, Sims, Coibion-Gorodnichenko-Kamdar) rather than before it.
6. Everything else, roughly in cluster order — the Ha/Kose/Ohnsorge (2019) book chapters, Figueiredo & Fachada (2002) (now superseded by da Gama Machado 2024 but still useful as a historical baseline), and Areosa (2016) are lower urgency.
