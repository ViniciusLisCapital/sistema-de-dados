# Labor Market — Bibliography Candidates

**Purpose:** first-pass candidate literature list for the labor market agent, built following `BIBLIOGRAPHY_METHODOLOGY.md`. Nothing here has been acquired yet — this is the pre-acquisition step. Once a source is sourced into `repository/labor_market/` (naming convention: `topic_description (Author, Year).pdf`), process it into a new `labor_market_conceptual_map.md`, one source at a time, following the same pattern used for `exchange_rate_conceptual_map.md`.

**Scope — the split confirmed in `economic_activity_bibliography_candidates.md`.** That file explicitly reserved search/matching models, the Beveridge curve, and NAIRU estimation for this agent, in exchange for keeping business-cycle theory and growth theory on the activity side. This file owns: how unemployment and vacancies coexist and interact (search/matching theory, the Beveridge curve), how wages get set (bargaining, the wage curve, minimum wage), what "full employment" even means (NAIRU/natural rate), and — because it is unusually consequential for Brazil specifically — informality and dual labor markets.

**Scope boundary with `inflation_bibliography_candidates.md`:** the Phillips curve itself (the *price* side of the slack-inflation relationship, its estimation, the missing-inflation/disinflation debates) stays in inflation's cluster 1 — this file owns the *labor market* mechanics that feed into it (unemployment gap, NAIRU, wage-setting), the same division already established between inflation and economic activity for the output gap. Wage and wage-bill data itself (`macro_brasil.mt_pnad`) is already documented from the wage-price-spiral angle in `inflation_data_inventory.md` §6 — this agent would own that table, with inflation as a consumer, mirroring the reciprocal relationship documented between economic activity and monetary policy.

**Scope boundary with `economic_activity_bibliography_candidates.md`:** business-cycle theory (RBC, DSGE), growth theory, and output-gap/potential-output measurement all stay there — this file's search/matching and NAIRU clusters are the labor-market-side companion to that file's output-gap cluster (5), not a substitute for it. The "hours worked" component of potential growth (bibliography cluster 9 there) is a Tier 2 dependency that file already lists as needed *from* this agent.

**Foundational score:** 1-5, defined in `BIBLIOGRAPHY_METHODOLOGY.md` §c.

30 literature candidates across 8 clusters. As with the other topics, Brazil-specific material is unusually load-bearing here — cluster 7 (`#informality_dual_labor_markets_and_development`) exists because informality is not a peripheral feature of the Brazilian labor market but close to half of it, and two of the field's most-cited informality papers (Ulyssea 2018, Meghir-Narita-Robin 2015) are Brazil-specific structural estimates, not international theory applied secondhand.

## 1. `#search_matching_theory_foundations` — the DMP framework: why unemployment and vacancies coexist

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Why | Suggested filename |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Diamond, P. (1982), "Wage Determination and Efficiency in Search Equilibrium," *Review of Economic Studies* | Paper | 5 / 1982 | One of the three founding papers of search-and-matching theory (with Mortensen and Pissarides below) — search frictions mean unemployment and job vacancies can coexist in equilibrium, unlike in a Walrasian labor market | `wage_determination_search_equilibrium (Diamond, 1982).pdf` |
| Mortensen, D. (1982), "Property Rights and Efficiency in Mating, Racing, and Related Games," *American Economic Review* | Paper | 5 / 1982 | The second founding contribution — formalizes the matching function and the bargaining problem over the surplus created by a match, the core building block of the whole DMP apparatus | `property_rights_efficiency_matching (Mortensen, 1982).pdf` |
| Pissarides, C. (1985), "Short-Run Equilibrium Dynamics of Unemployment, Vacancies, and Real Wages," *American Economic Review* | Paper | 5 / 1985 | The third founding contribution and the one that gives the framework its clearest empirical hook — the Beveridge curve (cluster 2) falls directly out of this model's equilibrium | `short_run_equilibrium_unemployment_vacancies (Pissarides, 1985).pdf` |
| Mortensen, D. & Pissarides, C. (1994), "Job Creation and Job Destruction in the Theory of Unemployment," *Review of Economic Studies* | Paper | 5 / 1994 | Extends the static DMP model into a full job-creation/job-destruction dynamic framework — the standard reference for modeling gross (not just net) labor market flows | `job_creation_job_destruction (Mortensen & Pissarides, 1994).pdf` |
| Pissarides, C. (2000, 2nd ed.), *Equilibrium Unemployment Theory*, MIT Press — **book, get relevant chapters** | Book | 5 / 2000 | The standard graduate textbook synthesizing the DMP framework — Diamond, Mortensen and Pissarides jointly won the 2010 Nobel largely on the strength of this research program. ## 3. `#nairu_and_natural_rate_of_unemployment` — what "full employment" means, and how imprecisely it can be estimated

**Cross-reference, not re-acquired:** Friedman, M. (1968), "The Role of Monetary Policy" — already acquired under `monetary_policy/` and cross-referenced in `inflation_bibliography_candidates.md` cluster 1 as the natural-rate hypothesis's founding statement. This cluster covers the *estimation* problem the natural rate concept creates, not its original theoretical statement.

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Why | Suggested filename |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Staiger, D., Stock, J. & Watson, M. (1997), "How Precise Are Estimates of the Natural Rate of Unemployment?," in *Reducing Inflation: Motivation and Strategy*, University of Chicago Press | Paper (NBER conference volume chapter) | 5 / 1997 | The definitive statement of just how wide the confidence intervals around any NAIRU estimate actually are — essential caution before treating any single NAIRU number (Brazilian or otherwise) as precise | `precision_natural_rate_estimates (Staiger, Stock & Watson, 1997).pdf` |
| Gordon, R. (1997), "The Time-Varying NAIRU and Its Implications for Economic Policy," *Journal of Economic Perspectives* | Paper | 5 / 1997 | Argues the NAIRU itself moves over time rather than being a fixed structural constant — directly relevant to reading Brazil's own apparent NAIRU decline since the 2017 labor reform (cluster 8) as a real structural shift rather than noise | `time_varying_nairu (Gordon, 1997).pdf` |
| Ball, L. & Mankiw, N.G. (2002), "The NAIRU in Theory and Practice," *Journal of Economic Perspectives* | Paper | 4 / 2002 | Accessible synthesis of the theoretical status and practical estimation problems of the NAIRU a decade after Staiger-Stock-Watson — good bridge piece between the pure theory and the applied Brazilian estimate below | `nairu_theory_and_practice (Ball & Mankiw, 2002).pdf` |
| Borges, B. ## 4. `#unemployment_volatility_and_wage_rigidity` — why the basic DMP model doesn't move enough, and how to fix it

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Why | Suggested filename |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Shimer, R. (2005), "The Cyclical Behavior of Equilibrium Unemployment and Vacancies," *American Economic Review* | Paper | 5 / 2005 | The "Shimer puzzle" — shows the basic DMP model, calibrated to match average labor market flows, generates far too little cyclical volatility in unemployment and vacancies relative to the data. The single most influential critique the search-and-matching program has faced | `cyclical_behavior_unemployment_vacancies (Shimer, 2005).pdf` |
| Hall, R. (2005), "Employment Fluctuations with Equilibrium Wage Stickiness," *American Economic Review* | Paper | 5 / 2005 | The standard proposed fix — if wages are sticky rather than continuously re-bargained via Nash bargaining, the DMP model can generate realistic volatility. Published in the same AER issue as Shimer's critique, effectively as its direct answer | `employment_fluctuations_wage_stickiness (Hall, 2005).pdf` |
| Pissarides, C. (2009), "The Unemployment Volatility Puzzle: Is Wage Stickiness the Answer?," *Econometrica* | Paper | 4 / 2009 | Pissarides's own later assessment of the Shimer-Hall debate — argues the puzzle is more about wage stickiness specifically for *new hires* than for the stock of existing matches, refining rather than simply endorsing Hall's fix | `unemployment_volatility_puzzle (Pissarides, 2009).pdf` |

## 6. `#minimum_wage_theory_and_evidence` — does it cost jobs, and under what conditions

**Brazil relevance note:** the minimum wage (salário mínimo) plays an outsized role in Brazil relative to most economies, since it's also the indexing base for a large share of pension and social-benefit payments (Previdência, BPC) — its labor-market effects and its fiscal effects are unusually entangled here, which cluster 8 picks up on the institutional side.

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Why | Suggested filename |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Card, D. & Krueger, A. (1994), "Minimum Wages and Employment: A Case Study of the Fast-Food Industry in New Jersey and Pennsylvania," *American Economic Review* | Paper | 5 / 1994 | The paper that overturned the textbook consensus that minimum wage increases necessarily reduce employment — found no negative, possibly positive, employment effect from a New Jersey minimum-wage increase relative to Pennsylvania. The single most consequential empirical minimum-wage paper | `minimum_wages_employment_case_study (Card & Krueger, 1994).pdf` |
| Neumark, D. & Wascher, W. (2000), "Minimum Wages and Employment: A Case Study of the Fast-Food Industry in New Jersey and Pennsylvania: Comment," *American Economic Review* | Paper | 4 / 2000 | The direct rebuttal, using payroll records rather than Card-Krueger's phone survey data, finding the standard negative employment effect after all — necessary to include as the debate's other side, not just its most famous provocation | `minimum_wages_employment_comment (Neumark & Wascher, 2000).pdf` |
| Cengiz, D., Dube, A., Lindner, A. & Zipperer, B. (2019), "The Effect of Minimum Wages on Low-Wage Jobs," *Quarterly Journal of Economics* | Paper | 5 / 2019 | The modern synthesis — a bunching-estimator approach across 138 US minimum wage increases finds essentially no job losses even as wages rise, updating the Card-Krueger-vs-Neumark-Wascher debate with two more decades of data and a cleaner identification strategy | `effect_minimum_wages_low_wage_jobs (Cengiz et al., 2019).pdf` |

## 7. `#informality_dual_labor_markets_and_development` — the segment of the Brazilian labor market international theory was built to explain

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Why | Suggested filename |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Harris, J. & Todaro, M. (1970), "Migration, Unemployment and Development: A Two-Sector Analysis," *American Economic Review* | Paper | 5 / 1970 | The founding dual-labor-market model — rural-urban migration continues even with urban unemployment because migrants weigh the *expected* urban wage (formal wage × probability of getting a formal job) against the rural wage. The direct theoretical ancestor of every later formal/informal dual-market model | `migration_unemployment_development (Harris & Todaro, 1970).pdf` |
| Meghir, C., Narita, R. & Robin, J-M. (2015), "Wages and Informality in Developing Countries," *American Economic Review* 105(4): 1509-1546 | Paper | 5 / 2015 | A structural equilibrium wage-posting model with heterogeneous firms choosing formal/informal status, estimated directly on Brazilian labor force survey data — finds tighter enforcement raises wages, output, and welfare without raising unemployment, a materially different policy conclusion than the simple Harris-Todaro intuition would suggest | `wages_informality_developing_countries (Meghir, Narita & Robin, 2015).pdf` |
| Ulyssea, G. (2018), "Firms, Informality, and Development: Theory and Evidence from Brazil," *American Economic Review* 108(8): 2015-2047 | Paper | 5 / 2018 | The other landmark Brazil-specific structural informality paper — decomposes informal firms into three distinct types (entry-cost-constrained "De Soto" firms, ~9%; tax/regulation-evading "parasite" firms, ~42%; low-productivity "survival" firms, ~49%) and shows reducing informality is *not* automatically welfare-improving, since it depends which type of firm is affected. A genuinely load-bearing, policy-relevant nuance for any Brazil informality discussion | `firms_informality_development_brazil (Ulyssea, 2018).pdf` |

## Suggested acquisition order

1. **Ulyssea (2018)** and **Meghir, Narita & Robin (2015)** — the two-paper core of this bibliography's most distinctive contribution; nothing else here is as directly load-bearing for explaining the Brazilian labor market's central structural feature (informality) to a reader.
2. **Harris & Todaro (1970)** — cheap, short, and the necessary theoretical setup before either 2015/2018 Brazil paper makes full sense.
3. **Diamond (1982), Mortensen (1982), Pissarides (1985)** — the three founding search-and-matching papers, read together since they're conventionally treated as a single package (and jointly earned their 2010 Nobel).
4. **Borges (2022)** — cheap (a blog post, not a paywalled journal article) and the single most current, load-bearing Brazil-specific number in the whole file (NAWRU ≈ 8.5%).
5. Everything else, roughly in cluster order — the Pissarides (2000) and Blanchflower-Oswald (1994) book chapters, and the two Brazilian official methodology notes (PNAD Contínua, CAGED), are lower urgency since their core content is already summarized secondhand elsewhere or is reference material rather than argument-bearing.
