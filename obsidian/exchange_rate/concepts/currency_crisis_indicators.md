## The nine leading indicators

Compiled across empirical currency-crisis studies; no single factor is necessary or sufficient, but their co-occurrence raises crisis probability materially:

1. Capital markets recently liberalized (free capital flow newly allowed)
2. Large foreign capital inflows relative to GDP, especially short-term, foreign-currency-denominated funding
3. Currency crises often coincide with or are preceded by banking crises
4. Fixed/partially-fixed regimes are more vulnerable than floats (see [[currency_regimes]])
5. FX reserves decline sharply as the crisis approaches
6. The currency has risen substantially above its historical mean beforehand
7. Terms of trade deteriorate pre-crisis
8. Broad money growth and the M2/bank-reserves ratio rise pre-crisis
9. Inflation runs significantly higher than in tranquil periods

## Case study: Iceland, 2008

A textbook instance of nearly every indicator firing at once:
- Banking sector grew explosively post-liberalization (Kaupthing's assets grew 30x, 2000–2008); the three main banks' assets reached >14x GDP, foreign debt 5x GDP.
- Massive short-term foreign-currency funding with a maturity mismatch (long-term loans funded by short-term foreign deposits).
- Krona appreciated 40% vs. USD (2001–2007) on the back of the boom, then depreciated >40% against the euro in H1 2008 alone as global interbank lending seized up.
- A one-day attempt to peg the krona to the euro (October 2008) was abandoned immediately; the currency fell >60% once trading resumed, and interest rates were raised to 18% alongside capital controls.
- Central bank became technically insolvent (EUR 2bn in assets vs. EUR 50bn owed to foreign banks); the government could only guarantee domestic deposits, triggering a diplomatic dispute with the UK/Netherlands over foreign depositors.
- Ended in an IMF/Nordic bailout (USD 4.6bn).

Iceland demonstrates the **banking-crisis/currency-crisis feedback loop**: krona depreciation reduced the collateral value of krona-denominated bank assets right when banks most needed liquidity, accelerating both crises simultaneously — the same self-reinforcing dynamic (though via banks rather than the central bank's own reserves) as the balance-of-payments-crisis mechanics in [[Krugman 2023 - Fixed Exchange Rates and Foreign Exchange Intervention]].
