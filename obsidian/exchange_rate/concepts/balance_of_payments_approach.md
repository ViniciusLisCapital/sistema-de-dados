# Balance of Payments and Portfolio Balance Approaches

**Type:** Theoretical model — long-run current-account/debt-stock driven exchange rate determination
**Tags:** #balance-of-payments #portfolio-balance #current-account #external-debt #debt-sustainability #risk-premium

---

## Balance of payments approach

Countries running persistent current account deficits should see their currency weaken over time; persistent surplus countries should see appreciation. The mechanism (**debt sustainability channel**): a persistent deficit means rising net external debt as a % of GDP; once investors judge that debt path unsustainable, they price in the eventual large depreciation needed to narrow the deficit and stabilize external debt — long before the depreciation itself occurs.

**Key empirical caveat — long, unpredictable lags.** The US dollar's three post-1973 downcycles (1977–78, 1985–87, 2002–08) were each eventually driven by current account concerns, but:
- Early 1980s: US current account deteriorated sharply while the dollar *soared* — high US real rates attracted capital inflows that dominated the trade-deficit effect for years before the eventual 1985–87 reversal.
- Late 1990s: same pattern — deficit widened, dollar rose on FDI/portfolio inflows, before the 2002–2008 decline.
- Surplus side: Japan's yen and (for years) China's yuan faced persistent appreciation pressure from their current account surpluses, compounded by US protectionist pressure on the bilateral trade imbalance.

**Implication:** current account imbalances are a real, structural driver of long-run currency direction, but the "when" is dominated by capital-flow considerations (rate differentials, risk appetite) that can run opposite to the trade-flow signal for years.

## Portfolio balance approach

Extends Mundell-Fleming (a short-run model) to the long-run consequence of *sustained* fiscal deficits. Global investors hold a diversified portfolio of domestic/foreign bonds at a desired allocation; a growing deficit steadily increases the supply of domestic government bonds outstanding. For the market to willingly absorb this larger stock, investors must be compensated via:
1. Higher interest rates and/or a higher risk premium, or
2. Immediate currency depreciation large enough to create the expectation of subsequent appreciation (compensating future capital gains), or
3. Some combination.

**Combined with Mundell-Fleming**: an expansionary fiscal policy under high capital mobility can be currency-*positive* in the short run (rate-driven inflow, per [[mundell_fleming_policy_mix]]) but currency-*negative* in the long run, once the growing debt stock itself becomes the priced risk — and markets may separately start pricing the probability that the central bank will eventually be pressured to monetize the debt, which would sharply reverse any initial appreciation.

This is the same logic as Krugman's formal `ρ = ρ(B − A)` risk premium (see [[risk_premium]]) — a larger stock of domestic government debt the private market must hold (B, net of central bank holdings A) requires a higher expected return to be willingly held, whether that return comes via rates or via currency depreciation.
