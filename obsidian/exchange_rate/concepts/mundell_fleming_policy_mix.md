# Mundell-Fleming Policy Mix

**Type:** Theoretical model — monetary/fiscal transmission to the exchange rate
**Tags:** #mundell-fleming #capital-mobility #monetary-policy #fiscal-policy #policy-mix

---

## Definition

The Mundell-Fleming model explains how monetary and fiscal policy move the exchange rate through interest rates → capital flows → the exchange rate (in high-capital-mobility economies) or through aggregate demand → the trade balance → the exchange rate (in low-capital-mobility economies). The same policy can push the currency in opposite directions depending on which channel dominates — which depends entirely on how mobile capital is.

---

## High capital mobility (developed markets)

| Policy | Interest rate effect | Currency effect |
|---|---|---|
| Expansionary monetary | ↓ rates | Depreciation (capital flows to higher-yielding markets) |
| Restrictive monetary | ↑ rates | Appreciation |
| Expansionary fiscal | ↑ rates (deficit financing) | Appreciation (capital inflow dominates) |
| Restrictive fiscal | ↓ rates | Depreciation |

**Policy-mix table (high mobility):**

| | Expansionary monetary | Restrictive monetary |
|---|---|---|
| **Expansionary fiscal** | Indeterminate | Currency appreciates (classic case: Germany 1990–92, reunification fiscal expansion + Bundesbank tightening → DM appreciation) |
| **Restrictive fiscal** | Currency depreciates | Indeterminate |

## Low capital mobility (many EM economies)

Policy transmits mainly through the trade balance, not capital flows — expansionary policy (monetary or fiscal) boosts imports and worsens the trade balance, which is unambiguously bearish for the currency:

| | Expansionary monetary | Restrictive monetary |
|---|---|---|
| **Expansionary fiscal** | Currency depreciates | Indeterminate |
| **Restrictive fiscal** | Indeterminate | Currency appreciates |

---

## Why this matters

The same headline policy announcement (e.g., "fiscal stimulus") can be currency-bullish or -bearish depending on the country's capital-account openness — there is no universal sign. This is the reduced-form, capital-mobility-conditioned version of the same logic Krugman's DD-AA model derives from first principles (see [[Krugman 2023 - Output and the Exchange Rate in the Short Run]]): fiscal expansion appreciates the currency there too, but *because* higher money demand pushes up R under UIP, not because of a stylized "capital mobility" parameter.
