# Capital Mobility and Stabilization Policy under Fixed and Flexible Exchange Rates

**Author:** R. A. Mundell  
**Source:** The Canadian Journal of Economics and Political Science, Vol. 29, No. 4 (Nov. 1963), pp. 475–485  
**Type:** Foundational theoretical article  
**Tags:** #mundell-fleming #exchange-rate #fiscal-policy #monetary-policy #capital-mobility #fixed-vs-floating #foundational

---

## Summary

Mundell proves that with **perfect capital mobility**, the effectiveness of fiscal and monetary policy is completely determined by the exchange rate regime — not by the policy itself. The result is the sharpest possible version of the Mundell-Fleming theorem: monetary policy is **impotent** under fixed rates; fiscal policy is **impotent** under floating rates. Each policy finds its power in the opposite regime. The mechanism in both cases is capital flows: with perfect mobility, any domestic interest rate deviation is immediately arbitraged away, and this arbitrage forces the exchange rate (or reserves) to absorb the adjustment.

---

## Model Assumptions

- **Perfect capital mobility**: the country cannot maintain an interest rate different from the world rate `r*`. All arbitrage is instantaneous.
- Small open economy: no feedback on foreign income or `r*`.
- Fixed prices, elastic supply: unemployment context; price level constant.
- Monetary policy = open market purchase of domestic securities.
- Fiscal policy = increase in government spending financed by bond issuance (money supply held constant).
- Floating = central bank does not intervene in FX market.
- Fixed = central bank buys/sells reserves to maintain the peg.

---

## Main Results

### 1. Monetary expansion under floating exchange rates

| Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| ↑M_s (open market purchase) | Puts downward pressure on `r` |
| Capital outflow | `r` cannot fall below `r*` — arbitrage pushes capital out |
| Exchange rate depreciates | Excess demand for foreign currency |
| ↑NX | Trade balance improves |
| ↑Y (multiplier) | Export surplus stimulates income |

**Conclusion:** Monetary policy is **powerful** under floating rates — not through the interest rate channel (which is closed by capital mobility), but through the exchange rate / trade balance channel. Income rises in proportion to the money supply increase (quantity theory result).

---

### 2. Fiscal expansion under floating exchange rates

| Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| ↑G → ↑Y (on impact) | Multiplier effect |
| ↑demand for money → upward pressure on `r` | LM tightening |
| Capital inflow | `r` cannot rise above `r*` |
| Exchange rate appreciates | Excess supply of foreign currency |
| ↓NX | Trade balance deteriorates |
| Crowding-out of Y | Appreciation exactly offsets the fiscal multiplier |

**Conclusion:** Fiscal policy is **completely impotent** under floating rates with perfect capital mobility. The entire fiscal stimulus leaks into an import surplus — the budget deficit equals the trade deficit. Income and employment are unchanged.

---

### 3. Monetary expansion under fixed exchange rates

| Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| ↑M_s (open market purchase) | Puts downward pressure on `r` |
| Capital outflow | `r` cannot fall below `r*` |
| Balance of payments deficit | Excess demand for foreign currency |
| Central bank sells reserves to defend peg | Absorbs domestic currency, reducing M_s |
| M_s returns to original level | The expansion fully reverses |

**Conclusion:** Monetary policy is **completely impotent** under fixed rates. The open market purchase is undone by the reserve outflow. The only lasting effect is a fall in foreign exchange reserves — the central bank has swapped domestic assets for foreign assets at no gain.

---

### 4. Fiscal expansion under fixed exchange rates

| Effect | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| ↑G → ↑Y (multiplier) | Spending effect |
| ↑demand for money | Upward pressure on `r` |
| Capital inflow | `r` cannot rise — foreign capital enters |
| Central bank buys reserves | Defends peg, expands M_s endogenously |
| M_s expands ("through the back door") | Monetary expansion validates fiscal expansion |
| ↑Y confirmed | Both goods and capital markets clear at higher Y |

**Conclusion:** Fiscal policy is **powerful** under fixed rates. Capital inflows force the central bank to expand the money supply, which amplifies rather than offsets the fiscal effect. Simple Keynesian multiplier results hold.

## Summary Table (Mundell)

| Instrument | Fixed exchange rates | Floating exchange rates |
|---|---|---|
| Monetary policy (↑M) | **Impotent** — reserves fall, M reverts | **Powerful** — exchange rate channel |
| Fiscal policy (↑G) | **Powerful** — endogenous M validates | **Impotent** — appreciation crowds out |

---

## Special Case: Sterilization

Mundell shows that sterilization (offsetting reserve changes with open market operations to hold M_s constant) is **internally inconsistent** under fixed rates with perfect capital mobility. The central bank cannot simultaneously fix the exchange rate, hold the money supply constant, and have a goods market equilibrium. One constraint must give. In practice, sterilization under a peg merely accelerates reserve depletion — "running water into a sink that is already full."

---

## Key Results

1. **Regime determines policy effectiveness** — the same policy instrument has opposite power depending on whether rates are fixed or floating.
2. **Capital mobility is the transmission mechanism** — without capital mobility, both policies work under both regimes (standard closed-economy result).
3. **Fiscal policy under floating = trade balance manipulation** — output unchanged, trade deficit changes.
4. **Monetary policy under fixed = reserve manipulation** — output unchanged, reserves change.
5. **Sterilization is unsustainable** — incompatible with a credible peg and perfect capital mobility simultaneously.
