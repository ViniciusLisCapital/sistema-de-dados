## Context and motivation

Brazil entered 2025 with inflation above target, unanchored expectations, and a sharp rise in the country risk premium following disappointments with fiscal adjustment announcements. The report assesses whether Brazil is experiencing fiscal dominance and how close it is to that regime.

## Core argument / thesis

Fiscal dominance is a spectrum, not a binary state. Brazil in January 2025 is **not fully in fiscal dominance** — the BCB remains active and consistent with conventional monetary rules. However, Brazil is moving in that direction due to rapidly deteriorating fiscal credibility.

Two conditions lead to fiscal dominance: (1) a passive central bank and (2) a government without credible limits on the public debt trajectory. Condition 2 alone can prevent inflation stabilization even with an active CB (Woodford 1996).

Itaú's conclusion: BCB acting correctly is necessary but not sufficient. Credible fiscal measures are essential to prevent further deterioration.

## Key mechanisms / model

**Sargent-Wallace (1981) "Unpleasant Monetarist Arithmetic":**
When debt trajectory is out of control and the CB is the "follower," it is eventually forced to monetize, fueling inflation — even if initially restrictive.

**Leeper (1991) — 2×2 regime matrix:**

| | Monetary Active (CB follows Taylor rule) | Monetary Passive (CB lenient) |
|---|---|---|
| Fiscal Irresponsible | Hyperinflation + debt explosion | **Fiscal Dominance** — ex: Argentina |
| Fiscal Responsible | **Inflation targeting works** — ex: Brazil most of the time | No anchor |

**Blanchard (2004) — Continuous spectrum:**
Rate hike → ↑ probability of debt default → ↑ risk premium (ρ) → capital outflows → BRL depreciation → ↑ inflation. Under sufficiently bad fiscal conditions, tighter monetary policy can be *inflationary*. This generates a spectrum of fiscal dominance rather than a binary threshold.

**FTPL (Fiscal Theory of the Price Level):**
```
D_t / P_t = E_t Σ [(T_{t+i} - G_{t+i}) / (1+r)^i]
```
Deficits not offset by future surpluses equilibrate via inflation eroding the real value of debt.

**Wealth effect channel (Woodford 1996):**
Fiscal expansion raises private net wealth → increases domestic demand → inflationary pressure. Even an active CB may be insufficient to stabilize inflation without credible debt limits. Does not require CB monetization.

## Main results / findings

**Monetary regime — BCB active:**
- Selic at or above Taylor Rule levels (contrast: 2010–2014, when Selic was ~280bps below TR)
- Responding to unanchored inflation expectations (contrast: 2011–12 rate cuts despite rising expectations)
- Operational autonomy preserved de jure and de facto

**Fiscal regime — deteriorating:**
- Debt/GDP: 72% (2022) → expected 85% by 2026, no path to convergence
- Fiscal rule insufficient to stabilize debt without significant revenue increase; gap ≈ 3.2pp of GDP vs. stabilization primary result
- Share of floating-rate securities rising (rate hikes mechanically worsen debt trajectory)
- Primary surplus expectations worsening again — similar pattern to 2011–2015

**Market dynamics:**
- Risk premium: broad composite measure sharply higher, accompanied by BRL depreciation
- BRL decomposition: exchange rate responded *positively* to Selic hikes (consistent with Dornbusch), but offset by global USD strength and domestic risk premium — net effect: depreciation
- Yield curve: not significantly steeper (unlike 2015)
- Breakeven inflation: elevated but below 2015–16 peak
- Capital outflows: moderate through November 2024; significant in December but not yet confirmed as structural

## Limitations and caveats

- Point-in-time analysis (January 2025); fiscal situation may have evolved since
- Blanchard's mechanism (rate hikes → depreciation) was not yet empirically confirmed at writing — Selic hikes were *not* causing BRL depreciation at that point; conventional carry was still partially working
- Capital outflows ambiguous as of December 2024
