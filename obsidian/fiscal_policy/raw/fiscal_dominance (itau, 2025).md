# Macro Vision: Fiscal Dominance in Brazil

**January 21, 2025**

## Executive Summary

Fiscal dominance refers to a situation where deterioration of public finances limits or undermines the central bank's ability to control inflation—and may even have the opposite effect to what is intended. This situation exists on a continuous spectrum rather than as a binary outcome, with intermediate regimes of interaction between monetary and fiscal authorities.

Two key conditions bring a country closer to fiscal dominance:

1. A central bank hesitant in fighting inflation
2. A government that does not set credible limits on public debt trajectory

The latter condition may be sufficient to prevent inflation stabilization even without the first.

While domestic inflation runs above target and inflation expectations become increasingly unanchored, the Brazilian Central Bank (BCB) remains consistent with conventional monetary rules and has not shown hesitation in pursuing its inflation target. However, the credibility of the fiscal framework—even if strictly adhered to—has deteriorated significantly regarding its capacity to generate sustainable medium-term fiscal trajectories.

The BCB's Monetary Policy Committee (Copom) has noted that market variables are under stress, with significant increases in risk premium. While interest rate hikes have not contributed to FX depreciation or yield curve steepening, breakeven inflation measures have risen, and capital outflows warrant close monitoring.

**Conclusion:** If trends in fiscal variables remain unchanged, the Brazilian economy will move closer to fiscal dominance. While the BCB continues to act consistent with inflation targeting—a necessary condition—this alone is insufficient. Measures improving the public debt outlook are essential to reduce the danger of inflation getting out of control. The economy has not deteriorated completely, but the fact that economic agents and independent analysts are discussing fiscal dominance suggests Brazil is not in a fully normal situation.

## 1. Fiscal Dominance: Understanding the Concept

Fiscal dominance describes a situation where deterioration of public finances limits or undermines the central bank's actions to control inflation—and may even have the opposite effect because fiscal policy exerts significant pressure on the economy. This pressure operates through several channels, leading to FX depreciation, unanchored inflation expectations, and rising risk premiums, among other consequences.

### 1.1. Fiscal Dominance as a Coordination Problem

The concept stems from the influential article "Some Unpleasant Monetarist Arithmetic" (1981) by Thomas Sargent and Neil Wallace. The article demonstrates that when a country's debt trajectory is out of control, its central bank may be forced to monetize it, fueling inflation—even if restrictive monetary policy is initially implemented to contain inflation.

A key assumption for this outcome is that the upward debt trajectory does not depend on current or future monetary policy. Implicitly, there is a coordination problem between monetary and fiscal authorities, in which the former is unable to "discipline" the latter. The "unpleasant monetarist arithmetic" arises from a coordination scheme in which fiscal policy "dominates" monetary policy and the monetary authority faces constraints imposed by the demand for government bonds.

When the monetary authority is the "dominant player" and the fiscal authority is a "follower," the latter must adjust to rebalance the public budget. Restrictions on monetary policy actions—such as the gold standard, fixed exchange rates, inflation targeting regimes, and central bank operational autonomy—can be seen as attempts to prevent the fiscal authority from exercising leadership over monetary policy. Therefore, periods when monetary frameworks are weakened are particularly susceptible to fiscal dominance.

When society values monetary stability, it produces institutions, habits, and rules that allow the central bank to act autonomously, thus limiting the risk of fiscal dominance.

#### Coordination Regimes Framework

Based on the work of Leeper (1991), updated for an inflation targeting regime, the following table shows different coordination schemes between fiscal and monetary authorities:

|  | **Monetary Policy: Active** (CB not lenient, follows Taylor rule) | **Monetary Policy: Passive** (CB lenient, irresponsible) |
|---|---|---|
| **Fiscal Policy: Responsible** (sustainable debt) | **Inflation Target Regime works** Example: Brazil most of the time | **No anchor** |
| **Fiscal Policy: Irresponsible** (no effort to generate surplus) | Hyperinflation and debt explodes | **Fiscal Dominance** (inflation lowers real rate and prevents debt explosion; capital controls) Example: Argentina |

When monetary policy is active and fiscal policy is irresponsible, there is a total lack of coordination: interest rates rise to restrain inflation but there are no concurrent adjustments to the public budget. This scenario produces increasingly high interest rates and rising inflation. If neither authority deviates from this "game of chicken," the result is simultaneous explosion of inflation and debt (although the government remains solvent). In practice, it is unreasonable to imagine this situation persists indefinitely, and deviations must be analyzed.

Suppose the government refuses to adjust the budget appropriately to contain debt growth and the monetary authority agrees to monetize, to some degree, the budgetary needs—deviating from its primary objective of inflation control. The central bank becomes lenient. This is precisely the situation of fiscal dominance: monetary policy is passive and fiscal policy is irresponsible, as in Sargent and Wallace's "unpleasant arithmetic" (upper right quadrant).

When monetary policy remains active and fiscal policy is responsible, the central bank is committed to meeting its inflation target, while fiscal policy eventually changes, deviating from an explosive debt trajectory through budget adjustments via spending cuts and/or tax increases. In this situation, the inflation targeting regime functions at its full potential, with inflation at desired levels and debt on a controlled trajectory (lower left quadrant).

**Note on fiscal responsibility:** Qualifying fiscal policy as "responsible" does not eliminate the possibility of counter-cyclical actions. It only requires that, whatever the short-term fiscal trajectory, the policy will ensure debt sustainability over time.

### 1.2. Fiscal Dominance and the Fiscal Theory of the Price Level

The Fiscal Theory of the Price Level (FTPL) received significant attention following recent episodes of high inflation partly resulting from strong fiscal stimulus linked to the COVID-19 pandemic.

FTPL is based on the government's intertemporal budget constraint:

$$\frac{D_t}{P_t} = E_t \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} \left[\frac{T_{t+i} - G_{t+i}}{(1+r)^i}\right]$$

where:
- $D_t$ = nominal value of public debt at the beginning of period t
- $P_t$ = price level in t
- $T_{t+i}$ and $G_{t+i}$ = taxes and primary government expenditures (real terms) in period t+i
- $r$ = real discount rate (constant for simplicity)

The constraint shows that public debt must be financed by the present value of expected primary results, although the timing of when surpluses will be generated is quite flexible. One consequence is that deficits not offset by surpluses can only occur if accompanied by price increases that reduce the real value of debt:

$$\frac{D_t}{P_t} > E_t \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} \left[\frac{T_{t+i} - G_{t+i}}{(1+r)^i}\right] \Rightarrow \frac{D_t}{P_t \uparrow} = E_t \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} \left[\frac{T_{t+i} - G_{t+i}}{(1+r)^i}\right]$$

This results in an equilibrium in which, absent credible fiscal adjustments, prices adjust to ensure that the government's budget constraint is respected. To obtain this result, one typically assumes accommodation of fiscal policy by monetary policy—that is, some degree of passive monetary policy and irresponsible fiscal policy—as in Sargent and Wallace.

### 1.3. Fiscal Dominance as a Continuous Spectrum Phenomenon

The hypothesis of passive/active or responsible/irresponsible authorities focuses on extreme situations. In practice, intermediate regimes occur.

Fiscal policy generally seeks future surpluses but may not assume the full burden of fiscal adjustment. Monetary policy, while geared toward stabilizing inflation, may have to deal with the inflationary consequences of public debt that is partially not financed by surpluses.

In a context of flexible exchange rates, closer to the current Brazilian reality, Olivier Blanchard's article "Fiscal dominance and inflation targeting: lessons from Brazil" (2004) studies the case in which an increase in the interest rate increases the probability of non-payment of public debt (particularly with high debt levels), affecting the risk premium. This occurs continuously, generating a spectrum of fiscal dominance rather than a binary framework.

As the risk premium rises, resource outflows occur, leading to exchange rate depreciation. Depreciation, in turn, leads to higher inflation. Under these conditions, therefore, a higher interest rate can, in theory, be inflationary—but only when the fiscal situation is bad enough.

The concept of fiscal dominance as a continuum is quite realistic and captures the nuanced relationship between monetary and fiscal policy in flexible exchange rate regimes.

### 1.4. Fiscal Dominance and the Wealth Effect of Debt

Even if the Central Bank remains committed to fighting inflation without deviating from this goal, fiscal policy may be inflationary to the point of preventing stabilization of the price trajectory. This occurs when the increase in debt is perceived as an increase in wealth (thus not applying the so-called "Ricardian Equivalence")—a realistic hypothesis explored by, among others, Woodford (1996).

High inflation would result from "too much nominal wealth in search of few goods" and would have the role of eroding wealth to rebalance supply and demand. The mechanism aptly captures situations in which fiscal expansions are accompanied by expansion of domestic private demand and rising inflation, as has occurred in Brazil recently.

**Charts show:** Fiscal expansion and private demand have moved together, with government spending rising from roughly 110 in 2002 to 170 by 2024, while domestic private demand (C+I) rose from approximately 100 to 220. IPCA inflation has remained elevated, tracking above the target in recent years.

Woodford shows that even with a combative central bank, fiscal expansion can lead to higher inflation. The result goes back to Sargent and Wallace's "nasty monetarist arithmetic," but with a crucial difference: it does not require the central bank to be "led" by the government's budgetary need to monetize debt.

In other words, even if the Central Bank does not deviate from its commitment to stabilizing inflation, the fiscal situation may make a typically adequate monetary regime (e.g., an inflation targeting system with an autonomous central bank) insufficient to achieve stabilization. In such situations, raising interest rates may even lead to higher inflation.

Woodford shows, however, that by setting credible limits on the trajectory of public debt, it is possible to prevent fiscal shocks from having undesirable effects on inflation and ensure that appropriate monetary policy rules can achieve inflation stability.

**In summary:** When fiscal expansion increases domestic private demand and thus puts pressure on inflation, control of public accounts is necessary to ensure the possibility of inflationary stability—even in an inflation targeting regime with an autonomous central bank. This control can be achieved through credible rules limiting the trajectory of public debt.

## 2. Assessing the Risk of Fiscal Dominance in Brazil

Three main sets of information are important for assessing the risk of fiscal dominance:

1. **Coordination problem indicators:** Signs of passivity in monetary policy in relation to fiscal policy—that is, signs that the Central Bank may be acting to monetize debt, to the detriment of its inflation stabilization objective.

2. **Fiscal unsustainability risk:** Trajectories of fiscal variables such as public debt/GDP, public expenditure/GDP, primary deficit/GDP, and debt composition/duration.

3. **Market dynamics:** Variables reflecting market stress, such as correlation between interest rates and exchange rates, risk premium, yield curve slope, and breakeven inflation.

### 2.1. Evaluating the Monetary Regime

Although Brazil's inflation rate is above target and inflation expectations are increasingly unanchored, there are no signs of the Central Bank hesitating to act to control inflation. Copom and its members have highlighted the risks that fiscal imbalances pose to the economy, particularly to inflation expectations, and the importance of coordinating fiscal and monetary policies.

#### Taylor Rule Assessment

One way to assess whether the Central Bank has been acting consistently with the data to achieve the inflation target is to examine a simple version of the Taylor Rule:

$$\text{Nominal interest rate} = (\text{neutral real rate} + \text{inflation target}) + \alpha(\text{inflation expectation} - \text{inflation target}) - \beta(\text{output gap})$$

The data shows that the Selic rate has recently been maintained at levels consistent with, if not higher than, those indicated by the rule. As comparison, in the period from 2010 to 2014—when there was also intense discussion about fiscal dominance in Brazil—the interest rate was systematically below the rule-indicated level.

#### Response to Inflation Expectations

The policy rate has moved in response to unanchoring of inflation expectations relative to the target. The current behavior of the monetary authority differs from that observed in 2011-2012, when the interest rate was cut despite rising inflation expectations at the time.

#### Conclusion on Monetary Regime

From any perspective, the monetary authority remains active and committed to achieving the inflation target. Furthermore, the operational autonomy of the Central Bank has been preserved both de jure and de facto.

### 2.2. Evaluating the Fiscal Regime

While there are no signs of passivity on the part of the monetary authority in the face of high inflation, recent developments in fiscal variables point to greater risk of fiscal dominance. Brazil's public debt has grown rapidly, starting from levels already high compared with peer countries, with no prospect of stabilization ahead. The median market expectation is that debt/GDP will reach 85% in 2026, up from 72% in 2022, with no prospect of convergence.

#### Debt Composition

The composition of public debt may be a barometer of fiscal dominance risks. The significant increase in the share of floating-rate securities could foster perception that interest rate increases needed to control inflation will make the public debt trajectory mechanically even more challenging. This accentuates the wealth effect mentioned above and reduces the effectiveness of monetary policy.

However, the recent period has not seen shortening of the average maturity of public debt issuances—a phenomenon that marked previous crises, such as those of 2002 and 2014. Despite worse debt composition, demand for longer-term public bonds has not collapsed, suggesting that rejection of public debt has not yet occurred at the intensity that would result from high fiscal risk perception.

**Charts show:** Deterioration of the composition of domestic public debt, with floating-rate securities rising from approximately 24% in 2010 to above 40% by 2025. However, average maturity of DPMF issuances (6-month moving average) has remained relatively stable between 4.5 and 7 years, with no evidence of significant shortening.

#### Primary Result and Fiscal Credibility

An important aspect of a fiscal regime's character is its credibility regarding the generation of primary surpluses that guarantee debt sustainability. The primary result and expected trajectory at each point in time according to the Focus survey reveal important patterns.

Between 2011 and 2015—the period of the last intense debate on fiscal dominance—there was significant deterioration in expectations concurrent with worsening of the current result. Subsequently, as a result of structural reforms such as approvals of the spending cap and the pension reform in 2017 and 2019 respectively, Brazil saw better-than-expected primary results and subsequent positive revisions of expectations. More recently, deterioration of expectations for the primary result has been observed again, signaling the need for measures to reverse a challenging scenario.

#### Required vs. Actual Primary Surplus

Conceptually, the primary result sufficient to stabilize public debt close to current levels of 80% of GDP is around 2.5% of GDP, depending on the real interest rate and potential GDP assumed. Until 2026, the distance between this required primary result and the result implemented by simply complying with the fiscal rule in a conservative growth and real interest rate scenario is 3.2 percentage points of GDP. This confirms that the fiscal rule is not capable of generating expectations of debt stabilization unless there is a significant increase in revenues.

This trajectory increases the risk of fiscal policy being perceived as irresponsible, which reinforces the need for structural measures capable of generating an improvement in the primary result trajectory going forward. Through these measures, it would be possible to strengthen coordination between economic policies aimed at the full functioning of the inflation targeting regime and debt sustainability.

**Proposed measure:** As suggested in a recently published report, an alternative would be reduction of the upper limit of annual real growth in primary expenditures from 2.5% to 1.5%, accompanied by underlying measures that give consistency and credibility to the announcement.

### 2.3. Assessing Market Dynamics

Disappointments with recent announcements of fiscal adjustments led to a sharp rise in the Brazilian risk premium, accompanied by sharp exchange rate devaluation.

#### Exchange Rate Decomposition

The decomposition of the Brazilian real's movement shows that the exchange rate reacted positively to the rise in interest rates (and possibly to recent foreign exchange market auctions). However, this boost was more than offset by negative effects of the strong-dollar international scenario and the domestic risk premium, which affected all Brazilian assets.

#### Capital Flows

According to data through November, there were no signs of capital outflows in external accounts. In fact, recent flows, although negative, are much less intensely so than in comparable recent periods—especially the 2014-2015 period. In December, however, financial flow data reported by the Central Bank did indicate significant outflows, although it is not possible to conclusively state they result from structural outflow of Brazilian residents' capital.

#### Interest Rate Market

In the interest rate market, there are no signs of significant stress in the slope of the curve (as existed in 2015). On the other hand, breakeven inflation, although at much more moderate levels than in 2015-16, has risen sharply recently.

#### Market Stress Summary

In short:

- **No signs of:** Central Bank hesitation in combating inflation, steepening of the interest rate curve, or interest rate increases contributing to exchange rate depreciation.

- **However:** Deterioration in credibility of the fiscal trajectory has been significant, contributing to increase in risk premium and leading to a period of stress. Although less pronounced than in 2015-16, this period has been accompanied by increases in breakeven inflation and may lead to clearer capital outflows.

The fact that fiscal dominance remains an important topic of public debate suggests that Brazil is not in full normality. The analysis reinforces the need for measures that will lead to more balanced prospects for public debt going forward.
