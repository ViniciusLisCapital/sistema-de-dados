# Exchange Rate Theory — A Brief Intellectual History

**Purpose:** a narrative introduction to the exchange-rate literature base, tracing how the field's central questions — what determines a currency's value, why pegs break, who wins and loses from a given regime, capital controls — developed from the 1960s to today. Every claim below is grounded in a specific source already mapped in `bibliography.md`; nothing here is invented history. Written as the fourth piece in this `consolidated/exchange_rate/` folder, alongside the bibliography, the data inventory, and the two interactive explorers — meant to be the entry point a reader without prior exposure to the topic would read first.

**How to use this:** each section corresponds to one or more `bibliography.md` clusters, cited by author/year exactly as the bibliography does. A summary table of the field's central thinkers closes the document.

## 1. Before the modern literature: fixed rates as the default

For most of the era this literature actually documents, a floating, market-determined exchange rate was the exception, not the rule. The classical gold standard, then the Bretton Woods system (1944–1973) — a global network of currencies pegged to the US dollar, itself convertible into gold — treated a stable exchange rate as an institutional anchor, not a market outcome (`historical_monetary_systems`, in Barker, McNelis & Nickelsburg's CFA curriculum reading, the base's standard-reference synthesis of this history). Two structural features of that era matter for everything that follows: first, a reserve-currency issuer sits in a structurally different position from every other pegging country — with N countries pegging to the dollar, there are only N−1 exchange rates to defend, and the dollar itself was never one of them (`reserve_currency_asymmetry`, Krugman Ch. 18) — the origin of what later became known as America's "exorbitant privilege." Second, the entire theoretical apparatus of "defending a peg" — reserves, intervention, sterilization — was built to describe exactly this world, and stays the reference point even after 1973, when the major currencies floated and reserves-defense mechanics became the exception for advanced economies but the norm for most emerging markets, Brazil included.

The purchasing power parity (PPP) idea — that exchange rates should track relative price levels over the long run — predates this literature's academic core by decades, but it enters the base as a component of the modern determination framework rather than as a standalone historical claim: covered here via its textbook (Krugman Ch. 16) and CFA-curriculum treatments, alongside the sobering empirical fact that PPP is "persistently violated in practice" over any horizon short enough to matter for a trading or allocation decision (`purchasing_power_parity`).

## 2. Two ways to price a currency: goods versus assets

The single most consequential reframing in the base is what Krugman's textbook calls the **asset approach to exchange rates**: treat the exchange rate as the relative price of two assets — currency deposits — rather than the relative price of two baskets of goods (`asset_approach_to_exchange_rates`, Krugman Ch. 14). Under this lens, today's exchange rate depends on the *expected future path* of returns, exactly the way a stock price depends on expected future dividends, not on today's trade flows. This single move explains why the field's workhorse equilibrium condition is **uncovered interest rate parity (UIP)** — expected returns on deposits of any two currencies must be equal once expressed in the same currency — rather than any goods-market-clearing condition. It also explains why capital flows, not trade flows, dominate exchange rate determination in the short-to-medium run, with trade balances only asserting themselves over much longer horizons (`current_account_adjustment_channels`, CFA Level II).

Two no-arbitrage relationships anchor everything built on top: **covered interest rate parity (CIP)** — a pure arbitrage identity, F/S = (1+r_foreign)/(1+r_domestic), that holds mechanically wherever capital and forwards are both freely tradeable — and UIP, its *behavioral* cousin, which requires market participants to actually hold the exchange rate at the level that equalizes expected returns (CFA Level I's `covered_interest_rate_parity`; CFA Level II's `international_parity_conditions_synthesis` ties CIP, UIP, ex ante PPP, real interest rate parity, and the international Fisher effect into one master chain). The chain matters because, as later sections show, it is precisely the *behavioral* link — UIP — that breaks down empirically, while the arbitrage link — CIP — holds by construction (with one notable post-2008 exception, `cip_basis_breakdown`, Krugman Ch. 14).

## 3. The postwar regime question: Mundell's dual legacy

The field's founding figure for exchange-rate-regime economics is **Robert Mundell**, whose two papers — three years and one subtle shift in focus apart — still frame every regime-choice discussion in the base. His 1961 paper, *A Theory of Optimum Currency Areas*, asks a logically prior question to "should a country peg or float": what geographic unit should even share a currency in the first place? His answer — a region defined by high *internal* factor mobility and low *external* factor mobility, not any political border — deliberately decouples currency-area membership from national sovereignty, and directly (if speculatively, thirty years ahead of Maastricht) anticipates the euro debate via the Meade-Scitovsky exchange the paper itself cites (`optimum_currency_area_definition`, `oca_application_european_monetary_union_debate`; Mundell, 1961). Two later papers close out what became the field's canonical OCA trio: **McKinnon (1963)** added economic openness as a second criterion, and **Kenen (1969)** added production diversification as a third — both currently gaps in the acquired base, flagged for acquisition rather than skipped.

Mundell's 1963 paper, *Capital Mobility and Stabilization Policy Under Fixed and Flexible Exchange Rates*, asks the complementary question once a currency's boundary is fixed: what does a country give up by fixing its exchange rate? The answer is the paper's namesake result — the **impossible trinity** (or trilemma): a country cannot simultaneously have a fixed exchange rate, free capital mobility, and independent monetary policy. Mundell shows the mechanism formally: under perfect capital mobility, monetary policy is powerful under floating rates and completely impotent under fixed rates, while fiscal policy is the mirror image (`monetary_fiscal_policy_effectiveness_by_regime`, `mundell_fleming_model`). This is not a side observation — the trilemma is the single load-bearing idea underneath nearly every other regime-choice, intervention, and sterilization concept the base contains, from the textbook mechanics of defending a peg (Krugman Ch. 18) to the BCB's own operational toolkit (§4 below).

## 4. Why exchange rates move so much: Dornbusch's overshooting

By the mid-1970s, with major currencies newly floating post-Bretton Woods, a new puzzle emerged: exchange rates were visibly far more volatile than the price levels or money supplies they were supposedly tracking. **Rudiger Dornbusch's** 1976 paper, *Expectations and Exchange Rate Dynamics*, remains the field's canonical answer. His mechanism: asset markets (money, FX) clear *instantly* via UIP-style arbitrage, while goods-market prices adjust only *slowly*. A permanent monetary expansion must eventually depreciate the currency in exact proportion to the money-supply increase — but because prices cannot jump immediately, the immediate effect is a *larger* depreciation than the long-run equilibrium requires, followed by a gradual appreciation back as prices catch up (`overshooting_model`). This single sticky-price mechanism — later given the plain textbook derivation via the money-market/interest-parity diagram (Krugman Ch. 15) and the intuition-level "currency reform" analogy for long-run monetary neutrality — is still the field's standard explanation for excess FX volatility, and it resolves what would otherwise look like a contradiction: the same interest-rate move can signal either currency strength (a credible tightening) or currency weakness (validation of a higher permanent inflation path), depending entirely on *why* the market believes rates moved (`fisher_effect_uip_ppp_paradox_resolution`, Krugman Ch. 16).

## 5. The empirical embarrassment that still hasn't gone away: Fama (1984)

If Dornbusch explained why exchange rates are volatile, **Eugene Fama's** 1984 paper, *Forward and Spot Exchange Rates*, delivered one of the most durable empirical anomalies in all of economics: UIP's central prediction — that a currency at a forward premium should subsequently depreciate — is not just weakly supported, it is **reliably violated in the opposite direction**. Using nine major currencies against the USD (1973–1982), Fama found that currencies at a forward premium tend to *appreciate further*, the "wrong-signed beta" that gave the "forward premium puzzle" its name (`wrong_signed_beta_uip_regression`). His more consequential finding, by his own account, is that most of the variation in the forward-spot differential is variation in a time-varying risk premium, not genuine information about the future spot rate (`premium_variance_dominance`) — meaning forward rates mostly move because required risk compensation is fluctuating, not because the market is updating its view of where the currency is headed. Fama's own catalog of candidate explanations — market irrationality, government "leaning against the wind," the peso problem (small-probability, large-magnitude devaluation risk skewing realized samples), and a structural risk-premium story requiring no irrationality at all — anticipated essentially the next forty years of research on the puzzle, without settling on a single winner (`fama_1984_candidate_explanations`). This is also the formal origin of the **carry trade**'s persistent profitability: if UIP held, borrowing in a low-yield currency to invest in a high-yield one would break even on average; because UIP fails in exactly the way Fama documented, it doesn't (`carry_trade`, Krugman Ch. 14; CFA Level II).

## 6. How pegs die: three generations of currency crisis theory

No cluster in the base is deeper than the one built around a single recurring question: how does a fixed or managed exchange rate actually collapse? The literature answers this in three distinct theoretical "generations," developed across three decades and, tellingly, in direct argument with each other.

**First generation (Krugman, 1979; Flood & Garber, 1983/84).** Paul Krugman's original model treats a currency crisis as the inevitable, mechanical consequence of a government financing a persistent fiscal deficit through money creation under a fixed exchange rate: reserves decline every period regardless of speculation, and rational speculators attack **before** the reserves would exhaust naturally — at the exact moment a post-float price jump would otherwise hand them a windfall — rather than waiting for the government to run out (`speculative_attack_timing_condition`). Robert Flood and Peter Garber's companion paper supplies the same logic in closed, linear form via the "shadow exchange rate" technique — computing the rate that would prevail if the peg were abandoned right now, then finding the moment that shadow path crosses the pegged rate — and adds a sharp rebuttal to the traditional case for fixed rates: pegging does not eliminate the risk of arbitrary speculative bubbles, it simply relocates *when* that risk shows up, from continuous volatility to a sudden regime break (`shadow_floating_exchange_rate`, `arbitrary_speculative_bubbles_under_fixed_rates`).

**Second generation (Obstfeld, 1984).** Maurice Obstfeld's contribution is not a rejection of the first generation but a proof that a second, qualitatively different crisis type can arise from essentially the same model, depending entirely on what the public expects the government to do *after* a crisis. If the public expects policy to continue unchanged regardless of whether a run occurs, crises stay fully fundamentals-driven exactly as in Krugman (1979). But if the public instead expects a crisis to trigger a discrete policy shift — say, to inflationary financing — the belief becomes self-validating: a continuum of self-consistent equilibria opens up, and a peg that would otherwise survive indefinitely can collapse purely because the market believes it will (`self_fulfilling_multiple_equilibria_crisis`, `policy_contingent_indeterminacy_mechanism`). This is the literature's formal origin of "self-fulfilling" crisis logic — genuinely analogous to a Diamond-Dybvig bank run, with the important caveat, which Obstfeld states explicitly, that a currency crisis (unlike a bank run) is only self-fulfilling if the specific policy-contingency channel is present (`extrinsic_uncertainty_bank_run_analogy`).

**Third generation (Chang & Velasco, 1998; Krugman, 1999/2000) — the Asian crisis.** The 1997–98 Asian financial crisis broke both prior frameworks: it spread from Thailand to countries with no meaningful direct trade or financial links, a contagion pattern neither pure fundamentals nor a single-country self-fulfilling story could explain cleanly. Two competing accounts emerged in direct dialogue with each other. Roberto Chang and Andrés Velasco formalized the "financial fragility" school as a fully worked-out open-economy Diamond-Dybvig bank-run model, with a precise, falsifiable illiquidity criterion for when a self-fulfilling run is even possible (`bank_illiquidity_necessary_and_sufficient_condition`) — and a sharp policy result showing that a currency board, a fixed-rate-with-lender-of-last-resort regime, and a floating-rate-with-LOLR regime all share the *same* underlying vulnerability; the exchange-rate regime only determines whether the crisis surfaces as a bank run or as a currency collapse (`currency_board_vs_lolr_crisis_relocation`). Paul Krugman's own third-generation model instead builds a pure private-sector balance-sheet mechanism — no government reserves or deficit anywhere in the story — in which a leverage constraint interacting with foreign-currency-denominated debt produces multiple equilibria through a real-exchange-rate feedback loop, formalized via the classical Keynes-Ohlin "transfer problem" applied to sudden-stop current-account reversals (`balance_sheet_currency_mismatch_investment_channel`, `transfer_problem_current_account_reversal`). What makes this paper historically notable beyond its own content is Krugman's explicit public reversal opening it: "I hereby capitulate... Maury Obstfeld was right" — a rare, on-the-record admission that the self-fulfilling school he had argued against just years earlier was correct (`krugman_capitulation_on_self_fulfilling_crises`). A discussant's comment appended to the same volume, Peter Garber's, is worth preserving as a check on the whole "generations" framing this literature (and this document) uses for convenience: Garber calls it a "name swindle," arguing the supposedly sequential generations were mostly developed in parallel and that a third generation was premature marketing rather than a genuine theoretical advance (`garber_generational_taxonomy_critique`) — a useful reminder to hold the tidy three-generation story loosely.

A Brazil-specific companion to this entire arc — Garcia & Valpassos's account of Brazil's own 1999 Real devaluation — remains a gap in the acquired base, flagged as the natural next addition given how thoroughly the general theory is now covered.

## 7. Who wins and loses from a regime: the political economy turn

Every model above treats the exchange-rate regime as a technocratic optimization problem — but **Jeffry Frieden's** 1991 paper, *Invested Interests*, reframes the whole question as a distributional conflict with real winners and losers. Building on the specific-factors trade model layered onto Mundell-Fleming, Frieden maps which domestic sectors want *policy autonomy* (a floating rate) versus *currency stability* (a fixed or managed rate): import-competing tradables producers are largely indifferent to volatility but want autonomy; export-oriented tradables want stability, since they carry direct FX exposure on foreign sales; nontradables producers (most domestic services) have no direct FX exposure and want autonomy to manage domestic demand; and international investors and traders — the group least tied to any one country's real economy — most strongly want stability (`sectoral_exchange_rate_preferences_framework`). A second, orthogonal axis governs the exchange rate's *level*: tradables producers systematically favor a weaker currency (more competitive at home and abroad), while nontradables producers and international investors favor a stronger one (`tradables_nontradables_level_preference`). The paper's broader argument is that rising international capital mobility doesn't just constrain which policies are technically available — it actively reshapes which domestic political coalitions form in the first place, shifting the salient fault line toward a tradables-versus-nontradables axis (`capital_mobility_reshapes_political_coalitions`). This is the theoretical machinery behind any specific country's political battle over its currency regime, including Brazil's own recurring cycles of election-driven currency uncertainty. Broz & Frieden's 2001 survey, extending the same lens to a broader field overview, remains an acquisition gap.

## 8. Capital controls: from taboo to official doctrine

For much of the period this literature covers, capital controls were treated in mainstream policy circles as, at best, a last resort and, at worst, a sign of policy failure — a position rooted partly in the "original sin" problem **Barry Eichengreen and Ricardo Hausmann** named in 1999: many emerging markets are structurally unable to borrow abroad in their own currency, a condition that shapes exchange-rate-regime choice and financial fragility independent of any single country's policy quality (a gap in the acquired base, flagged as the general theoretical anchor for any EM comparison beyond Brazil). The 2008–09 global financial crisis, and the subsequent surge of capital flows into emerging markets as advanced-economy central banks eased aggressively, forced a rethink. **Ostry, Ghosh, Habermeier, Chamon, Qureshi and Reinhardt's** 2010 IMF staff paper laid the empirical and pragmatic groundwork: a policy decision sequence for handling an inflow surge (let the currency appreciate, if undervalued; accumulate reserves; sterilize; adjust monetary/fiscal policy; and only then, under specific conditions, deploy capital controls), a four-condition justification test, and — the paper's most consequential empirical finding — evidence that controls have little robust effect on aggregate inflow *volume* or currency appreciation, but real, demonstrable effects on the *maturity composition* of external liabilities, plus improved resilience specifically for economies that had controls on debt inflows in place before the crisis hit (`capital_controls_effectiveness_stylized_facts`). Two years later, the **IMF's own 2012 Board paper** elevated this into the Fund's standing official doctrine — the "institutional view" — introducing the now-standard CFM/MPM terminology (capital flow management measures, targeting the flow itself, versus macroprudential measures, targeting systemic risk regardless of origin) and a liberalization sequencing framework: long-term before short-term, FDI before debt, inflows before outflows, with the explicit statement that "there is no presumption that full liberalization is an appropriate goal for all countries at all times" (`cfm_mpm_terminology_and_overlap`, `integrated_approach_liberalization_sequencing`). Brazil's own direct empirical test of this toolkit — Chamon & Garcia's 2016 study of the 2008–2012 IOF tax episode — remains an acquisition gap, and a natural next addition given how central Brazil's own capital-control history has been to the broader literature's case-study roster.

## 9. Putting a number on "fair value": two parallel valuation traditions

A separate strand of the base asks a more applied question: given all of the above, what is a currency actually *worth* right now? Two traditions have developed largely in parallel. The academic tradition, **BEER** (Behavioral Equilibrium Exchange Rate), estimates the real exchange rate as a cointegrated function of *actual* fundamentals — relative nontradable/tradable prices, terms of trade, the real interest rate differential, net foreign assets, and public debt — using Johansen vector error-correction methodology. **Claudio Paiva's** 2006 IMF working paper applies this directly to Brazil, and its central empirical finding is a useful corrective on its own: the real's roughly 25–40% real appreciation between 2003 and 2005 was found to be *largely an equilibrium phenomenon* — driven mostly by an improving net foreign asset position and terms of trade — not evidence of a currency running away from its fundamentals (`brazil_beer_2003_05_appreciation`). The practitioner tradition, Goldman Sachs's **GSDEER** and **GSFEER** models, takes a more calibrated, cross-country-tested approach: GSDEER is essentially a PPP model augmented with terms-of-trade and productivity components, while GSFEER is the macro-balance-approach cousin, linking the currency to the gap between the actual current account and its estimated sustainable "norm" (`gsdeer_model`, `gsfeer_model`). Neither approach uniformly dominates — Goldman's own research documents that the two models currently give *directionally different* signals for BRL specifically, concluding that carry and terms-of-trade shifts dominate BRL price action more than either fair-value anchor at present (`brl_gsdeer_gsfeer_divergence`) — a genuinely useful, humbling data point for anyone tempted to treat a single equilibrium-exchange-rate estimate as decisive.

## 10. The plumbing underneath the theory: Brazil's own market structure

All of the above is, in a real sense, abstract until it is grounded in how a specific market and a specific central bank actually operate. The **Banco Central do Brasil's** 2020 technical note supplies exactly that grounding for Brazil: a precise disambiguation of four related-but-distinct FX concepts the theory literature tends to blur together — *posição de câmbio* (the stock position), *saldo cambial* (the period flow), *disponibilidade cambial* (settled availability), and *exposição cambial* (the broadest, regulator-capped measure) — plus a concrete intervention toolkit distinguished by permanence: spot auctions (permanent), repo-style line auctions (temporary), and FX swaps (off-balance-sheet). A concrete, real-numbers case from 2019 shows the toolkit in action: facing a cumulative negative annual FX flow of roughly US$44 billion, the BCB sold US$36.9 billion via spot auctions while simultaneously repurchasing US$33.4 billion of FX swaps, cushioning the banking system's aggregate short position substantially below what it would otherwise have reached (`bcb_fx_intervention_toolkit`). This is the base's most concrete bridge between Mundell's abstract trilemma and Krugman's textbook peg-defense mechanics, on one side, and what a reader can actually watch the BCB do in real time, on the other.

## 11. Where this leaves a working macro desk

The academic and institutional arc above is not merely background reading — it is, largely without citation, the same set of mechanisms a real macro fund's own reasoning already runs on. Verde Asset Management's own mental-model documentation (a separate, practitioner-authored layer sitting alongside this academic bibliography — see `bibliography.md` cluster 9) treats fiscal credibility as the primary anchor of the BRL, in a direct, applied echo of the trilemma and the fixed/managed-rate-defense literature's insistence that fiscal deficits ultimately force a monetary/exchange-rate reckoning; it distinguishes a "carry trade" thesis for the real from a competing risk-premium narrative in exactly the terms Fama's 1984 decomposition formalized decades earlier; and it reads global risk appetite and Fed policy cycles through a lens that is, structurally, the same push/pull capital-flow framework the capital-controls literature uses to separate durable from reversible inflows. The academic literature supplies the mechanisms; a working desk's own judgment — about which mechanism dominates *right now*, and about political and fiscal developments no formal model prices in cleanly — is what turns the mechanism into a position.

## What this history deliberately leaves out

Three threads are visible in the base but intentionally not developed here, each for a documented reason: **exchange-rate pass-through to inflation** (Goldfajn & Werlang 2000, Belaisch 2003, Cortapasso et al. 2023) sits at an unresolved boundary with the inflation agent's own bibliography and is excluded from `bibliography.md` pending that decision; **FX options and volatility** has no representation in the base at all, academic or practitioner (the Garman-Kohlhagen 1983 gap); and **non-Brazil emerging-market depth** — RMB internationalization, a comparative "original sin" case beyond Brazil — remains thin, with Eichengreen & Kawai (2014) as the base's only current non-Brazil EM case study.
