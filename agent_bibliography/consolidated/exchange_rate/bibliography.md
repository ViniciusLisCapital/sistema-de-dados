# Exchange Rate — Consolidated Bibliography

**Purpose:** a single, presentable reading list for the exchange-rate literature base — merging the 28 sources already acquired and processed in `agent_bibliography/exchange_rate_policy/` with the not-yet-acquired candidates identified in `agent_bibliography/agent_mapping/recommended_bibliography/exchange_rate_bibliography_gaps.md`. This file does **not** distinguish which sources are already in hand vs. still to be sourced — the goal here is to map what the base needs to cover, not to track acquisition status (that remains the job of the gaps file). Organized into 8 of the 9 theme clusters used in `exchange_rate_conceptual_map.md`, plus a 9th cluster — practitioner mental models — that sits outside that map's academic taxonomy entirely.

**Excluded here: `#pass_through_and_inflation`.** That cluster sits at a genuine intersection between this exchange-rate bibliography and the inflation agent's own bibliography (`inflation_bibliography_candidates.md`) — which side of that boundary owns exchange-rate pass-through hasn't been decided yet, the same kind of explicit scope call other topics in this series have had to make (e.g. fiscal policy vs. monetary policy on fiscal dominance, in `fiscal_policy_bibliography_candidates.md`). Goldfajn & Werlang (2000), Belaisch (2003), and Araújo/Caldarelli/Cortapasso (2023) remain exactly where they are in `exchange_rate_conceptual_map.md` and `agent_bibliography/exchange_rate_policy/` — only removed from this consolidated list pending that ownership decision.

**Where this lives:** deliberately kept in a new `agent_bibliography/consolidated/` folder, separate from the working pipeline files (`exchange_rate_policy/`, `agent_mapping/conceptual_maps/`, `agent_mapping/recommended_bibliography/`, `agent_mapping/recommended_data/`). Nothing in those folders was modified to produce this file — this is a presentation-layer synthesis on top of them, not a replacement. Given the heavy overlap in source material, treat this folder as the one to update if the underlying files later change, rather than assuming the two stay in sync automatically.

**This is step (i) of a three-part deliverable.** Two more pieces follow in this same `consolidated/exchange_rate/` folder once this list is settled: (ii) a data inventory (what exchange-rate data series LIS tracks vs. should track) and (iii) the conceptual map integrated with that data. Not built yet.

**Foundational score:** 1–5, defined in `BIBLIOGRAPHY_METHODOLOGY.md` §c — canonicity, not recency. A score reflects how load-bearing the source is for its specific question, independent of publication date.

**Type note:** "Paper" covers journal articles and working papers; "Textbook chapter" and "CFA curriculum reading" are pedagogical syntheses rather than original contributions (scored accordingly — useful, current, rarely a 5); "Practitioner research note" covers bank/asset-manager research (Goldman Sachs); "Central bank technical note" and "Official institutional doctrine" cover primary policy-institution sources.

36 literature sources mapped across 8 clusters (25 already processed into the conceptual map, 11 identified gaps not yet acquired), plus a 9th cluster for practitioner "mental models" documents (2 entries: Verde done, Kinea pending).

## 2. `#exchange_rate_determination` — nominal, real, and equilibrium exchange rates

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Synthesis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Dornbusch, R. (1976), "Expectations and Exchange Rate Dynamics," *Journal of Political Economy* | Paper | 5 / 1976 | The founding overshooting model — sticky goods prices plus instantaneously-clearing asset markets mean a permanent monetary shock makes the exchange rate depreciate *more* than its long-run equilibrium level before appreciating back as prices catch up. The single most-cited explanation for why FX volatility so badly outpaces relative-price-level volatility. |
| Fama, E. (1984), "Forward and Spot Exchange Rates," *Journal of Monetary Economics* | Paper | 5 / 1984 | The original "wrong-signed beta" UIP-failure econometrics — the single most-cited empirical fact behind the forward-premium/carry-trade puzzle. Shows most of the variance in the forward-spot differential is risk-premium variance, not genuine forecast information, and catalogs the competing explanations (irrationality, official "leaning against the wind," the peso problem, Fama-Gibbons/Mundell-Tobin real-rate effects) the field still adjudicates between four decades later. |
| Froot, K. & Thaler, R. (1990), "Anomalies: Foreign Exchange," *Journal of Economic Perspectives* | Paper (survey) | 4 / 1990 | Accessible, non-technical survey of the forward-premium puzzle Fama's econometrics established — a synthesis companion for the intuition without re-deriving the regressions. |
| Krugman, Obstfeld & Melitz (2023), *International Economics: Theory and Policy*, Ch. 14 "Exchange Rates and the Foreign Exchange Market" | Textbook chapter | 4 / 2023 (ed.) | The standard textbook formalization of UIP as the FX market's core equilibrium condition, and the asset-approach reframing of exchange rates as forward-looking asset prices rather than goods prices — the spine the whole determination cluster organizes around. |
| Krugman, Obstfeld & Melitz (2023), Ch. 15 "Money, Interest Rates, and Exchange Rates" | Textbook chapter | 4 / 2023 (ed.) | Textbook derivation of Dornbusch overshooting via the money-market/interest-parity diagram, the long-run-monetary-neutrality "currency reform" intuition, and Latin American money-growth/inflation scatter evidence (1980–2014) grounding the quantity-theory logic empirically. |
| Krugman, Obstfeld & Melitz (2023), Ch. 16 "Price Levels and the Exchange Rate in the Long Run" | Textbook chapter | 4 / 2023 (ed.) | Long-run PPP/monetary-approach chapter — derives real interest rate parity as an accounting identity (not an empirical requirement), formalizes the Balassa-Samuelson effect behind BEER-style models, and reports Frankel & Rose's ~4-year PPP-deviation half-life, independently corroborating GSDEER's convergence-speed estimate. |
| CFA Institute (CFA Level II, 2025), "Currency Exchange Rates: Understanding Equilibrium Value" | CFA curriculum reading | 4 / 2025 | The densest single synthesizing source in the base (78pp) — ties UIP, CIP, ex ante PPP, real interest parity and the international Fisher effect into one master parity-conditions diagram, and is the direct source of the carry-trade return-distribution mechanics (fat tails, negative skew) used throughout the base. |

## 3. `#currency_regimes` — regime choice, OCA theory, and the trilemma

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Synthesis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mundell, R.A. (1961), "A Theory of Optimum Currency Areas," *American Economic Review* | Paper | 5 / 1961 | The founding OCA paper — defines the optimal domain for a shared currency as the region with high internal and low external factor mobility, not any particular political border. Directly prefigures the euro debate three decades early via the Meade-Scitovsky exchange cited in the paper. |
| Mundell, R.A. (1963), "Capital Mobility and Stabilization Policy Under Fixed and Flexible Exchange Rates," *Canadian Journal of Economics* | Paper | 5 / 1963 | The founding Mundell-Fleming result — under perfect capital mobility, monetary policy is powerful under floating rates and impotent under fixed rates, and fiscal policy is the mirror image. The paper the impossible trinity and every fixed-vs-floating policy-effectiveness argument in the base ultimately traces back to. |
| McKinnon, R. (1963), "Optimum Currency Areas," *American Economic Review* | Paper | 5 / 1963 | The second classic OCA criterion (economic openness) — the standard complement to Mundell (1961); together with Kenen (1969), completes the canonical Mundell/McKinnon/Kenen OCA trio still taught as the field's baseline. |
| Kenen, P. (1969), "The Theory of Optimum Currency Areas: An Eclectic View" | Paper | 5 / 1969 | The third classic OCA criterion (production diversification as a buffer against asymmetric shocks) — closes the canonical OCA trio alongside Mundell (1961) and McKinnon (1963). |
| Eichengreen, B. & Hausmann, R. (1999), "Exchange Rates and Financial Fragility," NBER Working Paper 7418 | Paper | 5 / 1999 | The paper that coined "original sin" — many emerging markets structurally cannot borrow abroad in their own currency, which shapes exchange-rate-regime choice and financial fragility independent of any single country's policy quality. The base's EM material is otherwise almost entirely Brazil-specific; this is the general theoretical anchor for comparison against other EMs. |
| Krugman, Obstfeld & Melitz (2023), Ch. 18 "Fixed Exchange Rates and Foreign Exchange Intervention" | Textbook chapter | 4 / 2023 (ed.) | The textbook mechanics of defending a peg — central bank balance-sheet mechanics, sterilized vs. non-sterilized intervention, the risk-premium channel under imperfect asset substitutability, and the reserve-currency asymmetry that let the US never have to intervene under Bretton Woods. |
| Eichengreen, B. & Kawai, M. (2014), "Renminbi Internationalization: Achievements, Prospects, and Challenges," Brookings/ADBI | Paper | 3 / 2014 | A concrete non-Brazil EM case study in managed-float/reserve-currency-status mechanics — a natural parallel to the BCB *posição de câmbio* material already in the base, partially closing the base's near-total lack of non-Brazil EM depth. |

## 5. `#policy_transmission` — monetary & fiscal policy under different regimes, and its politics

|---|---|---|---|
| Frieden, J. (1991), "Invested Interests: The Politics of National Economic Policies in a World of Global Finance," *International Organization* | Paper | 5 / 1991 | The canonical political-economy-of-exchange-rates paper — maps which domestic sectors (import-competing tradables, export tradables, nontradables, international investors) favor currency stability vs. policy autonomy, and a weak vs. strong currency. ## 6. `#applied_valuation_tools` — estimating the "equilibrium" exchange rate

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Synthesis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Paiva, C. (2006), "External Adjustment and Equilibrium Exchange Rate in Brazil," IMF Working Paper WP/06/221 | Paper | 4 / 2006 | Applies the BEER (Behavioral Equilibrium Exchange Rate) methodology to Brazil via Johansen cointegration — finds the real's 2003–05 appreciation was largely an equilibrium phenomenon (net foreign asset position + terms of trade), a direct empirical counterpoint to reading any large REER move as automatic misalignment. |
| Bhushan & Cahill, Goldman Sachs (2023), "GSDEER — A User's Guide" | Practitioner research note | 3 / 2023 | Goldman's own methodology note for its Dynamic Equilibrium Exchange Rate model (PPP adjusted for terms of trade and productivity) — documents which component actually drives fair value by currency (inflation for EUR/GBP/CAD, terms of trade for commodity exporters) and the empirical convergence timeline (~3 years to beat a random walk, ~5 years to fully converge, ~20%/year closure rate). |
| Alves & Jenkins, Goldman Sachs (2025), "Primer: GSDEER and GSFEER" | Practitioner research note | 3 / 2025 | Introduces GSFEER (the macro-balance-approach cousin to GSDEER, based on the current-account gap vs. its savings/investment "norm") and Goldman's own model-blending weights by horizon. Flags that GSDEER and GSFEER currently give *directionally different* fair-value signals for BRL specifically, concluding carry and terms-of-trade dominate BRL price action over either anchor. |

## 7. `#capital_controls` — restrictions on capital flows as a policy tool

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Synthesis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ostry, Ghosh, Habermeier, Chamon, Qureshi & Reinhardt, IMF (2010), "Capital Inflows: The Role of Controls," IMF Staff Position Note SPN/10/04 | Paper | 4 / 2010 | The empirical/pragmatic precursor to the IMF's later institutional view — lays out a policy decision sequence (let the currency appreciate → accumulate reserves → sterilize → adjust monetary/fiscal policy → controls as last resort) and a four-condition test for when inflow controls are justified. Finds controls have little robust effect on aggregate inflow volume or appreciation, but real, demonstrable effects on the *maturity composition* of external liabilities. |
| IMF Staff — Arora, Habermeier, Ostry, Weeks-Brown et al. (2012), "The Liberalization and Management of Capital Flows: An Institutional View," IMF Board Paper | Official institutional doctrine | 5 / 2012 | The IMF's own standing official doctrine on capital account liberalization and management — introduces the CFM/MPM terminology distinction, a liberalization sequencing framework ("long before short, FDI before debt, inflows before outflows"), and explicit design criteria (transparent, targeted, temporary, non-discriminatory) for when new controls are appropriate. The single most citable "what does official policy actually say" reference in this cluster. |
| Chamon, M. & Garcia, M. (2016), "Capital Controls in Brazil: Effective?" *Journal of International Money and Finance* | Paper | 4 / 2016 | Direct empirical test of Brazil's own 2008–2012 IOF-tax episode — the natural Brazil-specific complement the general capital-controls framework above is missing, and the base's most direct empirical evidence on whether Brazil's own use of the tool actually worked. |

## 8. `#currency_crisis_dynamics` — how currency crises build and break

| Source | Type | Score / Year | Synthesis |
|---|---|---|---|
| Krugman, P. (1979), "A Model of Balance-of-Payments Crises," *Journal of Money, Credit and Banking* | Paper | 5 / 1979 | The founding first-generation crisis model — a government financing a persistent deficit via money creation mechanically burns reserves every period regardless of speculation; rational speculators attack *before* exhaustion, at the exact moment a post-float price jump would otherwise hand them a windfall loss. The origin of "speculative attacks always come before reserves would have run out anyway." |
| Flood, R.P. & Garber, P.M. (1983/84), "Collapsing Exchange-Rate Regimes: Some Linear Examples," *Journal of International Economics* | Paper | 4 / 1983 | The standard linear, closed-form companion to Krugman (1979) — introduces the "shadow exchange rate" technique that makes collapse-timing solvable directly, and shows that fixing a rate doesn't eliminate self-fulfilling speculative-bubble risk, it just relocates *when* that risk manifests (as a sudden regime break rather than continuous volatility). |
| Obstfeld, M. (1984), "Balance-of-Payments Crises and Devaluation," NBER Working Paper 1486 | Paper | 5 / 1984 | The original second-generation, self-fulfilling crisis model — working-paper precursor to Obstfeld's canonical 1986 AER piece. Proves a government-policy-reaction-function contingent on the crisis itself is what makes multiple equilibria (and hence purely belief-driven collapses) possible; without that policy contingency, crises stay fully fundamentals-driven as in Krugman (1979)/Flood-Garber (1983/84). |
| Obstfeld, M. (1994), "The Logic of Currency Crises," NBER Working Paper 4640 | Paper (survey) | 3 / 1994 | Accessible single-source synthesis bridging first- and second-generation crisis logic — useful if a unified narrative is preferred over the two dense original papers above. |
| Chang, R. & Velasco, A. (1998), "Financial Crises in Emerging Markets: A Canonical Model," Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta WP 98-10 / NBER WP 6606 | Paper | 5 / 1998 | One of the two competing third-generation models of the 1997–98 Asian crisis — an open-economy Diamond-Dybvig bank-run model with a precise, falsifiable illiquidity criterion. Shows currency-board, fixed-with-LOLR, and floating-with-LOLR regimes share the *same* underlying vulnerability to a crisis; the regime only determines whether it surfaces as a bank run or a currency collapse. |
| Krugman, P. (1999/2000), "Balance Sheets, the Transfer Problem, and Financial Crises," *IMF Staff Papers* | Paper | 5 / 2000 | The other competing third-generation model — a pure private-sector balance-sheet/currency-mismatch feedback loop (no government reserves or deficit anywhere in the story), plus the classical Keynes-Ohlin "transfer problem" applied to sudden-stop current-account reversals. Notable as Krugman's own explicit public reversal on self-fulfilling crises ("I hereby capitulate... Maury Obstfeld was right"), driven by the 1997–98 crisis's contagion pattern. |
| Garcia, M. & Valpassos, M. (1998), "Capital Flows, Capital Controls and Currency Crisis: The Case of Brazil in the Nineties" | Paper | 3 / 1998 | Ties crisis theory directly to Brazil's own 1999 Real devaluation — the Brazil-specific case-study complement this cluster's otherwise entirely non-Brazil academic model set is missing. |
