# GSDEER—A User's Manual

**30 May 2023 | 3:12PM BST**

## Executive Summary

- There have been large swings in GSDEER fair value estimates over the last three years. The surge in energy prices has boosted Terms of Trade for commodity exporters, and in turn raised those currencies' 'fair value'. While the surge in inflation has been common across countries, differentials across countries and sectors may accumulate in coming quarters as central banks face different trade-offs.

- Fair value is an equilibrium concept, so analysis is required before determining how to incorporate these developments into spot FX views. It takes roughly three years for fair-value-based FX forecasts to outperform a random walk and roughly five years for spot to converge to fair value on average. This is consistent with the "rule of thumb" that the real exchange rate tends to converge to the underlying trend at a rate of roughly 20% per year. Beyond five years, spot tends to overshoot fair value, although there is even more uncertainty at this horizon.

- Looking across the G10, GSDEER forecasts perform best for AUD, EUR, CAD, and NZD, and the model provides a "firm anchor" for GBP, JPY, and CHF. This means that spot is less likely to have meaningful deviations from fair value, even if exact spot dynamics are harder to forecast. The inflation component of GSDEER works well for EUR, GBP, and CAD, which is logical because these countries have the strongest linkages with the US. The terms of trade component is naturally important for commodity exporters, although NOK is a notable outlier.

- Three key implications emerge from the results:
  1. Investors should take seriously the stronger fair value estimate for AUD because it is a currency where GSDEER forecasts spot well, especially when terms of trade are driving fair value estimates.
  2. Weaker fair value estimates for EUR and GBP suggest that valuation-driven boosts for these currencies should not be expected.
  3. The stronger NOK fair value should be discounted because it is driven by the terms of trade component, which is a poor forecaster of medium-term FX moves for this cross.

## Take the Long Way Home

There have been large swings in G10 GSDEER fair value estimates over the last three years. Big changes in commodity prices and inflation differentials have a direct impact on the fundamental value of currencies (changes in productivity would also have an impact, but this has not been as central of an issue lately). The surge in energy prices has boosted Terms of Trade for commodity exporters, and in turn raised those currencies' 'fair value'. This is especially true for NOK and AUD, where GSDEER has strengthened by over 10% (Exhibit 1). And while inflation differentials have not been too large yet given the common nature of the shock for most countries, there is scope for differentials across countries and sectors to accumulate in coming quarters as central banks face different trade-offs.

**Exhibit 1: Our GSDEER Fair Value Estimates Have Moved Sharply Higher for NOK and AUD but Weaker for EUR and GBP**

Source: Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research

However, moving fair value estimates may not translate into spot dynamics for a while. Even in the case of a currency such as Sterling, where fair value models work relatively well, there can occasionally be large and persistent mis-valuations (Exhibit 2). In the short-run, broad global macro variables and country-specific news will be more important for FX and dominate the signal from equilibrium fair value concepts.

**Exhibit 2: There Can Occasionally Be Large and Persistent Deviations from Fair Value**

Source: Bloomberg, Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research

To empirically assess at what horizon the GSDEER becomes most useful, we extend our recent forecasting evaluation analysis. Specifically, we assess the forecasting performance of GSDEER using an out-of-sample approach. In short, this means that we only use data up to a certain date in constructing fair value estimates and fair-value-based forecasts for the nominal exchange rate.¹

**Exhibit 3: It Takes Roughly Three Years for Fair Value Estimates to Outperform a Random Walk**

Source: Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research

Exhibit 3 plots the root mean square error (a measure of forecasting accuracy) relative to a random walk and R-squared (a measure of 'goodness of fit') for GSDEER at each horizon out to ten years. In line with the discussion above, it is roughly three years before fair-value-based FX forecasts outperform forecasting using a random walk. The relative performance of GSDEER improves rapidly over a five-year horizon, and substantially outperforms both a random walk and FX forwards (Exhibit 4). The model's forecasting performance continues to improve beyond this timeframe, but at a much slower pace.

**Exhibit 4: Forecasting Using GSDEER Meaningfully Outperforms Forwards**

For data availability reasons, our sample evaluation starts in 1999 and we exclude SEK, NOK, and NZD.

Source: Bloomberg, Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research

Consistent with this, GSDEER valuation gaps fully close only after roughly five years with a nearly linear 20% per annum convergence rate until then (Exhibit 5, blue line). Further out, spot tends to hover around fair value before overshooting at the eight-to-ten-year horizon. To test if the overshoot simply reflects that fair value is a moving target, we repeat the exercise but focus on the convergence of the valuation gap to zero, or the convergence of spot to future fair value. Logically, convergence for the valuation gap (red line) is slightly quicker and there is somewhat less of an overshoot but the difference is minimal.

**Exhibit 5: It Takes Roughly Five Years for Spot to Converge to Fair Value**

Source: Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research

Digging into the overshoot further, Exhibit 6 plots the convergence coefficients across countries in box and whisker format. Reasonably, there is a wider range of convergence coefficients across countries at longer horizons, especially relative to the tight distribution around full convergence at the six-year horizon. Put together, the empirical evidence suggests investors should not be surprised if currencies continue to appreciate or depreciate once they reach fair value, although there may be too much uncertainty to explicitly forecast this sort of overshoot long into the future.

**Exhibit 6: There Is a Wider Range of Convergence Coefficients Across Countries at Longer Horizons**

Source: Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research

Reassuringly, these results are broadly consistent with the longstanding "rule of thumb" for long-term FX forecasts, which was derived using a slightly different technique. Consistent with the academic literature on the topic, we have found that real exchange rates tend to mean revert over time, with a prevailing consensus of a half-life of roughly three-to-five years. As a result, we generally assume a 20% convergence rate in long-run forecasts.

## Choose the Right Vehicle

Even considering that GSDEER works best at longer horizons on average, Exhibit 7 shows that there are some countries where there is barely any convergence out to five years. Replicating Exhibit 3 for each cross individually, we find that GSDEER forecasts best for AUD, EUR, CAD, and NZD. The model works very slightly less well for JPY and GBP but performance here is still much better than using a random walk. However, there is a large gap to European satellites where our traditional GSDEER underperforms a random walk even at the five-year horizon.

**Exhibit 7: GSDEER Forecasts Best for AUD, EUR, CAD, and NZD, and Poorly for European Satellites**

Source: Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research

Exhibit 8 documents the convergence path for each cross individually. Conceptually, fair value would be the "firmest anchor" if spot converges quickly with a limited overshoot. The results from this exercise are broadly similar to Exhibit 7. On this metric, GBP and JPY perform rather well, with GBP converging towards fair value particularly quickly. AUD and CAD are more prone to overshooting, while the Scandi currencies converge very slowly.

**Exhibit 8: GSDEER Converges Fastest for Sterling**

## Test an Alternate Route

There are a number of ways to assess where fair value is the "firmest anchor". Our results in the previous section show where spot tends to converge to fair value (as in Exhibit 5 and Exhibit 6), but it is also useful to know where spot does not diverge from fair value. Exhibit 9 presents two examples to visualise the difference. In the first case (light blue line), currencies that converge quickly will cross fair value (dark blue line) frequently. On the other hand, the grey line would score poorly on convergence metrics, but fair value still provides a useful anchor as spot never gets too far away.

**Exhibit 9: Fair Value Is a Firm Anchor if Spot Converges Towards It or if Any Divergence Is Limited**

Source: Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research

To assess the extent to which a currency sticks closely to fair value, without necessarily converging, we construct a probit model for each cross where the probability of further divergence is based on the level of current mis-valuation. Exhibit 10 presents our results for the case of a 10% valuation gap. Our results suggest that GBP, CHF, and JPY perform best by this metric. The Scandi currencies once again underperform.

**Exhibit 10: GBP, CHF, and JPY Are Less Likely to Diverge Significantly from Fair Value**

Source: Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research

Put together, our results suggest that there are three groups of currencies. First, GSDEER forecasts best for AUD, EUR, CAD, and NZD. However, it is arguably a firmer anchor for GBP, JPY, and CHF—these crosses are less likely to have large deviations from fair value, even if the model does not necessarily predict exact spot dynamics. Exhibit 11 demonstrates how this looks in practice. AUD can meaningfully diverge from fair value, but converges and overshoots relatively quickly. On the other hand, large mis-valuations in CHF are rare, but can be relatively persistent. Looking across these two criteria, GBP and JPY have the best of both worlds. Finally, the traditional GSDEER model does not work well for Scandi FX, although our recent work suggests that other inflation measures could work better for these currencies.

**Exhibit 11: AUD Can Meaningfully Diverge From Fair Value, but Converges and Overshoots Relatively Quickly; Large Mis-Valuations in CHF Are Rare, but Can Be Relatively Persistent**

Source: Bloomberg, Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research

## Pick the Best Driver

GSDEER performs differently across countries for two main reasons.² First, data quality is not the same everywhere and we have already highlighted the difference that inflation measurement can make. Second, the model is also conceptually more appealing for certain crosses. This is because GSDEER is essentially a PPP model adjusted for terms of trade among G10 crosses where productivity differentials are not too large (Exhibit 12). As a result, GSDEER is not best equipped to capture fair FX value for countries where international trade is relatively small and where limited linkages means that the law of one price does not hold.

**Exhibit 12: GSDEER Is Similar to a PPP Model for JPY and CHF but Terms of Trade Drive Fair Value Estimates for Commodity Currencies**

Source: Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research

Put differently, fair value estimates derived in this way may be more useful if we look at what is driving the change in fair value and how useful that component typically is for a given cross. To test this, we construct component-level GSDEERs where we focus on each potential individual driver (Terms of Trade, productivity and inflation) separately, and run the same out-of-sample forecast evaluation. We find that the inflation component of GSDEER works best for EUR, GBP, and CAD (Exhibit 13, left), which is logical because these countries have the strongest linkages with the US, and so are where the law of one price is most applicable.

**Exhibit 13: The Inflation Component of GSDEER Is Useful for Countries with Relatively Close Linkages to the US, While the Terms of Trade Component Is Useful for Most Commodity Exporters**

Source: Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research

The results for the Terms of Trade component also appear sensible as this is particularly important in commodity currencies, such as CAD and AUD (Exhibit 13, right). Interestingly, Terms of Trade performs poorly for NOK (Exhibit 14). At first glance, this seems surprising given its large amount of oil and gas exports. However, Norway's unique fiscal mechanism (by which the government invests excess revenues internationally) blunts the impact of commodity prices on the currency.

**Exhibit 14: Although Terms of Trade Underperforms in Norway Because of Its Unique Fiscal Mechanism**

Source: Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research

## Navigate the Next Turns

We see three key implications of our results.

### AUD Fair Value Deserves Serious Consideration

First, investors should take seriously the stronger fair value estimate for AUD (0.94) because it is a currency where GSDEER forecasts spot well, especially when the Terms of Trade is driving fair value estimates as is currently the case (Exhibit 15). As a result, we estimate that AUD is roughly 40% undervalued against USD. And our earlier results (Exhibit 8) suggest that AUD tends to converge to fair value relatively quickly—about four years (and then overshoots). In light of this, our own longer-term forecasts imply over 20% AUD appreciation against USD.

### EUR and GBP Fair Value Is Weaker

Second, weaker fair value estimates for EUR (1.21) and GBP (1.27) suggest that we should not expect too much of a valuation-driven boost for these currencies. Indeed, GBP is roughly already at our fair value estimate and EUR is only undervalued by 10% after the rally so far this year. Even though it is still slightly undervalued, GSDEER is a less strong anchor for EUR relative to other crosses where GSDEER works well. Indeed, both currencies are already near our 12-month forecast against the Dollar.

### NOK Fair Value Should Be Discounted

Finally, it is advisable to discount the stronger NOK fair value (5.55) because this is driven by the terms of trade component, which is a poor forecaster of medium-term FX moves for this cross. While our long-term forecasts do incorporate NOK strength, we still expect it to be significantly undervalued by GSDEER at that horizon.

## Appendix: Explaining How We Assess the Performance of the GSDEER Model

We assess the forecasting performance of our GSDEER models using an out-of-sample approach. The left panel below explains the methodology taking the case of evaluation as of January 1995. We start by constructing the GSDEER time series at this date. Only using the in-sample data, we estimate a regression to calculate the estimated change in spot at various future horizons based on the level of mis-valuation at a given point in time. We then use this relationship to estimate the change in spot at each horizon after 1995 (up to 10 years). We then repeat this process for each quarter, using an expanding window for the in-sample data. Finally, we calculate the RMSE or R-squared using these forecasts and the realized out-of-sample values. In contrast, the right panel shows how an in-sample approach would differ. Here, we would regress the entire latest GSDEER (2023Q2) on the history of realized data and then evaluate the predicted values. This gives the GSDEER an advantage because, by construction, both series have the same average value.³
