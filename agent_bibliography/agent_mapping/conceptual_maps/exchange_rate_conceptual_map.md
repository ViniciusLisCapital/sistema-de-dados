## Concepts by theme

### #market_microstructure — FX market structure & quoting

- **[[fx_market_participants]]** — buy-side (corporate, real money, leveraged/hedge funds, retail, governments, central banks, sovereign wealth funds) vs. sell-side (tiered dealing banks); financial-client flow now exceeds interbank flow. *(capital_flow_fx_market)*
- **[[fx_market_composition]]** — spot/outright forward/FX swap turnover split (BIS Triennial Survey: swaps ~49%, spot ~33%, forwards ~14%, options ~5%); London > New York > Tokyo as trading hubs. *(capital_flow_fx_market)*
- **[[exchange_rate_quoting_conventions]]** — base vs. price currency; direct vs. indirect quotes; three-letter vs. six-letter codes; bid/offer two-sided pricing; quoting hierarchy (EUR > GBP > USD as base currency). *(capital_flow_fx_market)*
- **[[cross_rate_calculation]]** — deriving the exchange rate between two currencies from two other quotes sharing a common third currency (e.g. CAD/EUR from CAD/USD × USD/EUR), sometimes requiring one quote to be inverted first so the intermediary currency cancels. *(exchange_rate_calculation)*
- **[[triangular_arbitrage]]** — riskless arbitrage across three currencies when a directly-quoted cross-rate is inconsistent with the rate implied by its two component quotes; in practice competed away almost instantly by human and algorithmic traders. *(exchange_rate_calculation)*
- **[[forward_points_and_premium_discount]]** — forward rates are quoted as "points"/pips relative to spot; a currency at a forward premium (points positive, forward > spot) is the base currency being priced above spot; points scale roughly with the interest-rate differential × time to maturity. *(exchange_rate_calculation)*
- **[[vehicle_currency]]** — a currency (the USD, overwhelmingly — 88.3% of all FX trades in 2019) that gets used as an intermediary even when neither counterparty wants dollars, because it's cheaper to trade CHF→USD→ILS than to find a direct CHF/ILS counterparty. Explains why "cross-rate" calculation ([[cross_rate_calculation]] above) is a routine market operation rather than a curiosity. *(exchange_rate_foreign_exchange_market)*

### #market_microstructure (continued) — Brazil's FX plumbing (BCB)

- **[[posicao_de_cambio]]** (FX position) — the accumulated net result, since inception, of an authorized institution's FX purchase/sale contracts, updated daily; can be **nivelada** (matched), **comprada** (net long FX), or **vendida** (net short FX). The BCB explicitly distinguishes four related-but-distinct concepts that are easy to conflate: **posição de câmbio** (the stock, as above) vs. **saldo cambial** (the period *flow* — purchases minus sales, excluding BCB's own operations — whose commercial + financial components build up the country's international reserves over time) vs. **disponibilidade cambial** (actual settled FX balance available for immediate use — differs from the position because contracted-but-unsettled trades move the position immediately but the availability only at settlement) vs. **exposição cambial** (the broadest measure: all FX-linked assets/liabilities/derivatives combined, the *only* one of the four subject to a regulatory limit — 30% of regulatory capital, Res. 3.488/2007). Useful precision for reading any BCB-published FX market data. *(posicao_de_cambio)*
- **[[posicao_vendida_carry_incentive]]** — the balance-sheet-level mirror of the household/investor [[carry_trade]] already in this map: Brazilian banks have a financial incentive to run a *short* FX position ("posição vendida") — borrow/hold foreign currency liabilities, convert to reais, and lend domestically at the (typically higher) local rate — profiting from the interest-rate differential net of hedge costs and foreign-funding costs ("custo de posição vendida"). This is literally the supply-side counterpart of the demand-side [[carry_trade]] logic: someone has to be short the funding currency and long the local one for the trade to clear, and in Brazil that's often the banking system's own FX book. *(posicao_de_cambio)*
- **[[bcb_fx_intervention_toolkit]]** — concrete instruments distinguished by *permanence*: **leilões à vista** (spot auctions, buy or sell) permanently change the domestic FX supply and reserves; **leilões de linha com recompra / operações conjugadas** (repo-style paired buy-sell auctions) provide *temporary* FX liquidity without a lasting reserves change; **swaps cambiais** (FX swap derivatives) let the BCB manage FX exposure/hedging demand off-balance-sheet without spot market impact. Concrete, well-quantified case: Aug–Dec 2019, facing a cumulative negative saldo cambial of ~US$44.4bn for the year, the BCB sold US$36.86bn via spot auctions (drawing down reserves) while *simultaneously repurchasing* US$33.38bn of FX swaps — cushioning the banking system's short position to end 2019 at US$33.9bn short rather than a projected US$70.7bn short without intervention. A precise, real-numbers illustration of the abstract [[sterilized_intervention]] and [[fx_intervention_effectiveness_evidence]] concepts already in this map, from the technical-plumbing side rather than the textbook-model side. *(posicao_de_cambio)*
- **[[cupom_cambial_spread]]** — the BCB's own named market gauge for "the cost of dollar liquidity in Brazil": the spread of the **cupom cambial** (the BRL-hedged implied dollar rate, derived from the DI-futures/dollar-futures market) over US Treasury yields. This is Brazil's local, market-specific instance of the [[cip_basis_breakdown]] concept already in this map (Krugman Ch. 18) — a widening cupom cambial spread signals FX-hedged dollar funding is getting expensive/scarce onshore, which is exactly the kind of stress that triggers the BCB's spot-auction intervention described above. Worth watching as a real-time indicator, not just a textbook curiosity. *(posicao_de_cambio)*

### #exchange_rate_determination — nominal, real, and equilibrium

- **[[nominal_vs_real_exchange_rate]]** — real exchange rate = nominal rate × (foreign price level / domestic price level); rises in the real rate mean falling relative purchasing power. *(capital_flow_fx_market)*
- **[[purchasing_power_parity]]** (PPP) — long-run equilibrium theory; persistently violated in practice (goods heterogeneity, trade barriers, capital flows dominating trade flows); Big Mac Index as illustration. *(capital_flow_fx_market)*
- **[[uncovered_interest_rate_parity]]** (UIP) — theoretical basis for expecting real interest-rate differentials to move the exchange rate (higher domestic real rate → currency appreciation, other things equal); invoked directly as a building block of the [[behavioral_equilibrium_exchange_rate]] model, and implicit in Mundell's perfect-capital-mobility assumption in [[mundell_fleming_model]]. **Full formal treatment (Krugman, Ch. 14):** UIP — which Krugman just calls "interest parity," the equilibrium condition for the whole FX market — states that expected returns on deposits of any two currencies must be equal when measured in the same currency: R$ = R€ + (Eᵉ−E)/E (domestic interest rate = foreign interest rate + expected rate of foreign-currency appreciation). This is an *asset-market* equilibrium condition (see [[asset_approach_to_exchange_rates]]), not a goods-market one — the exchange rate adjusts instantly whenever expected returns diverge, since deposits can be reallocated instantly while goods markets clear slowly. Diagrammatically: a rise in the domestic interest rate → immediate currency appreciation (to restore parity by raising the currency's expected future depreciation); a rise in the foreign interest rate, or a rise in the expected future exchange rate, → immediate depreciation. *(equilibrium_exchange_brazil; exchange_rate_foreign_exchange_market)*
- **[[asset_approach_to_exchange_rates]]** — treats the exchange rate as the relative price of two assets (currency deposits) rather than of goods: today's rate depends on expected future returns, exactly like a stock price depends on expected future dividends/prices. This reframes the whole determination question as "what makes market participants willing to hold the existing stock of deposits of every currency" rather than "what balances currency flows from trade." Sets up the expectations-driven volatility explored later (Dornbusch overshooting, monetary models). *(exchange_rate_foreign_exchange_market)*
- **[[carry_trade]]** — borrowing in a low-interest "funding" currency (historically JPY) to invest in a high-interest "investment" currency (historically AUD, BRL), profiting from the interest differential *unless* UIP holds and the funding currency's expected appreciation exactly offsets it. Empirically profitable on average over long periods but exposed to sudden, large currency crashes (AUD/JPY fell from ¥100 to ¥65 between July–Dec 2008). Krugman's textbook explanation: a **[[peso_problem]]** — investors rationally expect a high-probability small gain plus a low-probability large loss (crash), so realized returns look like free money most of the time even though the return is fairly priced ex ante; risk/liquidity effects (crash losses hit hardest exactly when liquidity is most valuable, i.e. during financial crises) are a complementary explanation. This is the real-world, textbook-canonical version of the [[forward_rate_as_predictor_puzzle]] flagged from the CFA reading. **CFA L2 adds the return-distribution mechanics:** carry trade returns are not normally distributed — more peaked around the mean (many small gains) but with fat tails and *negative skew* (occasional large losses), because the payoff structure is "gradual income accrual, occasional sudden capital loss." Leverage (borrowing in the funding currency) amplifies volatility of returns on equity, and crashes are self-reinforcing: rising vol triggers stop-losses → mass unwinding → selling pressure on the investment currency and buying pressure on the funding currency → bigger crash. One documented explanation for the persistence of carry returns: high-yield currencies carry a genuine risk premium for economic instability, not a free lunch. *(exchange_rate_foreign_exchange_market; exchange_rate_understanding_equilibrium)*
- **[[international_parity_conditions_synthesis]]** — the master diagram tying together every parity concept in this map: covered interest rate parity (arbitrage-enforced, always holds in normal conditions) → uncovered interest rate parity (behavioral, frequently fails) → ex ante PPP (expected %ΔS = expected inflation differential) → **real interest rate parity** (if UIP and ex ante PPP both hold, real rates equalize across countries: r_f = r_d) → **international Fisher effect** (if real rates are equal, the nominal yield spread is explained purely by the expected inflation differential — CFA explicitly distinguishes this from UIP, which some other textbooks confusingly also call "the international Fisher effect"). If *all* of these held simultaneously, forward rates would be unbiased predictors of future spot rates and no currency strategy could earn consistent excess returns — the empirical failure of UIP (evidenced by the profitable carry trade) is exactly what breaks this chain in practice. *(exchange_rate_understanding_equilibrium)*
- **[[real_interest_rate_parity]]** — real interest rates should converge across countries if UIP and ex ante PPP both hold; a genuine bridge concept between the interest-rate-parity family and the PPP family. Breaks down when currency risk differs across countries — e.g. an indebted emerging market's measured "real rate" (nominal minus expected inflation) runs persistently higher than in stable markets because it's actually compensating for currency/default risk, not offering a genuinely higher risk-free real return. *(exchange_rate_understanding_equilibrium)* **Krugman's refinement:** real interest parity, re_US − re_E = expected %Δ(real exchange rate), is actually just an *identity* that falls straight out of nominal UIP plus the definition of the real rate (r = R − πᵉ) — it doesn't by itself require real rates to be equal across countries, only relative PPP does that. Persistent real-rate differentials are therefore consistent with no-arbitrage whenever a real exchange rate trend is genuinely expected — e.g. if the market expects ongoing Balassa-Samuelson-style productivity catch-up in a country's tradables sector, that implies an expected long-run real appreciation of its currency, which *by the identity* implies its real interest rate should be lower than the rest of the world's, indefinitely, without anyone being able to arbitrage the gap (investors in each country are still earning the same real return in terms of their *own* consumption basket — the baskets themselves just aren't equalized by relative PPP). Useful corrective to a common misreading of "real rates should converge" as a hard empirical prediction rather than a conditional one. *(price_levels_exchange_rate_lr)*
- **[[fisher_effect_uip_ppp_paradox_resolution]]** — a practically important synthesis point this map has been building toward across several sources: **the same observation ("interest rate rose") can imply opposite currency reactions depending on why the rate moved.** In the short-run sticky-price world ([[overshooting_model]], Krugman Ch. 14-15): a rate rise driven by *tighter money* (lower expected future inflation, unchanged long-run exchange rate path) → currency **appreciates** now, since UIP requires less future depreciation to compensate. In the long-run monetary-approach world (this chapter, flexible prices, ongoing money growth): a rate rise driven by *higher expected inflation* (faster money-supply growth going forward) → currency **depreciates** immediately, since PPP implies faster future depreciation, and UIP requires the higher rate precisely to compensate holders for that faster depreciation. Formally, combining UIP with relative PPP gives R_domestic − R_foreign = πᵉ_domestic − πᵉ_foreign (the [[international_fisher_effect]] derived from first principles rather than asserted) — interest differentials reflect *inflation* differentials once you're in a flexible-price, ongoing-inflation setting, not liquidity conditions. **Practical takeaway:** never interpret a central bank rate move on the exchange rate without first asking whether the market reads it as a tightening-for-credibility signal (bullish) or as validation of a higher permanent inflation path (bearish) — the CFA L2 and Dornbusch material already in this map describe each case separately; this is the explicit bridge explaining why they don't contradict each other. *(price_levels_exchange_rate_lr)*
- **[[balassa_samuelson_effect]]** — faster productivity growth in the tradables sector lowers tradables' relative price, and the resulting relative-price shift is balanced by a real appreciation; used in [[equilibrium_exchange_brazil]] as the rationale for including the nontradables/tradables price ratio in the [[behavioral_equilibrium_exchange_rate]] model. *(equilibrium_exchange_brazil)* **Empirical grounding (Krugman Ch.16, the "Penn effect"):** price levels, converted to a common currency, rise systematically with a country's real income per capita — a dollar goes much further in a poor country. Two competing explanations, both predicting cheaper nontradables in poor countries but via different mechanisms: **Balassa-Samuelson** (productivity gap concentrated in tradables → lower wages → cheaper nontradables, which are labor-intensive) vs. **Bhagwati-Kravis-Lipsey** (poor countries have low capital/labor ratios → lower marginal product of labor → lower wages → cheaper labor-intensive nontradables, with no productivity-gap assumption needed at all). Both mechanisms matter for interpreting any GDP-per-capita-based valuation channel (as in [[gsdeer_model]] or [[beer_fundamentals]]) applied to a wide income range of countries. *(price_levels_exchange_rate_lr)*
- **[[ppp_deviation_half_life]]** — Frankel & Rose's (1996) panel-data estimate that temporary deviations from relative PPP have a roughly **4-year half-life** — directly comparable to, and independently corroborating, [[gsdeer_horizon_dynamics]]'s "3–5 year convergence, ~20%/year" rule of thumb from an entirely different (Goldman practitioner) methodology and sample. Two independent lines of evidence converging on the same multi-year mean-reversion speed for real exchange rates is a genuinely reassuring cross-check for any house view built on that assumption. *(price_levels_exchange_rate_lr)*
- **[[icp_ppp_measurement_methodology]]** — the World Bank's International Comparison Program is the actual data infrastructure behind any "PPP-adjusted GDP" figure (used, e.g., to compare China/India's economic weight to the US on a PPP basis rather than at market exchange rates). Constructed via worldwide 6-yearly surveys of prices across ~155 "basic headings," aggregated via transitivity/country-invariance conditions. **Two caveats worth remembering before citing any PPP-adjusted statistic:** (1) reported error margins of ±15% for economies with large price/structural heterogeneity, explicitly including **China, India, and Brazil**; (2) the World Bank's own explicit caution that ICP-style PPPs "should not be equated to equilibrium exchange rates" — they measure relative purchasing power, not currency over/undervaluation, so they are not a substitute for [[behavioral_equilibrium_exchange_rate]] or [[gsdeer_model]]-style fair-value estimates despite superficially answering a similar-sounding question. *(price_levels_exchange_rate_lr)*
- **[[overshooting_model]]** (Dornbusch, 1976) — the original, fully worked-out source of the concept forward-linked from three earlier sources (Goldfajn 2000's forecast-bias discussion, Krugman Ch.14's expectations framework, and CFA L2's monetary-approach section). Core mechanism: asset markets (money, FX) clear **instantly** via UIP-style arbitrage (r = r* + expected depreciation), while goods-market prices adjust **slowly** (sticky in the short run, fully flexible in the long run). A permanent monetary expansion must eventually raise prices and depreciate the currency in exact proportion (long-run monetary neutrality/homogeneity — no money illusion). But because prices can't jump immediately, the initial monetary easing lowers the real interest rate; for UIP to hold *instantly* despite unmoved future expectations, the currency must depreciate **more than its long-run equilibrium depreciation** (de/dm = 1 + 1/(λθ) > 1) so that the market expects it to *appreciate back* at a rate matching the reduced interest rate — that immediate excess depreciation, self-correcting over time as prices catch up, **is** the overshoot. Degree of overshooting rises with a *lower* interest-elasticity of money demand and a *slower* price-adjustment/expectations coefficient (θ) — i.e., stickier goods markets → bigger overshoot. *(expectations_and_exchange_rate_dynamics)* **Textbook derivation (Krugman Ch. 15):** the same result via the money-market/interest-parity diagram rather than Dornbusch's differential equations — a permanent money supply increase lowers R (short-run liquidity effect) *and* raises the expected future exchange rate (since long-run depreciation is proportional to the money increase); both effects shift the FX equilibrium in the *same* direction, producing an immediate depreciation larger than the eventual long-run depreciation; as prices then rise toward their new long-run level, real balances fall, the interest rate rises back toward R*, and the currency partially *appreciates* back to its long-run level — tracing out the same overshoot-then-partial-reversal path. **Krugman also supplies the empirical motivation Dornbusch's paper only asserts:** month-to-month USD/JPY exchange rate changes are far more volatile than month-to-month changes in the US/Japan relative price level ratio — direct evidence for the sticky-price assumption the entire overshooting mechanism depends on. *(money_interest_rate_exchange_rates)*
- **[[long_run_monetary_neutrality_currency_reform_analogy]]** — Krugman's intuition pump for why a permanent money-supply change can't affect real variables (output, real interest rate, relative prices) in the long run: it's economically identical to a **currency reform** (e.g., Turkey's 2005 reform replacing 1,000,000 old lira with 1 new lira) — purely a relabeling of the unit of account, with zero effect on real economic allocation. A money-supply doubling is "the same as" a reform where every unit of currency is replaced by two — all nominal prices (including the exchange rate) eventually double, nothing real changes. This is the plain-language version of the homogeneity property already used in [[overshooting_model]] and [[monetary_approach_to_exchange_rates]]. *(money_interest_rate_exchange_rates)*
- **[[money_growth_inflation_empirical_evidence]]** — direct empirical grounding for the "pure monetary model" backdrop already flagged in [[monetary_approach_to_exchange_rates]]: a scatter of Latin American countries' annual money-supply growth against annual inflation, 1980–2014 (log-log axes), clusters tightly around the 45° line — strong confirmation that money supply and price levels move together long-run, exactly as the quantity-theory logic predicts, even though short-run deviations (driven by output/velocity shifts) are common. *(money_interest_rate_exchange_rates)*
- **[[hyperinflation_case_studies]]** — Venezuela (late 2010s) and Zimbabwe (2007-09) as extreme, "laboratory condition" tests of the money-supply/price-level link: both driven by government money-printing to cover fiscal deficits (direct real-world instance of [[central_bank_deficit_financing]] above, taken to its logical extreme). Zimbabwe's price level rose by a factor of ~36.7 million between Jan 2007 and July 2008 (monthly inflation exceeded 33,000,000% at one point); four separate currency redenominations failed to stabilize it; the government eventually **dollarized** (adopted the USD, suspending the Zimbabwean dollar entirely) and adopted a hard fiscal rule (spend only tax revenue, no more money-printing) — a direct real-world instance of the [[dollarization]]-as-fiscal-discipline-import argument already flagged from source 1's currency-regime taxonomy. Inflation collapsed immediately upon dollarization, illustrating just how completely a fixed/foreign nominal anchor can override domestic monetary indiscipline — at the cost (as befell Zimbabwe again post-2016 with "bond notes" and the Zimdollar) of needing genuine current-account/FDI/remittance inflows to source the anchor currency once local money-printing is off the table. *(money_interest_rate_exchange_rates)*
- **[[rational_expectations_perfect_foresight_path]]** — Dornbusch's methodological innovation: rather than assuming an arbitrary expected-depreciation rule, he solves for the *one* value of the expectations coefficient (θ) that makes agents' expectations exactly match the model's own actual convergence speed — i.e., expectations that are never systematically wrong given the model's true structure. This is the deterministic ancestor of "rational expectations" in exchange rate modeling: expectations aren't a free assumption, they're *derived* jointly with the price/exchange-rate dynamics they're supposed to predict. *(expectations_and_exchange_rate_dynamics)*
- **[[overshooting_interest_rate_comovement]]** — a genuine paradox worth flagging for market commentary: *during the adjustment path back to long-run equilibrium* (after the initial overshoot-depreciation), domestic prices are rising, real money balances are falling, interest rates are rising, **and the currency is appreciating** — all at once. So "rising rates → stronger currency" can be observationally true along Dornbusch's adjustment path, but for a different reason than the naive UIP intuition (higher rates attracting inflows on the way up): here it's the *unwinding* of an initial overshoot, driven by the economy converging back toward equilibrium after the shock, not a fresh attractive yield differential. Useful caution against reading "rates up, currency up" as confirmation of any single mechanism. *(expectations_and_exchange_rate_dynamics)*
- **[[overshooting_with_variable_output]]** — Dornbusch's own robustness check (Part V/Appendix): if short-run output can expand in response to a monetary easing (rather than being fixed), rising income raises money demand, which can *increase* interest rates instead of lowering them — in which case the exchange rate depreciates by *less* than the long-run proportional amount (undershooting, not overshooting), and terms-of-trade swings are dampened rather than amplified. Whether the model overshoots or undershoots is therefore an empirical question about income/price elasticities, not a universal law — a caution against treating "monetary easing ⇒ overshooting depreciation" as automatic. *(expectations_and_exchange_rate_dynamics)*
- **[[covered_interest_rate_parity]]** (CIP) — the no-arbitrage identity, not a behavioral theory: F/S = (1+r_foreign)/(1+r_domestic). Derived from the equivalence of (a) investing at home vs. (b) converting to foreign currency, investing abroad, and locking in the reconversion with a forward contract ("swap financing"). Holds by construction wherever capital can move freely and forwards are tradeable — the currency with the higher interest rate always trades forward at a discount, and vice versa. This is the rigorous version of the "arbitrage relationship" gestured at qualitatively in [[fx_market_composition]] above. Note: CIP and UIP coincide (F equals the market's true expected future spot rate) only if forward rates are unbiased predictors of future spot rates — a condition that itself frequently fails. *(exchange_rate_calculation)*
- **[[cip_basis_breakdown]]** — a genuine post-2008 anomaly: CIP held very tightly for decades but has *not* reestablished itself since the 2007–08 financial crisis, even after bank balance sheets recovered (Du, Tepper & Verdelhan, 2018). Persistent negative cross-currency bases mean a textbook-riskless CIP arbitrage (borrow USD → convert → invest abroad → hedge forward → convert back) appears profitable on paper, but market participants lack the balance-sheet capacity/regulatory room to exploit it fully. Useful caution: even the most "mechanical" no-arbitrage FX relationships can break down for reasons unrelated to counterparty default. *(exchange_rate_foreign_exchange_market)*
- **[[forward_rate_as_predictor_puzzle]]** — if forward rates are naively read as the market's expected future spot rate (Fₜ = E[Sₜ₊₁]), algebra with CIP implies %ΔS ≈ (r_foreign − r_domestic) — i.e., the higher-yielding currency should be expected to *depreciate* by roughly the rate differential, exactly offsetting the extra yield. Empirically forward rates are poor predictors of future spot rates, and the higher-yielding currency does *not* reliably depreciate as much as CIP-based interpretation implies. This is effectively the [[uncovered_interest_rate_parity|UIP]] failure / "forward premium puzzle," and it is the standard textbook explanation for why [[carry_trade|carry trades]] (going long the high-yield currency, funded in the low-yield one) have historically earned positive average returns rather than breaking even. *(exchange_rate_calculation)* — note: `carry_trade` and `uip`/`risk_premium` already exist as concept pages in the Obsidian vault (`obsidian/exchange_rate/concepts/`), not touched by this map, but worth cross-checking once this map is reconciled with the vault. **The original econometric source (Fama, 1984) is now in the base — see below.**
- **[[fama_regression_decomposition]]** — the formal origin of the "UIP regression" that every later carry-trade/forward-premium-puzzle paper (including the CFA-textbook narrative captured in [[forward_rate_as_predictor_puzzle]] above) ultimately traces back to. Fama splits the forward rate into a premium and an expected future spot rate, Fₜ = E(Sₜ₊₁) + Pₜ, then runs two *complementary* regressions on the current forward-spot differential Fₜ − Sₜ: (3) the future premium-plus-forecast-error, Fₜ − Sₜ₊₁, with slope β₁, and (4) the future spot-rate change, Sₜ₊₁ − Sₜ, with slope β₂ — where β₁ + β₂ = 1 always, by construction. Conditional on market efficiency/rationality, β₂ measures the proportion of the variance of Fₜ − Sₜ due to genuine information about the future spot-rate change, while β₁ measures the proportion due to the (rationally priced) premium — a considerably more precise decomposition than the single regression coefficient usually quoted in later textbook retellings. *(foward_spot_exchange)*
- **[[wrong_signed_beta_uip_regression]]** — the paper's headline empirical result, and the single most-cited "wrong-signed beta" in this literature: using nine major currencies against the USD over 1973-1982 (thirty-day forward rates, four-week sampling), the slope β₂ in regression (4) is **reliably negative** for nearly every currency — meaning a currency currently at a forward *premium* (implying, via CIP, a *lower* domestic interest rate) tends to subsequently **appreciate further**, rather than depreciate as naive UIP would predict. Equivalently, the complementary slope β₁ in regression (3) is **reliably greater than 1.0** rather than the value of 1.0 that market efficiency alone (with a constant or zero premium) would imply. This is the literal origin of the "forward premium puzzle" / "Fama puzzle" nomenclature used throughout the later academic literature, and the precise quantitative ancestor of the qualitative story already in this map's [[forward_rate_as_predictor_puzzle]] entry. *(foward_spot_exchange)*
- **[[premium_variance_dominance]]** — arguably the paper's most consequential finding for practical FX analysis, flagged by Fama himself as "more startling" than the wrong-signed beta: **most of the variation in the forward-spot differential Fₜ − Sₜ is variation in the risk premium Pₜ, not variation in the rationally expected future spot-rate change E(Sₜ₊₁ − Sₜ)**. In other words, forward rates mostly move around because required risk compensation is fluctuating, not because the market is updating its genuine view of where the currency is headed — a foundational reason why forward-implied and interest-differential-implied depreciation paths are poor trading signals on their own, directly reinforcing this map's existing [[carry_trade]] and [[peso_problem]] discussion of why carry-trade-style strategies can earn persistent average profits without those profits reflecting pure market irrationality. *(foward_spot_exchange)*
- **[[negative_premium_expected_change_covariance]]** — the specific statistical mechanism Fama identifies as *necessary* to produce the wrong-signed beta (not just "premium variance is large," but something sharper): the risk premium Pₜ and the expected future spot-rate change E(Sₜ₊₁ − Sₜ) must be **negatively correlated** for β₂ to come out negative — algebraically, a large enough negative covariance between the two components can more than offset a positive variance of E(Sₜ₊₁ − Sₜ) in the regression coefficient formula. Fama is candid that "a good story for negative covariation... is difficult to tell," and devotes the paper's final section to competing candidate explanations rather than asserting one — an intellectually honest posture that later got compressed, in secondary/textbook treatments, into the flatter claim that "UIP just fails." *(foward_spot_exchange)*
- **[[fama_1984_candidate_explanations]]** — Fama's own catalog of competing explanations for the negative premium/expected-change covariance, notable for anticipating essentially the entire subsequent 40 years of forward-premium-puzzle research in a few paragraphs: (1) **market inefficiency/irrationality** — investors' forecasts of future spot rates are systematically, persistently perverse rather than rational, the least parsimonious explanation since it requires irrationality that doesn't improve even after a full decade of floating-rate experience; (2) **government intervention "leaning against" market forces** (suggested by Richard Roll) — governments defend a currency *more* vigorously precisely when fundamentals (like an inflation differential) most strongly indicate it should depreciate, mechanically generating a negative correlation between the interest-differential-implied premium and the actual subsequent move; (3) **the "doomsday theory"** (suggested by Michael Mussa) — brief episodes of small-probability, large-magnitude devaluation risk skew the *ex ante* distribution of expected changes, so that *ex post* realized changes in any actual (necessarily finite) sample systematically undershoot the true ex ante mean — this is precisely the same **[[peso_problem]]** mechanism already in this map from the Krugman carry-trade discussion, now traced to its original methodological source; (4) **negative correlation between the expected-real-return and expected-inflation components of nominal interest rates** (the Mundell-Tobin effect, or Fama's own Fama-Gibbons mechanism) combined with **stochastic deviations from PPP** driving country-specific risk premia (formalized via Stulz's 1981 asset-pricing model) — a genuinely structural, rational-expectations-consistent story requiring no market inefficiency at all. Fama offers no verdict among these — a useful reminder that the "UIP fails" empirical fact has *never* had a single settled causal explanation, only a menu of candidates that current research (including the peso-problem and risk-premium material already in this map) continues to adjudicate between. *(foward_spot_exchange)*
- **[[interest_rate_parity_premium_link]]** — the paper's theoretical setup, connecting the forward premium directly to this map's existing parity-conditions cluster: since [[covered_interest_rate_parity|CIP]] pins the forward-spot differential to the nominal interest-rate differential (no-arbitrage, holds by construction with open capital markets), *any* premium in the forward rate must ultimately be explainable in terms of that interest-rate differential. Under the joint hypothesis of complete PPP *and* the Fisher equation holding in both countries, Fama shows algebraically that the premium collapses exactly to **the difference between the two countries' expected *real* returns on their nominal bonds** — i.e., a genuine FX risk premium is, under these assumptions, indistinguishable from a cross-country real-interest-rate/risk differential, not some separate currency-specific mystery term. This gives formal teeth to this map's existing [[real_interest_rate_parity]] entry: the "compensation for currency risk" language used there is, in Fama's framework, literally the same object as the forward premium being decomposed here. *(foward_spot_exchange)*

### #currency_regimes — regime choice & the trilemma

- **[[impossible_trinity]]** (a.k.a. the trilemma / "Ideal Currency Regime") — a country cannot simultaneously have a credibly fixed exchange rate, full capital mobility, and independent monetary policy; must give up one. *(capital_flow_fx_market)*
- **[[currency_regime_taxonomy]]** — IMF's spectrum: dollarization → monetary union → currency board → fixed parity → target zone → crawling peg/band → managed float → independent float; more discretion for the central bank as the regime moves toward floating. *(capital_flow_fx_market)*
- **[[historical_monetary_systems]]** — classical gold standard (price-specie-flow mechanism) → Bretton Woods (1944) → 1973 float → European "snake"/ERM → 1992 sterling crisis → euro (1999). *(capital_flow_fx_market)*
- **[[central_bank_fx_intervention]]** — Plaza Accord (1985), concerted G5 intervention to support the euro (2000), unilateral interventions against currency strength (2010); even "independent float" regimes intervene. *(capital_flow_fx_market)*
- **[[central_bank_balance_sheet_mechanics]]** — the mechanical link underlying every FX intervention: the central bank balance sheet has foreign assets + domestic assets on one side, currency in circulation + private bank deposits on the other; any asset purchase (foreign or domestic) automatically expands the money supply (it must, since assets = liabilities), and any asset sale automatically contracts it. This is *why* a non-sterilized FX purchase to weaken the currency is money-supply-expansionary, and why defending a currency (selling FX reserves) is automatically money-supply-contractionary — the intervention and the domestic monetary effect are not two separate choices, they're the same transaction, unless offset by [[sterilized_intervention]] below. *(fixed_exchange_rate_intervention)*
- **[[fixed_rate_mechanics]]** — to hold E fixed permanently, a central bank must keep the domestic interest rate equal to the world rate (since UIP with zero expected depreciation requires R = R*), which means it must let its money supply move passively to absorb any shock to money demand (e.g., an output increase raising money demand must be met with a central bank FX purchase that expands the money supply, or the currency would appreciate). This is the formal mechanism behind the qualitative result already in the map from Mundell (1963) and CFA L2: **under a fixed rate, the money supply becomes endogenous — a residual of defending the peg, not a policy lever.** Non-sterilized monetary policy is therefore mechanically self-defeating under a credible fixed rate: any attempted expansion via domestic asset purchases triggers an offsetting FX outflow that shrinks the money supply right back, with the net effect being purely a *swap of central bank domestic assets for a loss of reserves* — output unchanged. Fiscal policy, conversely, becomes *more* potent than under floating: the intervention needed to hold the peg while fiscal expansion raises money demand forces an accommodating money supply increase that floating exchange rates would not deliver (there, currency appreciation partly offsets the fiscal boost instead). *(fixed_exchange_rate_intervention)*
- **[[devaluation_revaluation_dynamics]]** — technical distinction: depreciation/appreciation are market outcomes under floating rates; devaluation/revaluation are deliberate government decisions to reset a fixed peg. Three classic motives for devaluing: (1) boost aggregate demand when monetary policy is unavailable (pegged) and fiscal policy is politically constrained; (2) improve the current account; (3) rebuild depleted reserves by making the currency cheap enough to reverse outflows. Long-run neutrality holds regardless of regime: a devaluation under a fixed rate has the *same* long-run effect as a proportional money-supply increase under floating — a one-time nominal reset with no permanent real effect, once prices catch up. *(fixed_exchange_rate_intervention)*
- **[[sterilized_intervention]]** — a central bank fully offsetting the money-supply effect of an FX operation with an equal, opposite domestic-asset transaction (matches the concept already flagged from [[mundell_1963]]'s "neutralization policy," now given modern balance-sheet mechanics). Under **perfect asset substitutability** (the base UIP world used throughout this map), sterilized intervention is mechanically powerless — with the money supply unchanged, the domestic interest rate and hence the UIP-determined exchange rate can't move, so sterilization is "self-defeating" whenever it's used to *resist* the exchange rate effects of a policy like fiscal expansion under a fixed rate. Under **imperfect asset substitutability** — domestic and foreign bonds are risky in different degrees — sterilized intervention *can* move the exchange rate, via a **risk premium channel**: r = r(B − A), rising in the net stock of domestic-currency government debt the private market must hold (govt debt B minus the central bank's own domestic asset holdings A). A sterilized FX purchase (sell domestic assets, buy foreign) raises B − A, raises the required risk premium on domestic bonds, and depreciates the currency — with the money supply held constant throughout. This formalizes the currency-risk-premium idea already flagged under [[real_interest_rate_parity]]. **Empirical verdict:** little evidence of a reliable effect in developed markets (deep, liquid bond markets absorb any B−A change cheaply); more evidence of effectiveness in markets with less-developed financial systems, which is why sterilized intervention is more commonly practiced by EM central banks — consistent with the DM/EM asymmetry already noted in [[fx_intervention_effectiveness_evidence]]. *(fixed_exchange_rate_intervention)*
- **[[signaling_effect_of_intervention]]** — even where sterilized intervention has no direct mechanical effect (perfect asset substitutability case), it can still move the exchange rate by signaling the central bank's *future* monetary/fiscal intent — the central bank visibly puts its own balance sheet at risk (it will lose money on the position if it doesn't follow through), lending credibility to a policy announcement. This only works as long as it's backed by real policy follow-through; a central bank that signals repeatedly without delivering suffers a "cry wolf" credibility loss and the signal stops moving markets. *(fixed_exchange_rate_intervention)*
- **[[reserve_currency_asymmetry]]** — in a reserve-currency system (e.g. the USD-centered Bretton Woods system, 1945–1973), with N countries there are only N−1 exchange rates against the reserve currency; if all N−1 non-reserve countries are busy pegging to it, there's no exchange rate left for the reserve-issuing country itself to defend. The reserve center therefore never has to intervene and — uniquely among fixed-rate participants — retains full freedom to run independent monetary policy despite everyone else being pegged to its currency. This is the structural origin of the "exorbitant privilege" the USD has historically enjoyed, and connects directly to [[vehicle_currency]] above (same underlying asymmetric role, applied to reserves rather than transaction flow). *(fixed_exchange_rate_intervention)*
- **[[seigniorage]]** — under a currency board the monetary authority earns the spread between zero-interest liabilities and yield on FX reserves; under dollarization that seigniorage goes to the anchor country instead. *(capital_flow_fx_market)*

### #currency_regimes (continued) — Optimum Currency Area theory (Mundell, 1961)

- **[[optimum_currency_area_definition]]** — Mundell's foundational OCA paper (distinct from, and earlier than, his 1963 capital-mobility paper already in this map as [[mundell_1963]]): defines a currency area as a domain within which exchange rates are fixed, then asks what the *appropriate* domain of such an area is — deliberately decoupling the question from national borders. His answer: the optimum currency area is **the region**, defined as a zone with high *internal* factor mobility (labor and capital move freely within it) and low *external* factor mobility (factors don't move freely across its boundary) — not any particular political unit. A genuinely different lens on [[currency_regime_taxonomy]] already in this map: that entry classifies regimes by *how tightly* a currency is pegged; this paper asks the logically prior question of *what geographic unit* should even share a currency in the first place. *(theory_optimum_currency_areas)*
- **[[interregional_vs_international_adjustment_asymmetry]]** — Mundell's core illustrative model, and a direct structural companion to [[mundell_fleming_model]] and [[monetary_fiscal_policy_effectiveness_by_regime]] already in this map (from his 1963 paper): consider a demand shift from region/country B's goods to region/country A's goods. With **separate national currencies and flexible rates**, country B's currency depreciates (or A's appreciates), directly correcting the imbalance while insulating both from unemployment and inflation — "the most favorable case for flexible rates." But with a **single common currency** across the same two regions (no exchange rate to adjust), the *entire* adjustment burden falls on monetary policy, and monetary policy can fix unemployment in the deficit region *or* restrain inflation in the surplus region, but never both — "full employment thus imparts an inflationary bias" to a common-currency area, just as "the policy of surplus countries in restraining prices imparts a recessive tendency" to a multi-currency area on fixed rates. This is the precise mechanism-level reason OCA membership is a real economic tradeoff, not just a convenience question — and a clean bridge back to this map's [[impossible_trinity]]: giving up the exchange-rate adjustment margin (by sharing a currency) doesn't just cost monetary independence in the abstract, it costs the *specific* ability to have low unemployment and low inflation simultaneously across heterogeneous regions. *(theory_optimum_currency_areas)*
- **[[cross_cutting_regions_currency_mismatch_paradox]]** — Mundell's sharpest and most counter-intuitive result, via a deliberately constructed example (Canada/US, but with an "East" that makes cars and a "West" that makes lumber products, cutting across the national border): a flexible *national* exchange rate (CAD/USD) does nothing to solve an imbalance between economic *regions* that don't align with the national currency boundary — East and West still face the exact same "can't have full employment and price stability simultaneously" dilemma as under a common currency, because the CAD/USD rate is the wrong instrument for the actual economic fault line. The general lesson: flexible national exchange rates are a solution only if national borders happen to coincide with the true factor-mobility boundaries between economically distinct regions — when they don't (a country with strongly regionally-differentiated production structures), national currency flexibility can be almost irrelevant to the underlying adjustment problem. Mundell uses this to predict, boldly and explicitly in the paper itself, that Canada's own then-recent experiment with a floating exchange rate would be "largely unsuccessful" as an interregional stabilization tool precisely because Canada's East-West economic structure — not its national border — is where the real adjustment problem lives. *(theory_optimum_currency_areas)*
- **[[optimal_number_of_currencies_tradeoff]]** — the paper's resolution to an apparent reductio ad absurdum of its own logic: if the stabilization argument alone determined currency-area size, it would seem to imply ever-smaller currency areas (down to "every minor pocket of unemployment... a separate region, each with a separate currency"), which Mundell calls absurd on its face. He identifies three genuine countervailing forces that push toward *larger*, *fewer* currency areas: (1) **money's function as numeraire and medium of exchange degrades** as the number of currencies grows — with N currencies equal to the number of commodities, money "would disappear" and trade would revert to barter, a direct transaction-cost argument for currency consolidation; (2) **thin FX markets become manipulable** — with too many small currency areas, individual speculators (central banks excepted) could move the market price, strengthening the classic "destabilizing speculation" objection to floating rates; (3) **the required degree of money illusion in wage bargaining becomes implausible** as currency areas shrink and import shares of consumption rise — the flexible-rate mechanism only works if workers accept real-income changes via exchange-rate-driven import price shifts without demanding fully compensating nominal wage adjustments, and this becomes less credible the smaller (and more import-dependent) the currency area gets. The synthesis: OCA size is a genuine optimization between a *stabilization* argument (favoring smaller/more numerous currency areas matched to factor-mobility regions) and a *transaction-cost-plus-market-depth* argument (favoring larger/fewer) — "optimum" literally means the balance point between these two forces, not a corner solution either way. *(theory_optimum_currency_areas)*
- **[[oca_application_european_monetary_union_debate]]** — a remarkably prescient (1961, three decades before Maastricht) direct application to what became the euro debate, framed as a genuine empirical disagreement rather than a theoretical one between two named economists: **Meade** argues the Common Market countries lack the internal factor mobility (specifically labor mobility) required for a viable common currency, and therefore favors national currencies with flexible rates between them; **Scitovsky** favors a common currency for Western Europe, but on the different ground that adopting one would *itself* induce greater capital mobility over time (reversing the usual causality — currency union as a driver of factor mobility, not just a consequence of pre-existing mobility) while separately conceding that labor mobility and supranational employment policy would still need to be built. Mundell's own framing: both economists agree the region (defined by internal mobility/external immobility) is the right OCA unit in principle — they simply disagree empirically about whether Western Europe *is* such a region, or could be made into one. Genuinely useful historical context for evaluating the euro's own actual OCA credentials decades later (persistent north-south current account imbalances, limited intra-EU labor mobility relative to the US) against the criteria this very paper laid out. *(theory_optimum_currency_areas)*

### #balance_of_payments — trade, capital flows, and identities

- **[[trade_balance_capital_account_identity]]** — X − M = (S − I) + (T − G); a trade deficit/surplus must be mirrored by an offsetting capital account surplus/deficit. Capital flows dominate exchange rate determination short-to-medium term; trade flows dominate long term. *(capital_flow_fx_market)*
- **[[flow_of_funds_sectoral_balances]]** — 1963 precursor to the same identity: a 4×4 sector (government/private/foreign/banking) × market (goods/securities/money/reserves) accounting table where every sector's deficit is financed by an identified counterpart flow; ex-ante the row/column sums are equilibrium conditions, ex-post they're accounting identities. *(mundell_1963)* → same logical skeleton as [[trade_balance_capital_account_identity]] above, ~40 years earlier.
- **[[current_account_adjustment_channels]]** — three distinct mechanisms by which persistent current account imbalances feed into the exchange rate, useful because they operate on different time horizons and can point in different directions simultaneously: (1) **flow supply/demand channel** — trade flows directly move currency demand (slow, subject to sluggish pass-through and demand-elasticity lags, per [[distribution_chain_pass_through]] above); (2) **portfolio balance channel** — persistent deficits shift financial wealth to surplus-country holders, who may rebalance away from the deficit country's assets once their holdings exceed desired portfolio weights; (3) **debt sustainability channel** — rising external debt/GDP eventually forces a downward revision of the market's own estimate of the currency's long-run equilibrium value, independent of any flow effect. Empirically, capital/financing decisions dominate exchange rates in the short-to-intermediate run (financial markets clear instantly; goods markets are sluggish) while current-account trends dominate over the long run — directly consistent with the [[asset_approach_to_exchange_rates]] logic from Krugman Ch. 14. Case evidence: the USD had three multi-year depreciation cycles (1977–78, 1985–87, 2002–08), each preceded by a widening current account deficit coinciding with low US real rates — i.e., the debt-sustainability channel dominating once capital inflows stopped compensating for the flow-channel pressure. *(exchange_rate_understanding_equilibrium)*
- **[[national_income_identity_open_economy]]** — Y = C + I + G + EX − IM, and the precise vocabulary the map has been using loosely up to now: **GNP** (output by a country's factors of production, wherever located) vs. **GDP** (output within a country's borders, regardless of who owns the factors) vs. **GNI** (GNP without the depreciation adjustment) — these diverge whenever a country has significant cross-border factor ownership (e.g. foreign-owned plants). "Domestic absorption" A ≡ C+I+G lets the current account be written compactly as CA = Y − A: a country's current account balance is just income minus total (domestic+government) spending. *(national_income_balance_payments)*
- **[[twin_deficits_identity]]** — a more granular decomposition of the trade_balance_capital_account_identity already in this map (X−M = (S−I)+(T−G) from source 1): splitting national saving S into private saving Sp and government saving Sg = (T−G) gives **Sp = I + CA + (G−T)** — private saving must be absorbed by domestic investment, net foreign asset purchases (current account surplus), or financing the government's budget deficit. This is the formal "twin deficits" logic: holding private saving behavior fixed, a widening government deficit (G−T rising) must show up as some combination of lower investment and a more negative current account. Useful discipline before asserting a causal link between a specific country's fiscal deficit and its currency — the identity holds by construction, but which term actually absorbs the deficit (I, CA, or Sp itself adjusting) is an empirical question, not given by the accounting alone. *(national_income_balance_payments)*
- **[[balance_of_payments_accounting_structure]]** — a terminology correction worth flagging, since this map (following the more casual usage in CFA/Mundell/earlier Krugman chapters) has been using "capital account" loosely to mean cross-border investment flows generally. In the modern (BPM6) accounting standard actually used here: **current account** (goods + services + primary income [interest/dividends/earnings on foreign investment] + secondary income [unilateral transfers]) + **capital account** (a typically small residual: nonmarket transfers like debt forgiveness, and transfers of nonproduced/nonfinancial assets) = **financial account** (net acquisition of foreign assets vs. net incurrence of liabilities to foreigners — this is what most of this map's other sources casually call "the capital account" or "capital flows"). Enforced by double-entry bookkeeping: every international transaction generates one credit and one debit somewhere in the accounts, so CA + KA ≡ FA by construction. In practice, imperfect and mismatched data sources across the credit/debit halves of each transaction produce a **statistical discrepancy** line that reconciles the accounts. *(national_income_balance_payments)*
- **[[global_current_account_discrepancy_puzzle]]** — since every country's exports are some other country's imports, global current account balances should sum to zero — they don't, and the sign of the persistent gap has flipped over time (negative/"missing surplus" through the early 2000s, positive/"missing deficit" since), suggesting a *systematic* measurement bias (likely under-reported investment income and/or mismeasured services trade) rather than random noise. A useful caution: even the most authoritative-looking aggregate BOP statistics carry meaningful, non-random measurement error. *(national_income_balance_payments)*
- **[[gdp_measurement_distortion_case]]** — Ireland's real GDP was revised to show 26.3% growth in 2015 (later 25.1%), driven almost entirely by multinationals relocating intellectual-property assets to Ireland to exploit its low 12.5% corporate tax rate — an accounting phenomenon with essentially no matching change in employment or real economic activity. A sharp illustration that GDP (and any GDP-denominated ratio — debt/GDP, current account/GDP, and by extension any [[behavioral_equilibrium_exchange_rate]]-style model using GDP-scaled fundamentals) can be badly distorted by tax-driven multinational accounting choices, not just by real economic developments — a data-quality caution in the same family as [[gsdeer_cross_country_heterogeneity]]'s Norway/NOK case, though the mechanism (tax arbitrage vs. sovereign wealth fund) is different. *(national_income_balance_payments)*
- **[[capital_flow_push_pull_factors]]** — "pull" factors are domestic policy improvements that attract foreign capital (lower/less volatile inflation, more exchange rate flexibility, fiscal improvement, privatization, financial liberalization); "push" factors originate abroad, independent of the recipient country's own policy (low rates in advanced economies post-2008 driving search-for-yield into EM; secular EM weight increases in global benchmark indices). Distinguishing the two matters because pull-driven inflows are more likely to reflect improving fundamentals (and be durable) while push-driven inflows are more likely to reverse abruptly when the external driver changes (e.g. G3 rate hikes) — the seed of [[capital_flow_surge_reversal]] below. *(exchange_rate_understanding_equilibrium)*

### #policy_transmission — monetary & fiscal policy under different exchange rate regimes

- **[[mundell_fleming_model]]** — open-economy IS-LM-BP framework (XX = internal/goods-market balance, LL = money market, FF = external/BP balance, dominated by the world interest rate under perfect capital mobility). The paper that the [[impossible_trinity]] is ultimately built on: under perfect capital mobility a country cannot set its own interest rate independent of fixing the exchange rate. *(mundell_1963)*
- **[[monetary_fiscal_policy_effectiveness_by_regime]]** — the paper's central, still-taught result: with perfect capital mobility, **monetary policy is powerful under floating rates / impotent under fixed rates**; **fiscal policy is powerful under fixed rates / impotent under floating rates**. Mechanism: any move in the domestic interest rate away from the world level triggers capital flows large enough to either force it back (fixed rate, via reserve changes) or move the exchange rate until the demand effect is fully offset (floating rate). *(mundell_1963)*
- **[[sterilization_policy]]** — central bank offsets FX-intervention effects on the money supply via offsetting open-market operations. Shown to be internally inconsistent under fixed rates + perfect capital mobility: it produces an accelerating, self-reinforcing capital flow that ends only when reserves are exhausted or the exchange rate is allowed to move. *(mundell_1963)*
- **[[central_bank_deficit_financing]]** — when the central bank directly monetizes a fiscal deficit under a fixed exchange rate, the paper shows reserves fall one-for-one with the deficit — an early formal statement of the fiscal-deficit → reserve-loss channel later central to [[fiscal_dominance]]-style arguments (see fiscal_policy vault). *(mundell_1963)*
- **[[monetary_fiscal_policy_mix_capital_mobility]]** — CFA L2's modern restatement of Mundell-Fleming, split explicitly by capital mobility regime (a refinement Mundell's 1963 paper doesn't make explicit, since it assumes perfect mobility throughout). **High capital mobility (developed markets):** tight money + loose fiscal (both raising real rates) is unambiguously currency-bullish; loose money + tight fiscal is unambiguously bearish; same-direction policy mixes are indeterminate. **Low capital mobility (many EMs):** the channel shifts from capital flows to the trade balance — loose money + loose fiscal is bearish (higher aggregate demand → more imports), tight + tight is bullish, mixed cases indeterminate. Same policy announcement can therefore move a currency in opposite directions depending on how open the capital account is — a first-order consideration before applying Mundell-Fleming intuition to any specific country. Case example: Germany 1990-92 (expansionary fiscal for reunification + restrictive Bundesbank monetary policy → sharp DEM appreciation, high-mobility case). *(exchange_rate_understanding_equilibrium)*
- **[[monetary_approach_to_exchange_rates]]** — an alternative to Mundell-Fleming that takes the *opposite* stance on the transmission channel: output is fixed, and money supply changes affect the exchange rate via the price level (through PPP) rather than via interest rates and capital flows. Pure form: an X% money supply increase → X% domestic price increase → X% currency depreciation (assumes PPP holds continuously — its main weakness, since PPP demonstrably doesn't hold in the short-to-medium run per [[purchasing_power_parity]] above). **Dornbusch's (1976) fix** — cited directly here — assumes sticky short-run prices but flexible long-run prices: a money supply increase still depreciates the currency proportionally in the long run (PPP restored), but in the short run, sticky prices mean the same money-supply shock lowers the domestic interest rate, triggers a capital outflow, and causes the currency to **overshoot** — depreciate *beyond* its new long-run equilibrium — before appreciating back as prices catch up. This is the [[overshooting_model]] forward-linked from the Goldfajn (2000) pass-through paper — see the full formal treatment now added under `expectations_and_exchange_rate_dynamics` below. *(exchange_rate_understanding_equilibrium)*
- **[[portfolio_balance_approach]]** — addresses what Mundell-Fleming leaves out: the long-run stock effect of sustained fiscal deficits. Investors hold diversified domestic/foreign bond portfolios; a growing deficit increases the supply of domestic government bonds, which investors will only hold at a higher expected return — achieved via (a) higher rates/risk premium, (b) an immediate currency depreciation large enough to create expected future appreciation, or (c) both. Combined with Mundell-Fleming, this implies a **short-run/long-run reversal**: expansionary fiscal policy under high capital mobility can appreciate the currency initially (Mundell-Fleming effect, higher real rates) but depreciate it later as accumulating debt raises sustainability concerns and risk premia, potentially forcing debt monetization — connecting directly to [[central_bank_deficit_financing]] above and to [[fiscal_dominance]] in the fiscal_policy vault. *(exchange_rate_understanding_equilibrium)*

### #policy_transmission (continued) — the political economy of exchange rate policy (Frieden, 1991)

- **[[sectoral_exchange_rate_preferences_framework]]** — the paper's central analytical contribution, and the founding theoretical treatment of a topic this map previously covered only narratively (via Cortapasso et al.'s Brazilian election-uncertainty discussion): a 2×2 mapping of which domestic economic actors want *policy autonomy* (a flexible exchange rate) versus *currency stability* (a fixed/managed rate), built directly on the specific-factors/Ricardo-Viner trade model layered onto [[mundell_fleming_model]]. **Import-competing tradables producers** (capital tied to a specific, price-sensitive domestic sector) are largely indifferent to volatility but care about retaining policy autonomy. **Export-oriented tradables producers** are exposed to FX risk on foreign sales and therefore favor *currency stability*. **Nontradables producers** (construction, most domestic services) have no direct FX exposure and are the group most interested in retaining *national monetary policy autonomy* to manage domestic demand. **International investors and traders** — the group least tied to any one country's real economy — most strongly favor *currency stability*, since exchange-rate volatility directly threatens the value of their cross-border asset holdings. This is the theoretical machine behind any specific country's political battle over "should we peg / join a currency union / float" — a genuine complement to this map's existing [[impossible_trinity]] and [[currency_regime_taxonomy]], which describe the trilemma's *economic* logic but not *who, domestically, wins and loses* from each corner solution. *(invested_interest)*
- **[[tradables_nontradables_level_preference]]** — a second, distinct axis of political conflict, orthogonal to the flexibility-vs-stability question above: on the *level* of the exchange rate (not its volatility), tradables producers systematically favor a **weaker/depreciated** currency, since depreciation raises the domestic-currency price of tradables relative to nontradables (their output becomes more price-competitive at home and abroad), while nontradables producers and international investors/traders favor a **stronger/appreciated** currency (a stronger currency raises the relative price — and hence the domestic purchasing power — of nontradables output, and directly increases what overseas-asset purchases are worth in home-currency terms). Frieden's own real-world illustration: the extensive 1981-86 U.S. political conflict over dollar policy, where trade-protectionist pressure from import-competing manufacturers was explicitly framed by many actors as a substitute for, or complement to, direct pressure to force a dollar depreciation — precisely the coalition structure this framework predicts. *(invested_interest)*
- **[[monetary_fiscal_mix_sectoral_coalition_politics]]** — a genuinely non-obvious application of [[monetary_fiscal_policy_effectiveness_by_regime]] already in this map (from Mundell 1963): since expansionary *monetary* policy depreciates the currency (favoring tradables producers) while expansionary *fiscal* policy appreciates it (favoring nontradables producers, especially where the spending itself falls on nontradables like defense, infrastructure, or social services — as government spending usually does), the observed *mix* of monetary and fiscal policy in any country reveals which sectoral coalition currently holds more political sway, not just what technocrats consider optimal. Frieden's own case study: the U.S. "loose fiscal + tight money → strong dollar" combination of the early Reagan years, widely criticized *at the time* by economists as an unfortunate policy mismatch, is reinterpreted here as the *outcome of a real distributional conflict* — nontradables-favoring constituencies (the administration's core support in defense, real estate, and the international-investor community) wanted the fiscal expansion and wanted it reinforced by tight money, while import-competing tradables producers (hurt by the resulting strong dollar and import surge) wanted monetary easing instead. A genuinely useful reframe: an apparently "inconsistent" macro policy mix may be a stable political equilibrium between competing sectoral interests, not a technical error waiting to be corrected. *(invested_interest)*
- **[[capital_mobility_reshapes_political_coalitions]]** — the paper's broader meta-argument, framing everything above: rising international capital mobility doesn't just constrain what policies are technically available (the standard [[impossible_trinity]] story) — it actively **reshapes which domestic political coalitions form** around macroeconomic policy. As capital becomes more mobile, the salient political fault line shifts away from older capital-vs-labor or left-vs-right divisions and toward a tradables-vs-nontradables axis and a diversified-vs-undiversified-investor axis, with the exchange rate itself becoming an increasingly important "intermediate" policy battleground — a country's exchange rate regime and level become proxies for a deeper distributional conflict, not merely a technocratic choice. Directly relevant lens for interpreting Brazil-specific political-economy material already narratively present in this map (Cortapasso et al.'s discussion of 2002-election and Dilma-era currency uncertainty) — this paper supplies the underlying theoretical mechanism that discussion gestures at without formalizing. *(invested_interest)*
- **[[emu_erm_political_economy_prediction]]** — a genuinely falsifiable, real-time application of the framework, written in 1991 as European Monetary Union was actively being negotiated: Frieden predicts that internationally-oriented finance, major exporters, and diversified multinationals should be the strongest supporters of European exchange-rate-mechanism (ERM) membership and eventual currency union, while domestically-oriented manufacturing and services should be comparatively lukewarm — and cites preliminary UK evidence (a British finance-sector lobbying group for rapid currency union whose corporate membership skews heavily toward banks, multinationals, and major exporters) consistent with the prediction. A useful historical marker for how well the sectoral-interests framework anticipated the actual political coalitions that formed around the euro project in the following decade, and a template for analyzing any country's own currency-union or dollarization debate (e.g., Central American dollarization, or any future Mercosur single-currency proposal) through the same lens. *(invested_interest)*
- **[[international_policy_coordination_sectoral_support]]** — extends the sectoral framework one step further, to the question of *international macroeconomic policy coordination* (joint exchange-rate targeting among major economies, of the kind proposed in the Williamson-Miller "target zones" literature): the same actors most exposed to cross-border financial conditions — international investors, traders, and export-oriented tradables producers — are predicted to be the most supportive of coordinating away national policy autonomy in exchange for reduced currency volatility, while the nontradables sector (which gains little from coordination but can be hurt by the resulting loss of domestic policy autonomy) is predicted to oppose it. A useful, generalizable template: any debate about ceding national monetary/fiscal autonomy to a supranational or coordinated framework can be mapped onto this same tradables/nontradables, diversified/undiversified sectoral split. *(invested_interest)*

### #applied_valuation_tools — estimating the "equilibrium" exchange rate

- **[[behavioral_equilibrium_exchange_rate]]** (BEER) — a family of methodologies (MacDonald & Clark, 1998; applied to Brazil by Paiva, 2006) that econometrically estimates the REER as a cointegrated function of "fundamentals," then defines "equilibrium" as whatever the model predicts given *actual* (not sustainable/normative) fundamental values. Explicitly contrasted with FEER-style approaches, which require fundamentals to be plugged in at *sustainable* levels (full employment, external sustainability) rather than actual ones — a distinction worth watching once [[gsdeer_gsfeer]] (Goldman Sachs models, not yet processed) enters the map, since FEER-family names suggest exactly that alternative branch. *(equilibrium_exchange_brazil)*
- **[[beer_fundamentals]]** — the canonical driver set: relative nontradable/tradable prices (→ [[balassa_samuelson_effect]]), terms of trade, real interest rate differential (→ [[uncovered_interest_rate_parity]]), net foreign asset position, and relative public debt (→ risk premium / country risk). *(equilibrium_exchange_brazil)*
- **[[cointegration_vec_methodology]]** — Johansen vector error-correction estimation used to separate the long-run "equilibrium" cointegrating relationship from short-run dynamics, with an adjustment-speed coefficient (α) indicating how fast the real exchange rate closes deviations from its estimated equilibrium. *(equilibrium_exchange_brazil)*
- **[[brazil_beer_2003_05_appreciation]]** — applied result: the real's ~25–40% real effective appreciation in 2003–05 was found to be largely an *equilibrium* phenomenon (models estimate 23–31% equilibrium appreciation over the same window), driven mostly by a stronger net foreign asset position (~60% of the move) and improved terms of trade (~40%), partly offset by Balassa-Samuelson-style productivity effects; declining public debt/risk premium contributed via a lower required risk compensation rather than through the interest-rate differential itself. Direct empirical counterpoint to any narrative that a large REER move is automatically a misalignment. *(equilibrium_exchange_brazil)*
- **[[gsdeer_model]]** — Goldman Sachs's Dynamic Equilibrium Exchange Rate model, the practitioner counterpart to academic BEER: essentially a PPP model *adjusted for terms of trade*, built for G10 crosses specifically because productivity differentials among G10 economies aren't large enough to dominate (unlike EM applications, where Balassa-Samuelson-style productivity catch-up — already flagged in [[balassa_samuelson_effect]] — would need to be more central). Three components decompose the fair-value estimate: **inflation** (relative price levels, i.e. straight PPP/law of one price), **terms of trade** (export/import price ratio, dominant for commodity exporters), and **productivity**. Which component actually *works* is cross-specific and empirically testable, not assumed — a methodological discipline worth importing into any in-house equilibrium-FX exercise. *(gsdeer_manual_guide)*
- **[[fair_value_convergence_vs_anchor_distinction]]** — a genuinely useful methodological split for judging *any* equilibrium exchange rate model, BEER or GSDEER: (1) does spot **converge** toward fair value in a predictable, forecastable way (useful for active positioning), vs. (2) does fair value act as a **firm anchor** that spot rarely strays far from, even if the path back isn't cleanly forecastable (useful for risk-management / tail-scenario framing)? These are different properties and currencies score differently on each — e.g. AUD diverges a lot from GSDEER fair value but converges (and overshoots) within ~4 years; CHF rarely diverges much, but when it does, mis-valuations can be very persistent. GBP and JPY score well on both. Practical translation: "how much is this currency mispriced" and "how much should I trust that mispricing to correct on my investment horizon" are two separate questions. *(gsdeer_manual_guide)*
- **[[gsdeer_horizon_dynamics]]** — empirical timing findings directly relevant to using *any* fair-value estimate as a trading signal: fair-value-based forecasts need roughly **3 years** to start beating a random walk; the valuation gap closes at a roughly linear **20%/year** rate, fully converging around **5 years** (consistent with academic real-exchange-rate half-life estimates of 3–5 years); beyond that, spot tends to **overshoot** fair value at the 8-10 year horizon before reverting again. Note this "long-horizon overshoot" is an empirically observed pattern in the data, distinct in mechanism from [[overshooting_model|Dornbusch's short-run monetary overshoot]] (which is driven by sticky prices and a specific policy shock) — the two concepts share a name and a "excess move beyond fair value" shape but are not the same phenomenon, and shouldn't be conflated. *(gsdeer_manual_guide)*
- **[[gsdeer_cross_country_heterogeneity]]** — the model's usefulness varies sharply and *predictably* by currency: convergence works best for AUD/EUR/CAD/NZD; "firmest anchor" (least likely to stray) for GBP/JPY/CHF; and the traditional model underperforms a random walk even at 5 years for the Scandi currencies. Component-level attribution explains why: the **inflation/PPP channel** works best for currencies with tight US linkages (EUR, GBP, CAD — law of one price most applicable); the **terms-of-trade channel** works for commodity exporters (CAD, AUD) but *fails specifically for NOK* — not because Norway lacks oil exposure, but because its sovereign wealth fund mechanism (investing oil revenue abroad rather than letting it flow directly into the domestic economy) deliberately severs the usual commodity-price → currency channel. A clean example of a country-specific institutional feature invalidating an otherwise-sound general model — worth checking for a BRL-specific equivalent (e.g., how much does Brazil's own fiscal/reserve-accumulation behavior alter which valuation channels are reliable here?). *(gsdeer_manual_guide)*
- **[[out_of_sample_forecast_evaluation]]** — a methodological caution applicable to evaluating *every* valuation model in this bibliography, not just GSDEER: a fair-value model tested **out-of-sample** (constructed only from data available *at the time* of each historical forecast) gives a genuine test of real-time usefulness; testing **in-sample** (fitting the whole history at once, then checking how well the fitted fair-value series lines up with realized spot) mechanically flatters the model, since both series share the same average by construction. Worth applying retroactively as a critical lens on [[behavioral_equilibrium_exchange_rate]] (Paiva, 2006), whose in-sample cointegration fit was not stress-tested this way in that paper. *(gsdeer_manual_guide)*
- **[[gsfeer_model]]** — the true FEER-family model this map's [[behavioral_equilibrium_exchange_rate]] entry (source 5) flagged as "worth watching for" — now confirmed. Where GSDEER is an augmented PPP model (slow-moving: inflation differential passes through 1-for-1 to fair value, terms of trade at 0.35, productivity at 0.28), **GSFEER is explicitly the macro-balance-approach cousin**: it links the currency to the gap between a country's *actual* current account and its theoretical "norm" — the current account level consistent with the country's long-run savings/investment fundamentals (the same S, I, and government balance building blocks as [[trade_balance_capital_account_identity]] and [[twin_deficits_identity]] elsewhere in this map) — plus the output gap and the currency's own recent path (to capture J-curve-style lags, see [[j_curve]]). A below-norm current account implies the currency needs to depreciate to help rebalance it. No universally accepted way to estimate the "norm," so — exactly like GSDEER's inflation-measure choice — the result is sensitive to modeling assumptions baked in upstream. *(primer_gsdeer_gsfeer)*
- **[[gsdeer_gsfeer_model_blending]]** — Goldman's own resolution of the [[fair_value_convergence_vs_anchor_distinction]] tension already in this map, now turned into an explicit weighting rule: a multivariate regression of realized real FX returns on both models' signals gives GSFEER real explanatory power at a 1-year horizon (≈30% weight, up from an earlier 40% in-sample estimate) but its relative weight *shrinks* at longer horizons (≈21% at 10 years) — consistent with GSFEER's current-account/output-gap inputs being inherently cyclical (a "tortoise vs. hare" framing: GSDEER slow-and-steady, GSFEER fast-reacting but noisier). Goldman's own 10-year FX forecasts therefore use GSDEER alone. GSFEER's distinct value-add: it can flag currency shifts driven by a genuine *structural* break (Brexit's effect on GBP; the EM aftermath of the 2013 taper tantrum) that a slow-moving PPP-based model like GSDEER will miss entirely until the break shows up in measured inflation/productivity data years later. *(primer_gsdeer_gsfeer)*
- **[[convergence_rate_heterogeneity_drivers]]** — three explicit reasons the ~20%/year convergence rule-of-thumb (already in this map via [[gsdeer_horizon_dynamics]] and independently cross-checked by [[ppp_deviation_half_life]]) varies so much by currency: (1) **input data quality** — no single "correct" FX-relevant inflation measure exists, and the choice materially changes both the fair-value level and its convergence speed; (2) **economic openness** — both the PPP channel (GSDEER) and the current-account-rebalancing channel (GSFEER) work better in more open, less complex-export-basket economies; (3) **managed/pegged regimes** — CNY, CZK, CHF, and PEN show near-zero convergence because the ordinary market forces that drive reversion to fair value are explicitly suppressed by policy. Point (3) is a direct, practitioner-side confirmation of this map's [[currency_regime_taxonomy]] — regime choice doesn't just affect volatility, it structurally breaks the mean-reversion mechanics that every valuation model in this cluster assumes. *(primer_gsdeer_gsfeer)*
- **[[brl_gsdeer_gsfeer_divergence]]** (dated view, Nov 2025) — a directly relevant, current house view: GSDEER and GSFEER give **directionally different** fair-value signals for BRL, and BRL's own historical convergence rate to either model has been low — Goldman's own conclusion is that "other factors like high carry or commodity terms of trade shifts" (i.e. [[carry_trade]] and the terms-of-trade channel already central to [[brazil_beer_2003_05_appreciation]]) dominate BRL price action more than either fair-value anchor. A useful, concrete caution against over-weighting any single equilibrium-exchange-rate estimate for BRL specifically — exactly the currency this bibliography is built to inform. *(primer_gsdeer_gsfeer)*

### #pass_through_and_inflation — how depreciation feeds into prices

- **[[exchange_rate_pass_through]]** — the coefficient linking accumulated depreciation to accumulated inflation over a given horizon; rises with the horizon and peaks around 12 months (in Goldfajn & Werlang's 71-country panel, roughly 0.01 at 1 month → 0.73 at 12 months), then flattens — evidence against an unbounded inflation-depreciation spiral. *(depreciation_pass_through)*
- **[[pass_through_determinants]]** — the two most robust drivers are (1) **RER misalignment** — a devaluation that merely corrects an existing overvaluation of the [[nominal_vs_real_exchange_rate|real exchange rate]] shows much less pass-through, since it's absorbed as a relative-price (tradable/non-tradable) adjustment rather than a generalized price shock — and (2) the **initial inflation level**, via persistence/inertia in price-setting. GDP-gap (business cycle) and trade openness matter but are less robust across samples/horizons. *(depreciation_pass_through)*
- **[[emerging_vs_developed_pass_through]]** — pass-through is much higher and more complete in emerging markets (~0.91 at 12 months) than in developed/OECD economies (~0.60), and the mechanism differs: RER overvaluation dominates in emerging markets, initial inflation dominates in developed ones (esp. Europe). *(depreciation_pass_through)*
- **[[exchange_rate_as_nominal_anchor]]** — the reason large depreciations trigger inflation fears in the first place: many crisis countries had used a fixed/managed exchange rate as their disinflation anchor, so a large devaluation raises fear that the "spiral" this anchor suppressed will return. *(depreciation_pass_through)*
- Forecast bias note: the model *systematically over-predicts* inflation after real-world currency crises (Mexico '94, Asia '97, Brazil '99, ERM '92) — the paper flags **exchange rate overshooting-and-reversion** as one candidate explanation (a depreciation that overshoots its long-run value need not fully pass through if part of it reverses). Direct forward link to [[overshooting_model]] — see [[expectations_and_exchange_rate_dynamics]] (Dornbusch, 1976), not yet processed at time of writing.
- **[[distribution_chain_pass_through]]** — pass-through is not one number: it differs sharply by where a price sits in the distribution chain. In Brazil (Belaisch, 2003, VAR on 1999–2002 data), wholesale prices (IPA, tradables-heavy) pass through almost fully within a year (~100–120%), while consumer prices (IPCA) pass through only ~17% at 12 months and administered/regulated prices barely react at all; nontradables react slowly but persistently (still adjusting at 12 months). Confirms and refines Goldfajn & Werlang's aggregate coefficient by showing *where in the chain* the absorption happens. *(depreciation_passtrought — Belaisch, 2003)*
- **[[incomplete_pass_through_explanations]]** — mechanisms proposed in the literature for why depreciation doesn't fully show up in consumer prices: (i) distribution costs make the effective non-tradable share of retail prices larger than headline weights suggest (Burstein et al.); (ii) local-currency price stickiness/menu costs (Betts & Devereux); (iii) "flight from quality" — consumers substitute toward cheaper local goods as imports get pricier (Burstein et al.); (iv) pass-through is procyclical — firms compress margins instead of raising prices during downturns (Leiderman & Bar-Or; Goldfajn & Werlang). *(depreciation_passtrought)*
- **[[marshall_lerner_condition]]** — the precise elasticity condition Krugman derives for the assumption running through this entire cluster ("depreciation improves the current account") to actually hold: η + η* > 1, where η and η* are the price-elasticities of export and import demand with respect to the real exchange rate (starting from balanced trade). If combined export+import demand isn't elastic enough, a depreciation can *worsen* the trade balance instead of improving it — the "volume effect" (more units sold) has to outweigh the "value effect" (each existing unit of imports costs more in domestic-currency terms). IMF elasticity estimates (Artus & Knight, 1984) across 14 industrial countries show impact elasticities (first 6 months) are almost always too low to satisfy Marshall-Lerner, short-run (1-year) elasticities satisfy it for most countries, and long-run elasticities satisfy it for virtually all — i.e., the condition typically fails at short horizons and holds at long ones. *(output_exchange_rates_sr)*
- **[[j_curve]]** — the time-path this elasticity pattern implies: a real depreciation *worsens* the current account for roughly 6–12 months (import/export volumes are locked in by pre-existing contracts, so the value effect dominates) before *improving* it as volumes adjust — tracing a "J" shape. Directly explains and generalizes the specific 12-month pass-through/current-account lags already documented in [[distribution_chain_pass_through]] (Belaisch, 2003) and the pass-through horizon effects in [[exchange_rate_pass_through]] (Goldfajn & Werlang, 2000) — this is the trade-volume-and-value-accounting mechanism underlying those empirical pass-through curves, not a separate phenomenon. **Feedback loop worth flagging:** a significant J-curve effect *amplifies* [[overshooting_model|exchange rate overshooting]] — if a depreciation doesn't even improve the current account for the first year, the currency must overshoot further to satisfy short-run asset-market equilibrium, since the usual demand-boosting channel is temporarily working in reverse. *(output_exchange_rates_sr)*
- **[[pricing_to_market]]** — Krugman's name for a specific incomplete-pass-through mechanism, complementing the list already under [[incomplete_pass_through_explanations]]: an imperfectly-competitive foreign exporter facing a segmented market may deliberately hold its destination-market price steady after a currency move (absorbing the hit to its own-currency revenue) to protect market share, rather than repricing immediately — only adjusting once it's confident the currency move is a trend rather than noise. Empirically, US import prices historically rose by only about half of a given dollar depreciation over the following year. *(output_exchange_rates_sr)*
- **[[structural_break_in_pass_through]]** — Belaisch finds (Chow test, one-step-ahead recursive residuals) tentative evidence that Brazil's pass-through rose starting ~Sep 2002, coinciding with the market's shift toward viewing the depreciation as permanent rather than temporary — i.e., the pass-through coefficient itself is regime-dependent on **expectations of persistence**, not a fixed structural parameter. Also documents that Brazil's own pass-through had *fallen* markedly vs. earlier local estimates (Schwartz & Rabanal 2001; Kfoury Muinhos 2001) and sits below the Latin America average but close to G-7. *(depreciation_passtrought)*

- **[[asymmetric_pass_through_fx_trend]]** (repasse cambial assimétrico) — the paper's central, somewhat counter-intuitive finding for Brazil, 2002–2021 (VAR, structural break confirmed at July 2011 via Bai-Perron test): exchange rate pass-through to IPCA is **stronger during BRL appreciation trends (2002–2011) than during depreciation trends (2011–2021)** — the opposite of the "firms opportunistically raise prices when costs rise from a weaker currency" intuition. The authors' explanation is demand-side, not cost-side: in Brazil's specific historical pattern, depreciation episodes have coincided with crises and weak aggregate demand (falling installed-capacity utilization, NUCI), making firms reluctant/unable to pass rising import costs into prices in an already-contracting market, whereas appreciation episodes coincided with robust demand that both supported margins and let the (favorable) currency move flow through cleanly. This adds a **state-dependency dimension** — conditional on the broader demand cycle, not just on whether the market believes the move is permanent (Belaisch, 2003) — to [[structural_break_in_pass_through]] and [[incomplete_pass_through_explanations]] already in this map. Practical implication: naively applying an average estimated pass-through coefficient across a full sample can badly mis-predict inflation depending on which phase of the currency cycle Brazil is actually in. *(padrao_trasmissao_cambial)*
- **[[price_puzzle_cost_channel]]** — a documented VAR anomaly directly relevant to interpreting any Brazilian (or other EM) monetary-transmission impulse-response study: a Selic (policy rate) hike sometimes shows up as *raising* inflation in the near term rather than lowering it — the "price puzzle." One explanation is a **cost channel** of monetary policy: higher interest rates raise firms' financing costs *directly and quickly* (a supply-side cost shock), while the demand-dampening effect of tighter policy takes longer to show up — so in the short window before demand cools, costs rise faster than demand falls, and measured inflation ticks up. Distinct from, and additional to, the exchange-rate transmission channel that is this map's main pass-through focus — a caution against reading any single-lag VAR coefficient on the policy rate as "the" effect of monetary policy on prices. *(padrao_trasmissao_cambial)*
- **[[brazil_juros_altos_cambio_apreciado_regime]]** ("high rates, appreciated currency" regime, 2002–2011) — the specific historical Brazilian policy combination this paper documents and critiques: under inflation targeting, Selic stayed structurally high without a proportional decline in the country-risk premium, creating persistent arbitrage room ([[carry_trade]]-like capital inflows) that appreciated the real and, per [[asymmetric_pass_through_fx_trend]] above, made the exchange-rate channel of disinflation unusually *effective* during this window — cheaper imports, cheaper administered prices (energy, telecom, health plans, which reset via the IGP-M index that is itself dollar-sensitive). The authors' critique (echoing Serrano, 2010, and Bresser-Pereira, 2007, both cited): this created a policy architecture **structurally dependent on the exchange rate channel** for inflation control, with real costs — a stronger currency discourages higher-value-added domestic industry and raises import penetration, eroding competitiveness — that standard IT-framework accounting doesn't price in. Directly extends the case-study set already in this map (source 1's Malaysia capital controls, source 10's Swiss franc defense, source 8's Iceland crisis) with a "successful-looking policy that has under-priced costs" example rather than an outright crisis. *(padrao_trasmissao_cambial)*

### #capital_controls — restrictions on capital flows

- **[[capital_controls]]** — government objectives (strategic industries, crisis management, monetary autonomy); mechanisms (price controls/taxes, quantity limits, administrative approval); IMF finding that outflow controls give only temporary, mixed relief; case study: Malaysia's 1998–2001 capital controls (fixed the ringgit, banned repatriation, later replaced by exit taxes) — stabilized the economy short-term but caused Malaysia's removal from the MSCI developed-market index and lasting damage to foreign investor access. *(capital_flow_fx_market)* **More examples (CFA L2):** Brazil's 2016 FX transaction tax; Thailand's 2006 unremunerated reserve requirement (30% of inflows) to slow baht appreciation; Vietnam's foreign-ownership caps on banks; Venezuela's multi-tier exchange rate system (2016) that pushed activity into a black market — illustrating that controls are porous almost by design and their side effects (index exclusion, black markets, distorted allocation) often outlast the crisis they were meant to manage. *(exchange_rate_understanding_equilibrium)*
- **[[fx_intervention_effectiveness_evidence]]** — direct central bank FX intervention works very differently by country type. **Developed markets:** central bank reserves are tiny relative to daily G10 FX turnover, so intervention to *strengthen* a currency has limited firepower — though intervention to *weaken* a currency is in principle unlimited (a central bank can print unlimited domestic currency to sell), constrained instead by side effects on domestic monetary policy. **Emerging markets:** reserves are large relative to (comparatively thin) domestic-currency turnover, so intervention has historically had more traction — it reduces volatility reliably, though the evidence on moving the *level* of the exchange rate is more mixed. This asymmetry is a useful prior when assessing whether a central bank's stated FX policy is likely to be effective. *(exchange_rate_understanding_equilibrium)*

### #capital_controls (continued) — the IMF's own "institutional view" precursor (Ostry et al., 2010)

- **[[capital_inflow_response_toolkit]]** — the paper's central organizing device (a policy decision sequence, their Figure 1), and a genuinely useful checklist for evaluating any real-world EM policy response to an inflow surge: (1) **let the currency appreciate**, if it's undervalued from a multilateral perspective — the clean first-best response; (2) if the currency is already fairly valued/overvalued and reserves are inadequate, **accumulate reserves**; (3) if inflation concerns arise from the resulting money-supply expansion, **sterilize** — but sterilization has real limits (shallow domestic bond markets can't always absorb the sterilization bonds; there's a fiscal quasi-cost from the domestic-bond-yield/reserve-yield spread; and persistently high sterilized rates can perversely *keep attracting* the same inflows they're meant to offset); (4) once sterilization capacity is exhausted, turn to **monetary/fiscal policy** (lower rates, tighten fiscal) — though this is often infeasible if the economy is already overheating or fiscal consolidation faces political/implementation lags; (5) only after exhausting 1-4, consider **capital controls**. This sequencing is the direct institutional-view precursor to the more formal 2012 IMF framework, and gives concrete texture to the more abstract policy-tradeoff language already in this map's [[monetary_fiscal_policy_mix_capital_mobility]] and [[sterilized_intervention]] entries. *(capital_inflow)*
- **[[capital_controls_justification_conditions]]** — the paper's central substantive conclusion, stated as a conjunction of four specific conditions under which controls on capital *inflows* are justified as *part of* (not a substitute for) the standard toolkit: the economy is operating near potential output, reserves are already adequate, the currency is not undervalued (so appreciation isn't the multilaterally preferred outcome anyway), and the inflow surge is likely to be transitory rather than a permanent shift. This is a considerably more conditional and narrower endorsement of capital controls than the phrase "capital controls are legitimate" might suggest in isolation — a precise counterpoint worth keeping in mind alongside this map's existing, more case-study-driven [[capital_controls]] entry. *(capital_inflow)*
- **[[sand_in_the_wheels_circumvention_tolerance]]** — a subtle but important point about *why* imperfectly-enforced capital controls can still work: controls "can retain potency even if investors devise strategies to bypass them, provided such strategies are more costly than the expected return from the transaction" — i.e. circumvention costs act as a friction ("sand in the wheels," in the Tobin-tax lineage) that discourages the marginal transaction even when it doesn't literally stop the determined investor. This reframes the common objection "but capital controls are always circumvented eventually" as a matter of degree (how costly is circumvention relative to the expected trade profit) rather than a binary pass/fail test of control effectiveness. *(capital_inflow)*
- **[[pecking_order_of_capital_inflows_riskiness]]** — a clean ranking, in decreasing order of financial-fragility risk, that the paper derives from the theoretical literature and its own empirics: **foreign-currency debt** (riskiest) > **CPI-indexed local-currency debt** > **local-currency debt** > **portfolio equity** > **FDI** (safest), with short-term instruments riskier than long-term ones within each category. The genuinely surprising empirical wrinkle, directly relevant to any headline "FDI inflows are the safe/stable kind" assumption: some flows *recorded* as "financial-sector FDI" actually disguise intragroup parent-to-subsidiary lending, which behaves like debt (and is just as prone to flight) despite the FDI label — a real measurement trap the paper flags explicitly using cross-country crisis data from 2008-09. Directly extends [[capital_flow_push_pull_factors]] and [[capital_flow_surge_reversal]] already in this map with a more granular, instrument-level risk taxonomy rather than a binary "flows reverse" story. *(capital_inflow)*
- **[[capital_controls_effectiveness_stylized_facts]]** — the paper's own honest empirical verdict, more nuanced than either capital-controls advocates or skeptics typically present it: existing country studies (Brazil, Chile, Colombia, Thailand, Malaysia — largely the same case-study roster already in this map's [[capital_controls]] entry) find **little robust evidence that controls reduce the aggregate volume of inflows or meaningfully dampen currency appreciation** — Chile's and Brazil's currencies kept appreciating steadily throughout their 1990s control regimes. Where the evidence is much stronger is on **composition**: URR-type measures in Chile and Colombia demonstrably lengthened the maturity structure of external liabilities (Chile's short-term debt share fell as FDI's share rose from 34% to 53% of foreign liabilities, 1991-98). Using the 2008-09 global financial crisis as a natural experiment, the paper's own regressions find that pre-crisis controls specifically targeting **debt inflows** (not FDI or equity) are significantly associated with smaller crisis-era output declines — a maturity/composition effect on crisis resilience, not a volume effect on the boom itself. Together this is a genuinely more calibrated empirical picture than a simple "capital controls work" or "capital controls don't work" headline would suggest — they're a composition tool and a resilience tool, not a currency-appreciation-prevention tool. *(capital_inflow)*
- **[[multilateral_spillover_caveat_capital_controls]]** — the paper's crucial qualifying counterweight to its own justification-conditions argument above, and the seed of what became the IMF's fuller 2012 institutional framework: a country's unilateral decision to impose capital controls is not costless to the rest of the system. Widespread adoption — especially by countries whose currencies are actually *undervalued* — could exacerbate global current-account imbalances by blocking the very appreciation needed for global demand rebalancing; controls can also be "contagious" (a beggar-thy-neighbor dynamic, where one country's controls redirect flows toward others who then face pressure to adopt their own); and broad-based adoption risks a longer-term chilling effect on financial integration, with real output and welfare costs. The paper's own resolution is characteristically balanced rather than either extreme: national capital-control decisions should be made with explicit awareness of these multilateral externalities, ideally coordinated through a multilateral framework — a genuinely different register from the case-study-level "did this specific country's controls work" question this map's other capital-controls material focuses on. *(capital_inflow)*

### #capital_controls (continued) — the IMF's own "institutional view" (2012 Board paper)

- **[[cfm_mpm_terminology_and_overlap]]** — the paper's core terminological contribution, and the one that stuck as the IMF's standing official vocabulary: **Capital Flow Management Measures (CFMs)** are measures (price-based or administrative) *specifically designed to limit capital flows*, as distinct from **Macro-Prudential Measures (MPMs)**, which are prudential tools designed to limit systemic financial risk regardless of whether that risk originates cross-border or domestically. A tax on cross-border inflows is a CFM; a countercyclical capital buffer on banks is an MPM; but a reserve requirement on banks' *foreign-currency* liabilities specifically is *both* at once, whenever capital inflows themselves are the proximate driver of a domestic credit/asset-price boom — the two categories are not mutually exclusive, they're defined by different questions (what is the measure targeting: the flow itself, or the systemic risk, regardless of source?). This is a genuinely more precise vocabulary than this map's pre-existing, more casual usage of "capital controls" (via [[capital_controls]]) — worth using this CFM/MPM distinction explicitly when discussing any specific policy measure going forward. *(the_liberalization_management_capital_flows)*
- **[[integrated_approach_liberalization_sequencing]]** — the IMF's own recommended order for *removing* long-standing capital controls (as opposed to the separate question, covered next, of when to *impose* new ones): loosely summarized in the paper's own words as "long term before short term, FDI (and non-debt) before debt, and inflows before outflows" — a three-phase sequence (FDI inflows first, since they're most stable and growth-correlated; then FDI outflows and long-term portfolio flows; short-term portfolio flows last) that should track, not outrun, a country's underlying financial/institutional development ("preconditions" — macroeconomic stability, financial market depth, institutional quality, trade openness). Explicitly rejects a one-size-fits-all liberalization mandate: **"there is no presumption that full liberalization is an appropriate goal for all countries at all times."** This is the practical machinery behind the Ostry-et-al.-2010 justification conditions already in this map ([[capital_controls_justification_conditions]]) — that paper asked *when controls are justified*; this one lays out the *sequencing logic* for the mirror-image process of dismantling them safely. *(the_liberalization_management_capital_flows)*
- **[[cfm_appropriateness_conditions_2012]]** — the fuller, more operational successor to the four conditions already in this map from Ostry et al. (2010): CFMs on new inflow surges may be useful specifically when (1) **macro policy room is genuinely limited** — the economy is already overheating or showing asset-bubble signs, the currency is not undervalued, and reserves are already adequate, so appreciation/reserve-accumulation/rate cuts are all unattractive; (2) **needed policy adjustments take time to design or take effect** (fiscal changes face approval lags; monetary transmission may be slow if inflation expectations are sticky) — CFMs buy time rather than substitute for the eventual adjustment; or (3) **the inflow surge itself threatens systemic financial stability**, in which case CFM and MPM tools converge (see [[cfm_mpm_terminology_and_overlap]]). Explicitly **not** appropriate: using CFMs to gain "unfair competitive advantage" via exchange-rate manipulation (potentially inconsistent with IMF Article IV obligations), or as a standing substitute for warranted macro adjustment. When used, CFMs should be **transparent, targeted, temporary, and (where possible) non-discriminatory between residents and non-residents** — a considerably more detailed design checklist than the "sand in the wheels" tolerance-for-circumvention point already in this map from the 2010 precursor. *(the_liberalization_management_capital_flows)*
- **[[source_country_spillover_responsibility]]** — a genuinely symmetric extension of the push/pull framing already in this map (via [[capital_flow_push_pull_factors]] from the CFA L2 source, which frames push/pull purely from the *recipient* country's perspective): this paper argues explicitly that policymakers in **capital-flow-*generating*** countries — chiefly the advanced economies whose monetary policy and financial-sector regulation are the dominant "push" factor — should themselves internalize the multilateral spillovers of their own policy choices, not just leave the adjustment burden to recipient EMs. Concretely ties post-GFC unconventional monetary policy in the G4 (and the resulting expansion in "global liquidity," a measured aggregate of G4 financial-institution liabilities) to the volume and riskiness of capital flows reaching emerging markets — a structural argument for why capital-flow management is inherently a two-sided, multilaterally-coordinated policy problem, not solely a recipient-country toolkit question. *(the_liberalization_management_capital_flows)*
- **[[institutional_view_as_policy_compromise]]** — worth noting as a meta-point about what kind of document this is: explicitly framed (paragraph 5) as an attempt to find "common ground" between two entrenched camps — those who view free capital mobility as an unambiguous good to be restricted only in exceptional circumstances, and those more skeptical of capital account liberalization who see flow-limiting measures as having genuine growth/stability benefits. Unlike the academic papers elsewhere in this map's `#currency_crisis_dynamics` and `#capital_controls` clusters, this is the IMF's own official Board-approved doctrine as of Nov. 2012 — the document that governs how Fund staff actually give country-level policy advice and conduct Article IV surveillance on capital-account matters, not a third-party research contribution to the academic debate. Useful to keep this institutional-authority distinction in mind when citing it versus citing, e.g., Ostry et al. (2010) or Chamon & Garcia's Brazil-specific empirical work (still an open gap — see [[exchange_rate_bibliography_gaps]]). *(the_liberalization_management_capital_flows)*

### #currency_crisis_dynamics — how currency crises build and break

- **[[capital_flow_surge_reversal]]** — the boom-bust pattern behind most EM currency crises: a capital inflow surge (often push-factor-driven) fuels currency overvaluation, external debt buildup, asset bubbles, and a domestic credit/consumption boom — then reverses "suddenly and often without adequate warning" (CFA's own phrasing), triggering downturn, default risk, banking crisis, and sharp depreciation. The reversal is amplified mechanically by leveraged positions unwinding simultaneously: carry traders closing positions, and foreign-currency borrowers scrambling to buy hard currency to cover debt, both selling the crisis currency at once. Named historical instances: ERM crisis (1992-93), Mexican peso crisis (1994), Asian financial crisis (1997-98) — each preceded by exactly this leveraged-inflow buildup. *(exchange_rate_understanding_equilibrium)*
- **[[currency_crisis_warning_signs]]** — nine empirically recurring pre-crisis conditions (Babecký et al. 2013/2014; Daniels & VanHoose 2018), useful as a checklist rather than a predictive model (crises are usually *not* well-anticipated by markets, rating agencies, or economists even with these signs present): (1) recent capital account liberalization, (2) large capital inflows relative to GDP, especially short-term FX-denominated funding, (3) concurrent/preceding banking-sector stress ("twin crisis" pattern), (4) fixed/managed exchange rate regime (more vulnerable than floating), (5) sharply declining FX reserves, (6) currency substantially above its historical mean, (7) deteriorating terms of trade, (8) rising money growth / M2-to-reserves ratio, (9) elevated inflation vs. tranquil-period norms. An explicit debate exists between the "fundamentals deteriorate first" school and the "crises can strike sound economies via pure sentiment shift/contagion" school — the map should not treat this checklist as sufficient on its own. *(exchange_rate_understanding_equilibrium)*
- **[[balance_of_payments_crisis_mechanics]]** — the formal machinery behind a currency crisis: the moment the market believes a peg will eventually be devalued, UIP forces domestic interest rates up *immediately* (to compensate for the now-expected future depreciation while the peg is still nominally in place), and this rate rise requires the central bank to sell reserves to keep the peg at its current level in the meantime — reserves fall and rates rise *before* the actual devaluation happens. This is the mechanism behind "capital flight": residents dump domestic currency for foreign currency at the central bank, and foreigners repatriate; the outflow itself drains the reserves that were the peg's last line of defense, potentially forcing a *sooner and larger* devaluation than fundamentals alone would have required. Krugman draws a sharp distinction between two crisis archetypes: (1) **fundamentals-driven/inevitable** — e.g. a government financing persistent deficits via central-bank bond purchases is *mechanically* burning reserves every period regardless of speculation; the collapse is coming regardless, speculative attack just front-loads the timing; (2) **self-fulfilling** — an economy not fundamentally doomed can still be forced into crisis if speculators' *belief* in a coming devaluation triggers real economic damage (e.g. banks funded by short-term deposits, holding risky long-term loans: a rate spike to defend the peg raises bank funding costs and triggers a recession that impairs loan quality at the same time, forcing the central bank to bail out banks by printing money — burning the very reserves needed to defend the peg). This formalizes and sharpens the "twin banking/currency crisis" pattern already flagged in [[currency_crisis_warning_signs]] above. *(fixed_exchange_rate_intervention)*
- **[[swiss_franc_2011_2015_defense]]** — a valuable counter-example to every other crisis case study in this map: a **strong**-currency defense rather than a weak one. Investors fled to the CHF as a safe haven during the 2008 GFC and 2010s eurozone crisis; the SNB responded by buying EUR (and thus, mechanically, printing CHF) to slow appreciation, then in Sept. 2011 explicitly pledged an unlimited floor of CHF 1.20/EUR. Because a central bank defending against *appreciation* is selling its own currency — which it can create without limit — this defense faced no reserve constraint (unlike every weak-currency defense in this map), only an inflation constraint that never bound (Switzerland stayed in deflation throughout, dragged down by the eurozone's weakness). SNB reserves swelled to roughly 75% of Swiss GDP. The floor was abandoned abruptly in Jan. 2015 — not from running out of ammunition, but from the SNB's own risk management: anticipated ECB QE threatened to force ever-larger EUR purchases, compounding the SNB's mark-to-market currency-translation losses on its swollen EUR reserves if the floor were ever abandoned later at a worse rate. Real-economy spillover: Polish households with CHF-denominated mortgages (a retail-level, unintentional [[carry_trade]]) saw payments jump 15-20% overnight. A clean illustration that "unlimited firepower" and "sustainable policy" are not the same thing even for a central bank that can print without limit. *(fixed_exchange_rate_intervention)*
- **[[iceland_2008_currency_crisis]]** — the canonical small-open-economy case study combining nearly every warning sign at once: post-2001 banking liberalization → three banks (Glitnir, Kaupthing, Landsbanki) funded via foreign-currency retail deposits and short-term wholesale borrowing → bank assets reached 14× GDP, foreign debt 5× GDP → real growth >20%/yr, krona +40% vs. USD (2001-07), unemployment <1% → global banking freeze in 2008 cut off rollover funding → krona fell 40% vs. EUR in H1 2008 → peg attempt abandoned after one day → banks collapsed, central bank technically insolvent (EUR 2bn assets vs. EUR 50bn foreign liabilities) → capital controls + 18% policy rate + IMF bailout (USD 4.6bn). A complete illustration of the maturity-mismatch/currency-mismatch mechanism that also appears (in less extreme form) across most crisis case studies in this bibliography. *(exchange_rate_understanding_equilibrium)*

### #currency_crisis_dynamics (continued) — the original first-generation model (Krugman, 1979)

- **[[krugman_1979_first_generation_model]]** — the founding academic source behind the textbook mechanics already in this map under [[balance_of_payments_crisis_mechanics]] (Krugman's own later Ch. 18 treatment). Built on a Kouri (1976) one-good, two-asset portfolio model: a small country under PPP (P = sP*), fixed full-employment output, and a government whose deficit is financed *entirely* by money creation. Two asset regimes are compared — under **floating rates**, shifts in expected inflation move the price level instantly (no reserve constraint); under a **fixed rate**, the same shifts are absorbed by the government's reserves instead, since domestic residents can freely swap currencies at the fixed parity. Because the deficit-financed money growth continuously pushes private wealth into foreign-currency holdings, reserves decline mechanically over time even with zero speculation — the "problem" phase — until a threshold is crossed and a "crisis" (discrete, total loss of remaining reserves) forces the regime to float. *(balance_of_payment_crisis)*
- **[[speculative_attack_timing_condition]]** — the paper's central formal result, and the origin of what the later literature calls the "shadow exchange rate" approach even though Krugman never uses that phrase here: if speculators did *not* attack, the exchange rate would jump discretely upward (a windfall capital loss for anyone still holding domestic money) at the exact instant reserves hit zero, because post-float portfolio equilibrium requires a different (higher) price level than the pegged one. Rational investors won't accept a foreseeable loss, so they instead attack *before* exhaustion, at the unique moment when private wealth W and remaining reserves R first satisfy P'/P = 1 (equation 20) — i.e., the moment at which the post-attack floating price level would exactly equal the still-pegged price level, so the transition involves no capital gain or loss for anyone. This is why every first-generation crisis "always comes before the government would have run out of reserves in the absence of speculation" — a sharp, falsifiable, and non-obvious prediction that distinguishes this class of model from a naive "run until the money's gone" story. The threshold is upward-sloping in (W, R) space: more initial reserves buys strictly more time before the attack, all else equal. *(balance_of_payment_crisis)*
- **[[one_way_option_uncertain_reserves]]** — Krugman's extension to the case where the market is unsure how much of a government's *total* reserves it is actually willing to commit to defending the peg (split conceptually into a certain "primary" reserve R₁ and an uncertain "secondary" reserve R₂, used with probability α < 1). Because a speculative attack is costless to reverse (investors just hold slightly more foreign currency for an instant) but a missed attack risks a windfall loss, rational speculators attack **as if the secondary reserve did not exist** — exhausting R₁ exactly per the standard timing condition — and only reverse the attack (selling foreign currency back) if the government then visibly commits R₂. This produces the empirically familiar pattern of **alternating speculative attacks and "recoveries of confidence"**: each newly-revealed layer of defensible reserves triggers one more attack-then-reversal cycle before the peg is finally abandoned for good. Directly explains, from first principles, why real-world currency crises so often look like a sequence of near-misses rather than one clean break. *(balance_of_payment_crisis)*
- **[[reserve_loss_trade_balance_decoupling]]** — a technical but practically relevant result: the rate of reserve loss under a fixed peg is *not* generally equal to the trade/current-account balance B, even though both eventually reflect the same underlying deficit-financing problem. Because part of any change in private wealth gets allocated to domestic money (not just foreign reserves) via the portfolio-balance condition, reserve depletion (R-dot = L·B − (1−L)(G−T)) can run faster or slower than the trade deficit itself — a caution against reading a country's headline trade/current-account numbers as a direct, one-for-one proxy for reserve-adequacy pressure. *(balance_of_payment_crisis)*
- **[[shadow_floating_exchange_rate]]** — Flood & Garber's coinage (this paper is where the term itself originates, even though it formalizes Krugman 1979's logic): the exchange rate that *would* prevail at time t if the government were to abandon the peg and let the currency float at that exact instant, computed purely from current fundamentals (domestic credit D(t), which grows deterministically at rate μ) as if reserves had just gone to zero. The core technique that makes the whole first-generation crisis literature tractable: rather than solving simultaneously for "when does the attack happen" and "what does the post-attack price level look like," compute the shadow rate as a function of time first, then find the *unique* moment its path crosses the still-pegged rate S̄ — that crossing point *is* the attack time (by the same no-discrete-jump/no-arbitrage argument as [[speculative_attack_timing_condition]] in the Krugman 1979 entry above, now made fully explicit and linear so the collapse time has a closed-form solution: z = [R(0)/μ − α/β] in the base case). A cleaner, linearized restatement of the same mechanism this map already has from the non-linear original — genuinely useful for actually computing (not just describing) when a peg of known reserves and known credit-growth rate should be expected to break. *(collapsing_exchange_rate_regimes)*
- **[[arbitrary_speculative_bubbles_under_fixed_rates]]** — a pointed rebuttal to a "traditional argument favoring fixed exchange rates" that this map hasn't previously encountered stated so explicitly: the belief that pegging protects the real economy from the kind of arbitrary speculative bubbles that can afflict a floating currency. Flood & Garber show the opposite — the shadow-rate framework admits an arbitrary constant (their "A") capturing exactly the same self-fulfilling-bubble indeterminacy that can appear under floating rates; a large enough bubble term can trigger an *earlier* collapse than fundamentals alone would require, with the post-collapse floating path itself following a bubble path. Punchline: "arbitrary speculative behavior, if present, is an economic force which is masked, not purged, by the fixing of exchange rates" — fixing the rate doesn't eliminate speculative bubble risk, it just relocates *when* that risk manifests (as a sudden regime collapse rather than continuous rate volatility). A genuine bridge toward the "self-fulfilling" logic that [[rational_self_fulfilling_bpc]] (Obstfeld) will formalize as second-generation crisis theory. *(collapsing_exchange_rate_regimes)*
- **[[stochastic_collapse_forward_discount]]** — the paper's discrete-time extension, and its most empirically important result: under *perfect foresight* (the baseline model, and Krugman 1979's setup), a fixed exchange rate that's about to collapse shows **no forward discount at all** right up until the instant of collapse — a counterfactual prediction, since real-world weak pegs routinely trade at a persistent forward discount for extended periods beforehand. Once domestic credit growth is made stochastic (agents know the *distribution* of shocks but not their realization), a collapse becomes a probabilistic event each period, and the forward rate must reflect a probability-weighted average of "peg survives" and "peg collapses" outcomes — generating exactly the sustained forward discount/premium seen in the data, rising as domestic credit approaches the collapse threshold D*. This is a first-generation-model, closed-form derivation of the same **[[peso_problem]]** phenomenon this map already has from Krugman's textbook carry-trade discussion — genuinely satisfying to have both the applied/empirical version (carry trade returns) and the formal collapse-probability derivation (this paper) side by side, and worth flagging explicitly as the same underlying logic applied to two different assets (interest-rate carry vs. peg-collapse timing). *(collapsing_exchange_rate_regimes)*
- **[[offset_coefficient_exceeding_unity]]** — a technical curiosity worth noting for anyone building a similar model empirically: naively, sterilized/unsterilized domestic credit expansion under a fixed rate should produce an "offset coefficient" of exactly 1 (every unit of domestic credit created is matched by a unit of reserve loss, per the mechanical logic already in this map under [[central_bank_balance_sheet_mechanics]]). Once the stochastic forward-discount channel above is added, the offset coefficient **exceeds** unity: faster domestic credit growth raises the forward discount, which raises domestic interest rates (via UIP), which further *reduces* domestic money demand — triggering additional reserve loss beyond the direct one-for-one mechanical effect. A concrete illustration that peso-problem-style forward-premium dynamics aren't just a pricing curiosity but feed back into the real pace of reserve depletion itself. *(collapsing_exchange_rate_regimes)*

### #currency_crisis_dynamics (continued) — the original second-generation model (Obstfeld, 1984)

- **[[rational_vs_self_fulfilling_crisis_distinction]]** — the paper's foundational theoretical contribution, and the formal source of the "two crisis archetypes" dichotomy this map already carries (in simplified textbook form) under [[balance_of_payments_crisis_mechanics]]. Obstfeld proves both cases can arise from essentially the *same* linear model (Flood & Garber's), depending purely on what the public expects the central bank to do *after* a crisis: (1) **rational/fundamentals-driven** (Krugman 1979, Flood & Garber 1984) — if agents expect the domestic-credit process to continue unchanged regardless of regime, and the peg is either unconditionally viable (stationary AR(1) credit, bounded shocks) or unconditionally doomed (credit growing at a constant positive drift μ), the collapse is a probability-zero *or* probability-one event with a determinate (or Flood-Garber-style probabilistic) timing — never "arbitrary." (2) **self-fulfilling** (this paper's real contribution — see below) — if agents instead expect a crisis to *trigger a discrete policy regime change* (a shift to inflationary credit growth), the model admits a continuum of self-consistent equilibria, and even a fundamentals-viable peg can collapse for no reason but the market's belief that it will. *(rational_self_fulfilling_bpc)*
- **[[self_fulfilling_multiple_equilibria_crisis]]** — the core formal result: assume the public believes that *if* a run occurs, the central bank will respond by abandoning the peg and switching to an inflationary domestic-credit rule (rather than reverting to its prior stationary process). This expectation alone is enough to make a run **self-validating**: conditional on a run happening, the post-collapse floating rate genuinely does depreciate (because credit really will grow faster going forward), so no individual investor loses by joining an anticipated attack — even though, absent that expectation, the peg was fully viable forever. Formally, the economy admits a continuum of equilibria indexed by π ∈ [0, π̄), the *subjective probability* the market assigns to a crisis occurring — each value of π is self-consistent (rational expectations are satisfied), and nothing in economic fundamentals pins down which π actually obtains. This is the original, quantitative, general form of the vague "self-fulfilling" language already used narratively in this map's case studies (e.g. the Krugman-Ch.18-derived "twin banking/currency crisis" story under [[balance_of_payments_crisis_mechanics]]) — here it is a genuine indeterminacy result, not just a description of a vicious feedback loop. *(rational_self_fulfilling_bpc)*
- **[[crisis_risk_premium_before_collapse]]** — a sharp, testable implication of multiple-equilibria pricing: whenever π > 0, the domestic nominal interest rate must exceed the world rate **even while the peg is still holding**, since UIP requires domestic bond-holders to be compensated for the expected capital loss if a crisis (and consequent depreciation) hits next period. A rise in domestic credit — which raises next period's crisis probability, since shocks are serially correlated — therefore raises *today's* domestic interest rate even with the exchange rate literally unchanged. Directly extends this map's existing [[uncovered_interest_rate_parity]] and [[currency_crisis_warning_signs]] entries with a precise mechanism for *why* interest-rate spreads widen visibly before a currency crisis materializes, not just after — a genuinely useful real-time monitoring implication (elevated onshore-offshore or policy-rate spreads can reflect self-fulfilling crisis risk being priced in, independent of any visible reserve decline yet). *(rational_self_fulfilling_bpc)*
- **[[policy_contingent_indeterminacy_mechanism]]** — the precise reason indeterminacy requires a *policy regime change contingent on the crisis itself*, not just "pessimistic expectations" in the abstract: Obstfeld shows explicitly (Section I) that under the *same* stochastic credit process, if the central bank is expected to keep behaving exactly as before regardless of whether a run occurs, runs are **probability-zero events** — any investor who thinks everyone else will attack still has no incentive to join, because a successful attack would only ever cause the currency to *appreciate* (reserves in excess of the collapse threshold get returned to a stronger footing), never depreciate, under an unchanged credit rule. Self-fulfilling crises only become possible once the private sector believes the government's *own reaction function* changes discretely at the moment of collapse (here: switching to accommodating/inflationary financing, plausibly because reserve loss forces recourse to money creation once external borrowing dries up — a policy-credibility channel, not a pure sunspot). This is a substantive qualification worth remembering before invoking "self-fulfilling crisis" loosely: the multiplicity requires a specific, economically motivated expectations-of-policy-response channel, not merely "beliefs matter." *(rational_self_fulfilling_bpc)*
- **[[extrinsic_uncertainty_bank_run_analogy]]** — Obstfeld's own framing of where this result sits in the broader theory literature: formally analogous to Diamond & Dybvig's (1983) bank-run model (two equilibria — confidence and panic — both self-fulfilling because a bank fails if and only if there's a run), and an instance of "extrinsic uncertainty" / sunspot equilibria (Cass & Shell, 1983) — outcomes determined by beliefs about a randomizing device unrelated to fundamentals, rather than by fundamentals themselves. The explicit disanalogy Obstfeld flags is important: currency crises, unlike bank runs, are **not automatically self-ratifying** — a run only becomes self-fulfilling *if* the authorities are expected to validate it after the fact via a policy switch; absent that policy-contingency channel (Section I's baseline case), speculative behavior converges back to the purely rational, fundamentals-driven Krugman/Flood-Garber story. Useful cross-reference for [[currency_crisis_warning_signs]]'s explicit note that "an explicit debate exists between the 'fundamentals deteriorate first' school and the 'crises can strike sound economies via pure sentiment shift/contagion' school" — this paper is the formal origin of the second school's theoretical grounding, with the important caveat about *which* sentiment-shift channels can actually be self-fulfilling. *(rational_self_fulfilling_bpc)*

### #currency_crisis_dynamics (continued) — a third-generation model (Krugman, 1999/2000): balance sheets and the transfer problem

- **[[krugman_capitulation_on_self_fulfilling_crises]]** — a genuinely notable piece of intellectual history worth preserving in this map: Krugman had explicitly argued *against* the self-fulfilling view as recently as his own 1996 NBER paper "Are Currency Crises Self-Fulfilling?", on the grounds that apparent indeterminacy in [[rational_vs_self_fulfilling_crisis_distinction|second-generation models]] could usually be explained instead by a predictable secular deterioration in fundamentals. This paper opens with an explicit public reversal — "I hereby capitulate... I was wrong; Maury Obstfeld was right" — driven specifically by **contagion**: the 1997-98 crisis spread from Thailand to countries (Russia, Brazil) with essentially no direct trade or financial links, which Krugman argues cannot be explained without positing multiple equilibria and self-validating collapses in confidence triggered by events elsewhere. Useful, concrete confirmation (from inside the profession's own intellectual history) of why this map's [[currency_crisis_warning_signs]] entry flags an ongoing, unresolved "fundamentalist vs. self-fulfilling" debate rather than treating either camp as settled. *(bs_transfer_problem_financial_crises)*
- **[[moral_hazard_vs_diamond_dybvig_asian_crisis_debate]]** — the two competing explanations of the 1997-98 Asian crisis that Krugman explicitly rejects before proposing his own third model, both worth recording since they're referenced elsewhere in the crisis literature: (1) the **moral-hazard / "over-borrowing syndrome"** view (McKinnon & Pill, 1996; Corsetti-Pesenti-Roubini, 1998; Krugman's own earlier 1998 position) — implicit government guarantees to banks/cronies created a hidden fiscal deficit that fueled reckless lending, later revealed as bad loans; Krugman's counter-evidence is that *all* forms of Asian investment boomed pre-crisis, including unguaranteed direct equity/real-estate purchases, which the pure moral-hazard story can't explain. (2) The **financial-fragility / bank-run** view, most fully worked out by **Chang & Velasco (1998)** — see the dedicated entry for that paper elsewhere in this cluster — modeling the crisis as a Diamond-Dybvig-style self-fulfilling liquidity panic. Krugman's critique of (2): Diamond-Dybvig's cost of premature liquidation is *physical* destruction of half-finished capital, which barely resembles what actually destroyed Asian firms — mostly balance-sheet-driven recession and currency-depreciation-driven dollar-debt blowups, not literal asset cannibalization. This sets up his own model as explicitly trying to capture a more realistic "financial fragility" mechanism. *(bs_transfer_problem_financial_crises)*
- **[[balance_sheet_currency_mismatch_investment_channel]]** — the paper's central formal mechanism, and a genuinely different circularity from both Chang-Velasco-style liquidity panics and Krugman's own first-generation reserve-depletion story: entrepreneurs' ability to borrow and invest is capped by a **leverage constraint** (borrowing ≤ λ × wealth), and their wealth itself depends on the **real exchange rate** because part of their debt (F) is foreign-currency-denominated — a real depreciation directly inflates the domestic-currency value of that debt, destroying net worth. Combine this with a "transfer problem" real-exchange-rate equation (below) in which *higher* investment (via induced import demand) requires a *stronger* real exchange rate, and the two feed back on each other: lower expected investment → weaker currency (via the current-account-reversal/transfer-problem channel) → weaker balance sheets → less actual investment, validating the initial pessimism. When the feedback loop's slope exceeds 1 (high leverage λ, low import propensity μ, large foreign-currency debt F relative to exports X — precisely equation (10)'s three named risk factors), the model has **multiple equilibria**: a high-investment "good" equilibrium and a low-investment "bankrupt" equilibrium, with an unstable interior equilibrium separating them (Figure 1's now-canonical S-shaped diagram). Genuinely distinct from [[balance_of_payments_crisis_mechanics]] and [[rational_self_fulfilling_bpc|Obstfeld's mechanism]] in this map: no government reserves or fiscal deficit appear anywhere in this story — it's a pure private-sector balance-sheet/valuation feedback loop. *(bs_transfer_problem_financial_crises)*
- **[[transfer_problem_current_account_reversal]]** — Krugman's explicit, direct application of the classical Keynes-Ohlin "transfer problem" (1920s reparations debate) to the emerging-market sudden-stop context — and, per his own framing, a channel conspicuously *missing* from every prior formal crisis model (all of which were one-good models with no real exchange rate at all). The mechanism: when capital inflows reverse, a country must generate a large, corresponding current-account surplus; because the domestic marginal propensity to spend on domestic goods (1−μ) differs sharply from the foreign marginal propensity to spend on the same country's goods (≈0), achieving that swing in net exports requires either a substantial **real depreciation** (which switches domestic and foreign expenditure toward domestic goods) or a **recession** that compresses import demand via lower income — or, empirically, some mix of both, exactly as observed in Thailand's 1996→1998 ~18-point-of-GDP current account swing. This gives a genuine, quantifiable macro-mechanics grounding for *why* a "sudden stop" translates into currency collapse and/or recession, complementing rather than duplicating this map's existing [[trade_balance_capital_account_identity]] and [[capital_flow_surge_reversal]] entries — those describe *that* capital flow reversals cause crises; this paper's transfer-problem equation explains the *arithmetic necessity* of the depreciation/recession that results. *(bs_transfer_problem_financial_crises)*
- **[[currency_mismatch_vs_maturity_mismatch_distinction]]** — a sharp, policy-relevant contrast Krugman draws explicitly against the Chang-Velasco/Diamond-Dybvig tradition: his balance-sheet mechanism depends entirely on the **currency composition** of debt (foreign- vs. domestic-currency-denominated), not on its **maturity structure** (short- vs. long-term). This has a non-obvious policy implication he states directly: Chilean-style controls on *short-term* foreign borrowing (URR-type measures, already covered narratively in this map's [[capital_controls]] entry) would **not** protect a country from this particular crisis channel, since long-term foreign-currency debt is just as exposed to a real-depreciation-driven balance-sheet blowup as short-term debt — what matters is the currency of denomination, not the rollover schedule. His own prescription instead: discourage foreign-currency-denominated borrowing at *any* maturity, on the grounds that it imposes a negative externality (a systemic real-exchange-rate-sensitivity risk) that individual borrowing firms don't internalize. *(bs_transfer_problem_financial_crises)*
- **[[stabilization_dilemma_price_vs_quantity_adjustment]]** — Krugman's own crude model of the (heavily criticized) "IMF strategy" of defending currencies with high interest rates during the Asian crisis: if policy successfully holds the real exchange rate p fixed despite declining foreign willingness to lend, the transfer-problem arithmetic above means the current-account adjustment must instead come entirely through an **output contraction** (a quasi-Keynesian multiplier channel) rather than depreciation. But falling output *also* reduces entrepreneurs' wealth (via the profit share of income), which triggers the exact same balance-sheet-driven credit-collapse feedback loop as before — just running through quantities instead of prices. Krugman's blunt conclusion: "closing one channel for potential financial collapse... opens another" — there is no clean way to avoid the self-reinforcing collapse dynamic once leverage is high enough; the *only* choice policy actually controls is whether the damage shows up as currency collapse or as recession, not whether damage happens at all. Directly extends this map's [[monetary_fiscal_policy_mix_capital_mobility]] and [[fx_intervention_effectiveness_evidence]] entries with a balance-sheet-specific reason why high-interest-rate currency defense in an EM crisis can be self-defeating even when it succeeds at its stated goal. *(bs_transfer_problem_financial_crises)*
- **[[garber_generational_taxonomy_critique]]** — a valuable epistemic caution worth flagging alongside the "first/second/third generation" labels this map has adopted for organizational convenience (following Eichengreen-Rose-Wyplosz, 1995): Peter Garber's discussant comment in the same volume calls the generational framing itself a "name swindle," arguing that first- and second-generation models were developed as "twins... almost simultaneously" in the 1980s rather than as a historical progression, that the 1980s single-equilibrium literature was never as narrowly "domestic-credit-only" as the stylized textbook retelling suggests, and that a third "generation" is premature marketing rather than a genuine theoretical advance. Worth keeping in mind precisely because this map itself uses the generational scaffolding (via the CFA/Krugman-textbook framing) as an organizing device — Garber's critique is a useful check against treating "which generation is this crisis" as a more settled, well-defined question than the field's own founders actually consider it to be. *(bs_transfer_problem_financial_crises — Comment by Peter Garber)*

### #currency_crisis_dynamics (continued) — the other third-generation model (Chang & Velasco, 1998): open-economy bank runs

- **[[open_economy_diamond_dybvig_illiquidity_model]]** — the paper's core framework, and the fully-worked-out version of the model Krugman's 2000 entry above only summarizes secondhand: an open-economy extension of Diamond & Dybvig's (1983) classic banking model. Domestic agents face privately-known "type" uncertainty (impatient — values only period-1 consumption — vs. patient — values only period-2 consumption), and a commercial bank pools their endowments plus foreign borrowing to fund a mix of a liquid world-market asset (1-for-1 return) and an illiquid but high-yielding long-term domestic technology. Demand deposits let the bank implement the socially optimal risk-pooling allocation via an incentive-compatible contract — but by construction leave the bank holding fewer internationally liquid assets than its potential short-term liabilities, creating exposure to a self-fulfilling run exactly as in the closed-economy original. This is the rigorous open-economy microfoundation behind the "financial fragility" school Krugman contrasts with his own balance-sheet mechanism in [[moral_hazard_vs_diamond_dybvig_asian_crisis_debate]] above. *(financial_crises_emerging_markets)*
- **[[bank_illiquidity_necessary_and_sufficient_condition]]** — the paper's central formal result, stated with unusual precision for this literature: a self-fulfilling bank-run equilibrium exists **if and only if** the bank is internationally illiquid, defined precisely as potential short-term obligations (total promised period-1 withdrawals) exceeding the maximum liquidation value of its assets net of external debt service. If illiquid, there are exactly two equilibria — the honest one (patient depositors wait, socially optimal) and the run one (everyone claims to be impatient, the bank fails) — with which one obtains genuinely indeterminate and driven by "extraneous" beliefs (a formal echo of [[self_fulfilling_multiple_equilibria_crisis]] from Obstfeld 1984, now grounded in banking microstructure rather than a government policy-reaction-function). If liquid, runs are impossible regardless of sentiment — a clean, falsifiable criterion (unlike vaguer "fragility" language) for when self-fulfilling panics are even a live possibility. *(financial_crises_emerging_markets)*
- **[[foreign_creditor_panic_ongoing_lending]]** — an extension distinguishing *domestic depositor* runs from *foreign creditor* runs, and showing the two can compound each other: whether the bank remains liquid enough to survive a domestic run depends critically on whether foreign lenders keep extending short-term credit *during* the panic ("ongoing lending"). If foreign creditors preemptively refuse to roll over short-term loans — a decision that is itself rational and self-fulfilling if they correctly anticipate the bank would otherwise fail to repay them — the bank's effective liquidation ceiling shrinks, which can convert an otherwise-viable bank into an illiquid, run-vulnerable one. The striking implication: "by withholding additional funds the creditors may provoke a crisis, and by providing them they may avoid it" — international lenders' collective behavior is not merely a response to fundamentals but an independent coordination problem layered on top of the domestic depositor game. *(financial_crises_emerging_markets)*
- **[[short_term_debt_maturity_fragility_channel]]** — a direct, quantified result on debt maturity that is genuinely complementary to (not duplicative of) Krugman's [[currency_mismatch_vs_maturity_mismatch_distinction]] entry above: in *this* model — unlike Krugman's, where only currency composition matters — maturity is the first-order driver of fragility. A bank funded with short-term (one-period, must-be-rolled-over) foreign debt is strictly more vulnerable than an otherwise-identical bank funded at longer maturities, because a rollover refusal forces immediate repayment precisely when liquid resources are scarcest. Formally, a larger overall credit ceiling only *raises* crisis vulnerability to the extent the additional borrowing is short-term — a large volume of purely long-term foreign debt cannot by itself make a previously-safe bank crisis-prone. Directly rationalizes the empirical finding (cited from Sachs-Tornell-Velasco 1996b, already gestured at elsewhere in this map) that the *maturity* structure of capital inflows, not their absolute size, best predicted vulnerability to the Tequila effect — and gives a clean theoretical case for maturity-lengthening capital control policies (e.g. Chile- and Colombia-style unremunerated reserve requirements, already in this map's [[capital_controls]] entry) as "all gain, no pain" in this specific model, a sharper and more optimistic verdict than Krugman's own skepticism of maturity-focused controls in his companion piece. *(financial_crises_emerging_markets)*
- **[[financial_liberalization_fragility_tradeoff]]** — formalizes, with two concrete policy experiments, the Kaminsky-Reinhart-style stylized fact already referenced in this map (via [[currency_crisis_warning_signs]]) that financial liberalization frequently precedes banking crises. (1) **Lowering reserve requirements** away from "narrow banking" (100%-liquid-backing) raises expected welfare *conditional on no run* — narrow banking is shown to be a costly, inefficient way to eliminate run risk entirely — but strictly enlarges the parameter region in which runs become possible. (2) **Increasing banking-sector competition** (moving from a monopoly bank to perfectly competitive banking) similarly raises depositor welfare absent a run, since a monopolist extracts rents by suppressing promised payouts, but a monopoly bank's lower promised payments and higher retained earnings make it *systematically less vulnerable to runs* than a competitive one — so pro-competition banking reform, like reserve deregulation, is a genuine welfare-vs-stability tradeoff, not a free lunch. Both results give a precise micro-mechanism for why 1990s-style financial liberalization (Mexico's reserve requirements cut to zero on peso deposits; competitive banking-sector reforms across Latin America and Asia) can be simultaneously good policy *and* a crisis-vulnerability-increasing one — not a contradiction, just two sides of the same coin. *(financial_crises_emerging_markets)*
- **[[endogenous_fundamental_value_asset_crash]]** — a conceptual point with real bearing on how to interpret any post-crisis asset-price collapse (equities, real estate), and a direct rebuttal-in-advance of the moral-hazard/"asset bubble" reading Krugman (1998) gave the Asian crisis, which Chang & Velasco explicitly contest: in a model with multiple equilibria, an asset's "fundamental value" is *itself* conditional on which equilibrium (run or no-run) obtains — it is not a single well-defined number that a crash simply "reveals." If a self-fulfilling run forces illiquid banks to liquidate long-term investments prematurely, the resulting lower asset prices reflect *inefficient forced liquidation*, not a return to some pre-existing true value; a higher price (and higher welfare) would have prevailed had the run never occurred. Practical caution directly relevant to reading any EM asset-price boom-bust cycle: a pre-crisis price rise is not automatically evidence of an "asset bubble," and a post-crisis price crash is not automatically evidence that prices are "correcting" to fundamentals — both readings assume away the very equilibrium-selection problem this paper's model is built to capture. *(financial_crises_emerging_markets)*
- **[[currency_board_vs_lolr_crisis_relocation]]** — the paper's sharpest policy result, and a precise, model-based version of the "twin crises" idea already in this map (via [[balance_of_payments_crisis_mechanics]] and Krugman's textbook treatment): comparing three exchange-rate/monetary arrangements holding the underlying illiquidity condition fixed. (1) **Currency board** (central bank never extends domestic credit) — the exact same illiquidity condition that triggers a bank run in the non-monetary baseline triggers one here too; the peg itself never breaks (the 1995 Argentina deposit-run episode, survived only via emergency World Bank/IADB capital infusions, is the cited real-world case), but the banking system can still collapse. (2) **Fixed exchange rate with the central bank acting as lender of last resort** in domestic currency — this *eliminates the bank run* (depositors can always get pesos), but under exactly the same illiquidity condition, the fixed parity itself now collapses instead, because "although the Central Bank can print pesos, it cannot print the dollars that are effectively needed" to back the resulting peso demand — a precise mechanism-level statement of why LOLR support under a peg mutates a bank run into a balance-of-payments crisis. Chang & Velasco's blunt punchline: **the vulnerability to *a* crisis is identical across both regimes — only the form the crisis takes (bank run vs. currency crisis) differs.** (3) **Flexible rates plus a domestic-currency LOLR** (per their companion paper, Chang & Velasco 1998a) is their preferred combination, since it can avoid *both* failure modes for domestic-currency claims — with the explicit caveat that it still offers no protection against foreign creditors holding short-term dollar-denominated debt, who can still panic regardless of the exchange-rate regime. *(financial_crises_emerging_markets)*
- **[[international_lender_of_last_resort_case]]** — the paper's final policy extrapolation, directly following from the illiquidity-not-insolvency diagnosis: if the underlying problem is genuinely a self-fulfilling liquidity squeeze rather than bad lending/insolvency, then an *international* lender of last resort (an IMF-type facility) can raise welfare by injecting liquidity to prevent unnecessary forced asset liquidation — the standard moral-hazard objection to this is real but, Chang & Velasco argue, no different in kind from the moral hazard inherent in fire insurance or domestic deposit insurance, both of which are managed via contract design and monitoring rather than abolished outright. Useful as the explicit theoretical counterpoint to [[moral_hazard_vs_diamond_dybvig_asian_crisis_debate]]'s moral-hazard camp: this paper's authors argue the two diagnoses (moral hazard vs. illiquidity) call for opposite crisis-response policy (recapitalize/close banks vs. inject liquidity), so correctly distinguishing them in real time is a first-order practical question, not just an academic dispute. *(financial_crises_emerging_markets)*

### One-paragraph overview (for board use)

The exchange-rate bibliography covers the full standard arc of international-finance theory — from FX market mechanics and quoting conventions, through the interest-rate-parity/PPP family that links currencies to interest rates and inflation (now including the original UIP-failure econometrics, Fama 1984), to the two workhorse short-run models (Mundell-Fleming policy transmission and Dornbusch overshooting) and their long-run monetary counterparts — and grounds all of it in practitioner tools actually used to value currencies today (BEER, GSDEER, GSFEER) plus a now-comprehensive, multi-generational treatment of how currency crises build and break, spanning the field's original academic models (Krugman 1979, Flood & Garber, Obstfeld 1984) through both competing third-generation accounts of the Asian crisis (Krugman 2000, Chang & Velasco 1998). It also now covers currency-regime choice from first principles (Mundell's OCA theory), capital-flow management as an official policy doctrine (the IMF's institutional view and its empirical precursor), and the political economy of exchange-rate policy (Frieden's sectoral-interests framework). Its standout strengths are a genuinely deep, Brazil-specific thread on exchange-rate pass-through and BCB market operations, and an equally deep academic-theory-plus-case-study treatment of currency crisis dynamics and capital account policy. Its main remaining gaps are FX options/volatility and comparative EM depth beyond Brazil.
