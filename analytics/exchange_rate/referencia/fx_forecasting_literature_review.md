## Scope and method

This review has three parts. **Part I** asks why the canonical theories of exchange rate determination fit the data so unevenly. In plain terms, those theories are:

- **Purchasing power parity (PPP)** — a *goods-market* theory: currencies should adjust so that the same basket of goods costs the same everywhere once converted.
- **Uncovered interest parity (UIP)** — an *arbitrage* theory: a currency paying a higher interest rate should be expected to lose value by roughly the size of that interest-rate gap, so that no one can earn a free lunch by borrowing cheap and lending dear.
- **Monetary/asset models** — a *money-and-output* theory: the exchange rate is priced like any other asset, driven by relative money supply and output (GDP) between two countries and the market's expectations of where those are headed — distinct from PPP because it's forward-looking and asset-market-based rather than anchored to today's relative prices.
- **Current-account adjustment** — a *trade-flow* theory: the exchange rate reflects the balance of trade and investment flows in and out of a country, adjusting to keep that flow roughly balanced over time.
- **Portfolio balance** — a *stock-of-holdings* theory: the exchange rate reflects how willing global investors are to hold that country's assets (a stock, not a flow), and what extra return they demand for the risk of doing so.

Part I gives the fullest treatment to the **scapegoat theory** of Bacchetta and van Wincoop, since it is the mechanism that ties the other five together: the idea that when an exchange rate moves for reasons nobody can directly observe, the market still needs some story to tell, so it "blames" whichever visible macro variable happens to be behaving unusually at that moment — and which variable gets blamed shifts over time as different variables take turns behaving unusually. That shifting blame is what makes each of the five models above look unstable in the data even when the underlying economics hasn't changed.

**Part II** asks what the forecasting literature has actually tried in response to this instability — time-varying-parameter shrinkage and policy-rate-driven Markov-switching across structural models — and how much of an edge, if any, these deliver over the simplest possible benchmark: a **random walk**, i.e., just guessing that tomorrow's exchange rate will equal today's.

**Part III** asks a question none of the first ten papers can answer on their own, because none of them study Brazil: does the *strength* of these relationships — how well a given theory actually explains the data — differ by country? Two Brazil-specific papers, read in full, test this directly for USD/BRL.

Twelve papers sit in `analytics/exchange_rate/referencia/er_forecasting/`, acquired specifically to ground Part I's model descriptions (PPP, UIP, current account, portfolio balance, and the scapegoat section), Part II's model-uncertainty discussion, and Part III's country comparison. ## Part I — Why the canonical models fit unevenly

### 1 · PPP and the persistence puzzle

**Purchasing power parity (PPP)** is the simplest theory of what an exchange rate "should" be: convert prices into a common currency, and the same basket of goods should cost the same everywhere. If a coffee costs $3 in the US and R$15 in Brazil, PPP says the "fair" exchange rate is R$5 per US$1. **Absolute PPP** — the strict, literal version, where price levels are equal once converted — fails almost everywhere in the real world; a Big Mac is not the same price in dollar terms in São Paulo and Chicago, and rarely has been. **Relative PPP** is a softer version: it doesn't require price *levels* to match, only that the *gap* between two countries' inflation rates eventually gets offset by exchange-rate moves. This softer version holds up somewhat better in the data, but painfully slowly — Rogoff (1996) estimates that when the real exchange rate (the exchange rate adjusted for the price gap between two countries) drifts away from its PPP-implied level, it takes **3 to 5 years for half of that gap to close** (a "half-life" of 3–5 years). Rogoff himself calls this "too slow to be explained by nominal price stickiness alone" — nominal price stickiness being the well-known fact that posted prices change slowly — and it doesn't match how much real exchange rates jump around from month to month.

That mismatch — a relationship that is true on average over decades, yet swamped by noise in any given month — is itself a preview of the scapegoat mechanism developed in §6 below: a channel can be real and can still be completely invisible in the data most of the time.

A separate practical question is *which* basket of goods this comparison should even use. Most PPP tests — Rogoff's included — compare countries using a headline consumer price index (CPI), which mixes **tradable goods** (things that can be shipped and arbitraged across borders, like electronics or soybeans) with **non-tradable goods and services** (things that can't — a haircut, a restaurant meal, rent). That mix matters, because non-tradable prices are set by local wages and productivity, not by cross-border arbitrage: a country where productivity in the tradable sector is rising faster than in services will see its overall price level — and therefore its PPP-implied "fair" exchange rate — drift for reasons that have nothing to do with currency misalignment. This is the **Balassa-Samuelson effect**[^bs]: poorer countries, whose productivity gap with rich countries is concentrated in tradables, tend to have persistently cheaper non-tradables, which shows up as a systematically "undervalued" currency under any headline-CPI PPP test even when nothing about the exchange rate itself is actually out of line.

**Main implication:** PPP is a slow-moving anchor, not a forecasting tool — useful for judging whether a currency looks cheap or expensive over a multi-year horizon, useless for predicting next month's move, and biased by construction for any country whose income level differs a lot from its trading partners' (via Balassa-Samuelson).

### 2 · UIP — a sign that flips with the measurement horizon

**Uncovered interest parity (UIP)** is the textbook prediction that a currency paying a higher interest rate should be expected to *depreciate* by roughly the size of that interest-rate gap. The logic: otherwise, an investor could borrow in the low-rate currency and lend in the high-rate one, pocketing the rate gap as a profit — UIP says that gap is exactly offset, on average, by the high-rate currency's expected depreciation, so there's no expected free lunch. This is *not* the same claim as covered interest parity (CIP), where a forward contract locks in the future exchange rate and the arbitrage really is close to risk-free; UIP describes an *uncovered* position — no forward hedge — so the trade still carries genuine exchange-rate risk. UIP is a claim about the *expected* return being zero on average, not a claim that any single such trade is safe. Fama (1984) tested this with a regression (a statistical technique that estimates the direction and strength of a relationship between two variables) forecasting future currency moves from the current interest-rate gap. UIP predicts that regression's key coefficient should be a clean +1. Instead, Fama found it reliably *negative* across nine currencies — high-rate currencies tend to *appreciate*, not depreciate, the opposite of the textbook prediction. This became known as the "forward premium puzzle."

Why does a relationship this thoroughly rejected remain a standard building block of open-economy macro models? Isard's survey[^isard] traces the concept back to Keynes's interest-parity writings of the 1920s and lays out the honest answer: the empirical rejection is strongest at short horizons (up to about a year) and gets progressively weaker at horizons of five to twenty years — and, more tellingly, every attempt to replace UIP with an explicit model of the missing risk premium (the same portfolio-balance approach covered in §5 below) has only ever explained a small share of the gap. UIP survives less because it has been vindicated and more because nothing empirically better has taken its place.

Hacker, Karlsson and Månsson (2014, *International Review of Economics and Finance* 29) show this isn't one single, fixed anomaly — it depends on *which frequency of the data you're looking at*, i.e., whether you're watching fast month-to-month wiggles or slow multi-year trends.

Using a technique called **wavelet multiresolution analysis** (specifically "MODWT with a Haar wavelet" — in plain terms, a method that produces a series of increasingly "blurred" versions of the same chart, each one averaging away the fast noise so that only one particular *speed* of movement remains, from 1-month wiggles up to 32-month cycles) on monthly Swedish krona (SEK) exchange rates against seven currencies (USD, JPY, EUR, GBP, NOK, KRW, CHF) from the early 1990s to 2009, they decompose both the exchange rate and the interest-rate differential into six time scales and test two things at each scale separately: **Granger causality** (a statistical test for whether knowing the past values of one variable — here, the interest-rate gap — helps predict another — here, the exchange rate — beyond what the exchange rate's own history already tells you) and **impulse responses** (what happens to the exchange rate, going forward in time, after a one-off shock to the interest-rate gap). Two findings stand out:

- **Granger causality from the interest differential to the exchange rate strengthens with the time scale.** At the 1–2 month scale it's rarely significant; by the 16-month scale, the interest differential Granger-causes the exchange rate for 6 of 7 country pairs (monthly data) and 5 of 7 (quarterly data) at the 5% confidence level (a standard statistical threshold for "this is unlikely to be a fluke").
- **The sign of the impulse response flips.** At the 1–2 month scale, negative signs dominate: a higher interest rate goes with a *stronger* currency. At the 16-month scale, positive signs take over: a higher interest rate goes with a *weaker* currency.

**That means:** the same interest-rate gap can point to opposite outcomes for the exchange rate depending purely on how far out you're looking — it isn't that one horizon is "right" and the other "wrong," both patterns are genuinely present in the data at the same time, just at different speeds.

The short-run negative sign matches three textbook models of how capital moves react to interest rates in the near term: **Mundell-Fleming** (with capital mobile across borders, a higher domestic rate pulls in foreign money seeking the better yield, and that inflow bids up the currency); **Dornbusch overshooting** (prices are sticky in the short run, so a rate change first moves the exchange rate — often by more than its eventual long-run adjustment — before goods prices catch up and pull it back); and **portfolio balance** (§5 below — investors rebalance toward the higher-yielding currency's assets, which are now relatively more attractive, pushing that currency up). The long-run positive sign instead matches the **Fisher-hypothesis/monetarist** logic — this is the review's own synthesis connecting Hacker, Karlsson and Månsson's statistical result to established macro theory, not a claim made by the paper itself: a persistently (not just temporarily) higher interest rate more likely reflects the market pricing in higher expected future inflation, and higher inflation erodes a currency's purchasing power and value over the long run, so the two move together instead of in the offsetting direction UIP predicts.

The paper's own theoretical framing (Section 2, pp. 322–323) does make clear that these are not two competing theories fighting for the title of "correct" — they are both right, just at different horizons, and a regression run over any single horizon will average them into whatever coefficient happens to fall out. This is exactly the sign-flip pattern the "New Fama Puzzle" (Bussière, Chinn, Ferrara & Heipertz, NBER 2018) documents at the macro level — UIP's coefficient flipping positive in the post-crisis low-rate decade — and it is the empirical soil the scapegoat theory grows in: if the "true" relationship between a fundamental and the exchange rate genuinely depends on which slice of time you're asking about, then any fixed-weight model is guaranteed to look unstable, entirely independent of any confusion or learning problem.

**Main implication:** UIP's sign isn't "broken," it's horizon-dependent — a rate-differential signal that looks predictive at a 1-month horizon can flip meaning entirely by 16 months, so any UIP-based carry read needs to specify which horizon it's for.

### 3 · Monetary/asset models — Meese-Rogoff and the disconnect puzzle

**Monetary/asset models** treat the exchange rate like the price of any other asset: driven by relative money supply and output (GDP) between two countries, plus the market's expectations of where those are headed. In its simplest ("flexible-price") form, if a country's central bank expands the money supply faster than its trading partner's, that currency should depreciate roughly in proportion — the same logic as domestic inflation, just applied across borders. A "sticky-price" version (Dornbusch's overshooting model, name-checked above in §2) adds that goods prices don't adjust instantly, so a monetary shock first moves the exchange rate by *more* than its eventual long-run change, then lets it partially reverse as prices catch up.

The most famous stress test of this class of model is the **Meese-Rogoff puzzle**. Meese and Rogoff (1983)[^meese-rogoff] tested three versions of the monetary/asset model — flexible-price monetary, sticky-price (Dornbusch-style) monetary, and a portfolio-balance-augmented variant — against 1973–1981 monthly USD data for the dollar/pound, dollar/mark, dollar/yen, and a trade-weighted dollar basket. The test was stacked in the models' favor: they fed each model the *actual, realized* values of money supply, output, and the other explanatory variables *after the fact* — information no real-time forecaster would ever actually have at the time of the forecast. Even with that unfair advantage, **none of the three structural models beat a naive guess that tomorrow's exchange rate equals today's** (a "random walk") on mean-squared forecast error, at any horizon from 1 to 12 months. The result has never been decisively overturned in its original form: Cheung, Chinn and Garcia Pascual (2005)[^ccg] reran the same horse race on 1990s data with an expanded model set and found the same thing — still no structural model consistently beats the random walk. Engel and West (2005)[^ew] give the theoretical account for *why*: when the fundamentals that are supposed to drive the exchange rate are themselves close to a random walk and the future is discounted at a rate close to 1 (as is standard in present-value asset pricing), the model's own rational-expectations solution collapses to something almost indistinguishable from a random walk *by construction* — this isn't a mysterious anomaly to be explained away, it's close to a mechanical consequence of how present-value pricing works when the discount factor is near 1. Obstfeld and Rogoff (2000) framed the broader pattern as the **"exchange-rate disconnect puzzle"**: exchange rates are far more volatile than, and appear disconnected from, the very fundamentals theory says should drive them.

**Main implication:** this is the empirical backdrop the rest of the review is reacting to — if the textbook monetary model, fed the *actual* future values of its own inputs, still can't beat "no change," then no fixed-weight fundamentals model should be expected to forecast well either, and the scapegoat theory (§6) and the time-varying-parameter literature (Part II) are both, in effect, different attempts to explain why that's true rather than a flaw specific to any one model.

### 4 · Current account / BOP

**Current account / balance of payments (BOP)** theories tie the exchange rate to the flow of money in and out of a country from trade and investment — a country running a large trade deficit, for instance, should see its currency weaken to make its exports cheaper and its imports pricier, restoring balance over time. The clearest full working-out of this idea is Dornbusch and Fischer's 1980 model[^db80] — a different paper from the Dornbusch overshooting model discussed in §2/§3 above, despite the shared lead author — which lets a country's terms of trade (the relative price of what it exports vs. what it imports) adjust so that, as the country accumulates or runs down foreign assets through the current account, its exchange rate and terms of trade move together to keep goods and asset markets in equilibrium — a country running a surplus builds foreign assets, which raises domestic wealth and spending, which in turn requires the terms of trade to adjust so the goods market keeps clearing. One of the model's more counter-intuitive results: an anticipated *future* shock (say, an expected future export boom) can move the exchange rate and current account balance *today*, before the shock itself even arrives, because asset markets price in the anticipated future current-account path immediately, not just the current flow.

A direct empirical test of how this plays out across countries is Romelli, Terra and Vasconcelos (2016)[^romelli], a Banco Central do Brasil working paper using 1970–2011 data for developed and emerging economies. Their finding: more trade-open economies see *smaller* currency depreciations during sudden-stop episodes (a sharp reversal of capital inflows), and *larger* current-account/trade-balance adjustments during abrupt depreciation episodes — trade openness, in other words, lets a country absorb a shock through *quantities* (imports and exports adjusting) rather than forcing the exchange rate to do all the work. This is the same openness-dampening mechanism Souza, Mattos and de Lima find for Brazil specifically in Part III (§(b) below, for the commodity-price channel) — two independent papers finding that how open an economy is to trade changes how much of a shock its exchange rate has to absorb.

**Main implication:** the current-account channel works, but slowly and through trade *quantities* rather than a snap price adjustment — and how much of a shock it can absorb depends on the country's trade openness, not just the size of its trade imbalance, which is exactly why the same channel resurfaces so much more strongly for Brazil specifically in Part III (§(b) below).

### 5 · Portfolio balance

**Portfolio balance** theories instead focus on investors' *stock* of holdings rather than *flows*: how willing global investors are to hold a given country's assets, and what extra return (risk premium) they demand to hold more of a riskier or less-liquid currency's assets. Kallianiotis's survey[^kallianiotis] lays out the standard version of this model plainly: households in each country choose a portfolio split between domestic money, domestic bonds, and foreign bonds, and the exchange rate is whatever price clears all three markets at once — a depreciation raises the domestic-currency value of foreign-bond holdings, which raises wealth, which spills over into higher demand for money and bonds, and interest rates on both sides adjust until the system balances again. His own econometric test (a monthly USD/AUD regression, 1988–2019) finds the exchange rate significantly related to money supply and bond stocks, and — most strongly of the variables tested — to the domestic and foreign interest rates themselves.

The classic empirical test of *how much* this framework actually explains is Dooley and Isard (1983)[^dooley]: they split the expected currency return into an observable forward premium plus an unobservable exchange risk premium, and derive that risk premium's theoretical drivers — budget deficits, current-account imbalances, official FX intervention — from the portfolio-balance framework itself. Their estimated risk premium, however, explains only a small part of the actual gap between forward premiums and realized exchange-rate changes, implying that most observed FX moves were unanticipated news rather than a risk premium the portfolio-balance model can account for. Isard's UIP survey (§2 above) cites this same result as the reason economists have never found a fully satisfying replacement for UIP: the leading risk-premium candidate model simply doesn't explain enough of the data to serve as one.

A modern reincarnation of the same stock-of-holdings logic replaces asset-*supply* shocks with demand-side heterogeneity: Evans and Lyons's (2002) order-flow model treats the actual buy/sell trades passing through dealers — as distinct from public news or scheduled data releases — as the variable that clears the market moment to moment, and finds it explains a striking **R² above 50–60%** of daily FX changes, far outperforming the fundamentals models that failed Meese-Rogoff (§3). Fratzscher, Sarno and Zinna's order-flow data (§6.2 below) is the direct empirical descendant of this market-microstructure approach (the study of how the mechanics of trading itself, not just fundamentals, move prices), applied there as a proxy for exactly the "unobserved fundamental" the scapegoat theory needs. Souza, Mattos and de Lima's (2021) BRL terms-of-trade findings are read in full for Part III below.

**Main implication:** portfolio balance is the theory that best explains why order flow and unobserved trading activity move exchange rates even when visible fundamentals don't — which is exactly the ingredient the scapegoat theory (§6, next) needs to explain why the market's *apparent* driver keeps shifting.

### 6 · The scapegoat theory — full treatment

**In one sentence:** when an exchange rate moves for reasons nobody can actually observe, the market still needs *some* story to tell — so it "blames" whichever visible macro variable happens to be behaving unusually at the time, even when that variable isn't the true cause. This collective blaming then shows up in the data as if the blamed variable really were driving the exchange rate, and the "blame" shifts over time as different variables take turns behaving unusually — which is exactly the instability the rest of Part I is trying to explain.

#### 6.1 The 2004 paper: the mechanism, in one page

Bacchetta and van Wincoop's starting observation (NBER WP 10245, presented at the AEA session in memory of Rudi Dornbusch, January 2004) is a "peculiar mismatch": academic consensus says macro variables barely explain exchange rates, yet market analysts routinely and confidently attribute a given move to a specific macro variable — and *change their mind about which variable* over time. The 1999 euro slide was blamed on US growth; by late 2003 the story had flipped to the US current account deficit, with *no growth data behind the flip* (Financial Times, Dec 1, 2003, quoted in the paper: "the movement of the US currency was no longer driven by growth fundamentals. All the focus is on the deficit now...").

Their model starts from a standard monetary exchange-rate model — PPP, money demand, and an interest-arbitrage/UIP condition — with a **risk premium** added on top (extra compensation investors demand for holding a currency exposed to unobservable buy/sell pressure), sized to however large the current unobserved liquidity trade happens to be. The one twist that drives the entire result: investors don't actually know how much weight money supply and output *should* carry in the exchange-rate equation — how strongly each fundamental's influence should persist. They only get private, noisy hints about that, and must combine those hints with whatever they can infer by watching the exchange rate itself move. In plain terms, the model says: *the exchange rate equals a baseline value, plus however much investors currently believe money supply's influence matters times the actual money-supply swing, minus the same logic for output, minus a term for the unobservable trading pressure.*

The mechanics of the confusion (their Section 3, pp. 6–9): suppose the exchange rate is high purely because of an unobserved liquidity sale that has nothing to do with money supply. If money supply also happens to be running unusually large that same period, an individual investor cannot tell the two explanations apart from the data alone — a high exchange rate is equally consistent with "a liquidity shock happened" and "money supply's influence is bigger than my private hint suggested." Because the *average* investor reasons this way, the market's collective belief about how much money supply matters gets pushed upward — even though **no single investor actually holds private information suggesting money supply's influence is genuinely high**. On average, the market ends up systematically wrong about how much money supply matters, specifically whenever a hidden trading shock coincides with money supply running unusually high at the same time. Money supply has become the scapegoat.

The paper derives one sharp result from this: how strongly a given hidden trading shock ends up reflected in exchange-rate moves rises with the *square* of how far money supply currently sits from its average — **the scapegoat effect only bites when the fundamental itself shows an unusually large deviation from its mean.** A fundamental sitting quietly near its average value can't become a scapegoat no matter how large the concurrent liquidity shock; you need both ingredients together — an unobserved shock *and* an observed variable that's out of its normal range. That two-part condition (a large unobservable shock coinciding with a large deviation in the candidate scapegoat) is exactly what later becomes the testable empirical hypothesis in Fratzscher et al. (2011), below.

#### 6.2 Fratzscher, Sarno & Zinna (2011): the first direct empirical test

Where the 2004 paper is theory, Fratzscher, Sarno and Zinna (ECB/Cass/Bank of England, January 2011) go looking for the mechanism directly in real market data — and this is the section that actually tests the 2004 story, so the data and method matter more here than anywhere else in Part I. **The data:** monthly Consensus Economics surveys of 40–60 FX market participants, each asked to rank six candidate fundamentals (short-term rates, long-term rates, growth, inflation, current-account/trade balance, equity flows) on a 0–10 importance scale, for 12 currencies (6 advanced: AUD, CAD, EUR, JPY, CHF, GBP; 6 emerging: CZK, MXN, PLN, ZAR, SGD, KRW) over 2001–2009 — this survey ranking is the direct, observable stand-in for "which fundamental the market currently thinks matters," precisely the thing the 2004 model treats as an unobservable belief. Paired with that: **proprietary UBS customer order-flow data**, the actual buy/sell trades passing through UBS's dealing desk, used as the empirical proxy for the model's unobservable liquidity/order-flow shock (the same order-flow logic developed in §5 above).

**The method:** a regression of the exchange-rate change on the six candidate fundamentals, each one weighted by how important that month's survey said it was (the "scapegoat weight"), plus order flow entered as its own separate regressor — estimated via a computational technique called Bayesian Gibbs sampling (a standard way of estimating a model's parameters when they can't be solved for directly; not important for the takeaway, only that it's a legitimate, widely used statistical method). This is tested against two benchmarks: a constant-parameter model (CP, one fixed weight per fundamental for the whole sample) and a time-varying-parameter model without the scapegoat term (TVP, weights drift but aren't tied to the survey data). Two results, both directly testing the theoretical predictions above:

- **Adding the scapegoat term dramatically improves in-sample fit.** Averaged across the 12 currencies, **adjusted R²** (R² measures what share of the exchange rate's moves the model explains — 0% means no explanatory power, 100% means perfect explanation; "adjusted" corrects for the fact that R² mechanically rises the more variables you throw in) rises from about 10% (TVP without scapegoats) to **37%** with scapegoats; the **directional hit ratio** (how often the model correctly predicts *which way*, up or down, the exchange rate will move, regardless of by how much) rises from ~60% to **~72%**. For some currencies the jump is stark — the euro, Swiss franc and South African rand go from essentially unexplained variance to a "much larger proportion" explained (see their Table 6 for currency-by-currency detail). Crucially, the improvement is **not just from order flow** — a version of the model with order flow but no scapegoat term recovers markedly less of the gain (their Figure 2), confirming that the survey-based scapegoat weights carry genuine independent information, not merely acting as a proxy for order flow itself.
- **The second hypothesis — when does a fundamental become a scapegoat — is confirmed with the predicted interaction structure.** Regressing the survey-based scapegoat weight on the absolute value of (order flow × fundamental deviation), interacted with an indicator that the fundamental is currently the largest of the six candidates, yields **positive and significant coefficients for all six fundamentals** — exactly the joint "large unobservable shock AND large fundamental deviation" condition the 2004 theory predicted, with R² (conditional on the indicator being active) of 20–38% for growth, inflation, and short-term rates. The short-rate differential dominates as scapegoat for advanced-economy currencies; growth and inflation dominate for emerging-market currencies — a genuinely new empirical fact not present in either theory paper.

The paper's own framing of the takeaway is worth quoting directly: the evidence is "remarkably robust, as it holds for all currency groups and for all macroeconomic variables in our sample" — a rare case in this literature of a theory's two distinct testable predictions both surviving contact with real, non-simulated market data.

**Main implication:** the scapegoat theory isn't just a plausible story — it's a survey-and-order-flow-verified mechanism, and it tells you *what to check* when USD/BRL's apparent driver shifts: is there a large recent move in some fundamental, coinciding with unusually heavy unobserved trading flow? If so, that's consistent with a scapegoat switch rather than a genuine change in what actually moves the currency.

## Part I takeaway — for USD/BRL and forecasting practice generally

- **No single fixed-weight model should be expected to track the exchange rate consistently — and this isn't a flaw in any one theory, it's close to unavoidable.** It isn't that PPP, UIP, or the monetary model are separately "wrong" and something else is "right." Any model that assigns fixed importance to a fixed set of variables will look unstable whenever (a) the true importance of those variables can plausibly shift a great deal over long horizons even while barely moving month to month, and (b) part of the exchange rate's movement comes from genuinely unobservable trading activity. Both conditions are close to a fact of life for real-world FX markets — not a special or unusual case, just how these markets actually work.
- **Two independent, purely statistical tests confirm that the *apparent* driver of exchange rates changes over time.** The UIP sign-flip found via wavelet decomposition (Hacker, Karlsson & Månsson) and the "New Fama Puzzle" coefficient flip both show, from raw data patterns with no story attached, that whatever variable seems to "explain" the exchange rate keeps changing.
- **The scapegoat theory supplies the causal mechanism behind that instability, and Fratzscher, Sarno and Zinna supply direct survey evidence that it operates exactly as theorized** — down to the level of *which* fundamental gets blamed in which kind of currency (short-term interest rates for developed-market currencies; growth and inflation for emerging-market ones).
- **The practical takeaway:** don't read a shift in the apparent dominant driver of USD/BRL as evidence that the market's *true* pricing model has changed. First check whether it's better explained as a scapegoat switch — a large, recent, unexplained move in some fundamental, coinciding with unusually large unobserved trading flow — before concluding anything structural has shifted. The flip side matters just as much: if a fundamental's dominance shows up *without* any large concurrent unobserved trading flow, that's evidence against a scapegoat switch — the scapegoat mechanism specifically requires both ingredients together (§6.1 above), so a clean shift with no unusual flow behind it is a better candidate for an actual structural change in what's driving the currency.

## Part II — Forecasting in practice: regime-switching, shrinkage, and model averaging

### (a) Time-varying-parameter shrinkage: Beckmann & Schüssler (2015)

Beckmann and Schüssler start from a diagnosis by Sarno and Valente (2009): maybe exchange rates forecast so poorly not because the right fundamentals are missing from the data, but because a forecaster using one fixed regression can't figure out *which* fundamentals matter *right now* — because the answer keeps changing. Their fix is a **shrinkage** architecture — "shrinkage," in forecasting, means deliberately pulling estimates toward a safer, simpler benchmark (here, the random walk) rather than trusting a complicated model at face value, precisely to avoid **overfitting** (building a model so tailored to the noise in past data that it performs worse, not better, on new data). Briefly, the machinery combines four ingredients: averaging across many smaller regressions instead of trusting one "kitchen-sink" regression with every variable at once (which mechanically shrinks the result toward the random walk); letting each regression's coefficients drift over time at a controllable speed rather than staying fixed; two flexible ways of blending multiple models' forecasts together, weighted by recent performance; and a final step combining all of that into one forecast. Applied to six US-dollar exchange rates (British pound, Japanese yen, German mark/euro, Canadian dollar, Swiss franc, Australian dollar; monthly, 1975–2013) against five candidate explanatory approaches (UIP, PPP, an interest-rate rule called a Taylor rule, and a monetary-fundamentals variable, plus a constant).

**The result, measured as out-of-sample R² relative to the random walk** (a version of R² that turns negative whenever a model does *worse* than the random walk, not just "explains less") **for 1985–2013: mixed and mostly negative at the 1-month horizon, and consistently very negative at the 12-month horizon.** The unshrunk "use every variable at once" regression is a cautionary tale on its own — its 12-month-ahead R² is −108.9% for the pound and −83.7% for the German mark, meaning it is dramatically *worse* than simply guessing "no change," a vivid demonstration of how badly overfitting punishes an unshrunk forecaster. The shrinkage-based versions pull this back to roughly break-even (mostly between −1% and +1%), and the only currency with a genuinely encouraging, repeatable positive result is the **Japanese yen** (up to +0.73% at 1 month, +0.83% at 12 months, for the best-performing combination), with the **Swiss franc** showing a smaller positive edge at short horizons. Every other currency is either matched or beaten by the random walk.

Two further details matter for interpreting "shrinkage helped": first, the model's own estimated weight on the random-walk forecast is **very high throughout the whole sample — above 50% for every currency except the yen, essentially all the time** — meaning even the "successful" cases are mostly a heavily dampened version of "assume no change," not a confident, fundamentals-driven call. Second, a chart of which variables the model currently thinks matter (its "inclusion probabilities") shows genuinely fast regime shifts — for the Swiss franc, the weight on UIP and the weight on PPP move in opposite directions partway through the sample — which is itself a second, purely statistical echo of the scapegoat theory's central claim: the variable that matters keeps changing, and changes abruptly.

The authors' own conclusion, stated plainly: "(short-term) forecasting of exchange rates with macro fundamentals cannot systematically beat a naive random walk benchmark... The naive random walk model is frequently selected as the most appropriate model" — but they argue the flexible machinery is still worth having, because it reliably tells you *when and where* fundamentals-based information actually exists, instead of blindly forcing a fixed model onto data that doesn't support it.

### (b) Markov-switching across structural models, tied to policy rates: Hauzenberger & Huber (2019)

Where Beckmann and Schüssler let individual coefficients drift gradually and let arbitrary combinations of variables drop in and out, Hauzenberger and Huber take a more theory-disciplined approach. Rather than blending everything continuously, they force the model to fully switch between exactly four named, well-known theories at any point in time — Taylor-rule fundamentals (interest rates set by a central-bank rule reacting to inflation and growth), the **long-run monetary model** (the plain monetary/asset model from §3 above, in its "no overshooting" flexible-price form: relative money supply and output alone determine the exchange rate, with no short-run sticky-price detour), PPP, and UIP — via a **Markov-switching process** (a formal statistical way of modeling "the world is in one of several discrete states, and there's a specific probability of moving between them each period"). Their key innovation: rather than fixing those switching probabilities as constants, they let them depend on the **current level of policy interest rates** at home and in the US, through a statistical technique called a multinomial logit (in plain terms, a formula that converts "how high or low are rates right now" into "how likely is the model to switch to each of the four regimes next period").

The motivating idea is direct: Taylor-rule-based exchange-rate models work well for describing currencies when central banks are actually setting rates according to a Taylor rule — but once rates hit the **zero lower bound (ZLB)** (the point where interest rates can't be cut much further below zero) and central banks turn to **unconventional monetary policy** (tools like quantitative easing, used when ordinary rate cuts run out of room), does the Taylor-rule story still fit? Instead of assuming this breaks at some arbitrarily chosen calendar date, the model lets the data — via the actual policy-rate level — decide when a switch away from the Taylor-rule regime becomes more likely.

Applied to eight currencies against the US dollar (Australia, Canada, Japan, Norway, South Korea, Sweden, Switzerland, UK; monthly data from the early 1970s to 2017, forecasting out-of-sample from 2005 onward), the estimated probability of being in each regime shows a strikingly consistent pattern across countries: **Taylor-rule and UIP fundamentals dominate before the 2008/2009 global financial crisis; monetary and PPP fundamentals gain support afterward**, for most of the eight currencies (Australia, Canada, South Korea, Sweden, UK show this clearly; Japan and Switzerland — both of which hit the zero lower bound much earlier, Japan already in the mid-1990s — show the reverse, drifting back toward Taylor-rule-consistent behavior; Norway's regime probabilities are noisier and don't split as cleanly into either camp). The model's estimated switching probabilities visibly track these regime shifts and move together with the actual policy-rate path, confirming this isn't a coincidence.

**For forecasting, there's an important split between two kinds of accuracy.** Point forecasts — literally, "what number will the exchange rate be" — are only marginally improved: the flexible model clearly beats the random walk for Australia, Canada, Norway, Sweden and the UK, especially in volatile periods, but South Korea, Switzerland and Japan see almost no improvement, and "close to zero when averaged over the whole sample" is the norm. **Density forecasts** — not just "what number," but "how confident should you be, and across what range of possible outcomes" — **show a much larger, more consistent edge.** That's because letting the model's uncertainty (not just its central guess) vary by regime captures swings in volatility that a single-number forecast simply can't get credit for; the scoring method used here rewards a forecast for correctly describing *how much* uncertainty there is, not just for guessing the central number, with the gap expressed in "log-points" — in plain terms, a bigger log-point gap means the winning model assigned meaningfully higher likelihood to the outcomes that actually happened, versus the random walk's assessment. The single largest gain is for Australia, where the full flexible model is the best performer across every forecast horizon tested (1, 3, and 12 months), scoring as much as 34.8 log-points better than the random walk at the 3-month horizon — a large, one-sided edge in that likelihood assessment. Japan is the one clear exception, where nothing beats a simple PPP model or the random walk outright.

The paper's own honest summary: "results appear to be rather mixed, with point forecasts being only slightly better than those obtained from standard models. For density predictions, by contrast, we observe pronounced accuracy increases for selected exchange rates" — echoing Beckmann and Schüssler's conclusion that these flexible, uncertainty-aware models earn their keep less by beating the random walk on a single number, and more by correctly describing when fundamentals genuinely carry information and when they don't.

---

## Part II takeaway

- **Two very different attempts to build "a model that changes when the world changes" reach the same verdict.** Time-varying-parameter shrinkage with flexible model blending (Beckmann & Schüssler) and policy-rate-driven Markov-switching across named theories (Hauzenberger & Huber) use completely different statistical machinery, yet arrive at a strikingly consistent conclusion.
- **Neither systematically beats the random walk on point accuracy for exchange-rate levels** — confirming that the Meese-Rogoff result still holds up four decades later, robust even to genuinely sophisticated nonlinear and Bayesian techniques.
- **Both find real value once the bar shifts from "guess the number" to "correctly describe the uncertainty":** Hauzenberger and Huber's large density-forecast gains from letting volatility (not just the average) vary by regime, and Beckmann and Schüssler's charts that correctly flag *when* fundamentals-based information exists, even when the resulting single-number forecast rarely beats a no-change guess.
- **The practical message matches the theory side exactly:** treat a fundamentals model as a tool for characterizing uncertainty and detecting regime change, not as a device for beating a coin-flip on next month's exchange-rate level.

## Part III — Does the story change for Brazil? Two USD/BRL deep dives

All ten papers behind Parts I and II study developed markets, a handful of diversified emerging markets (Mexico, South Korea, Poland, South Africa, and so on), or both pooled together — none isolates Brazil. That matters, because a theory that fits well "on average across a basket of currencies" can still fit very differently for one specific currency: a country's trade structure, capital-account openness, and central-bank behavior all shape *how strongly* a given fundamental shows up in the exchange rate. Two Brazil-specific papers, read in full below, test this directly for two of the theories already covered above — UIP (§2) and the terms-of-trade/current-account channel (§4/5) — and the answer is: yes, the strength (and even the *sign*) of the relationship is genuinely different for Brazil, in ways that are themselves further examples of the regime-dependence theme running through this whole review.

### (a) UIP for USD/BRL: Cieplinski, Braga & Summa (2017)

Recall from §2 that UIP predicts a currency with a higher interest rate should *lose* value by roughly the size of that rate gap, and that Fama (1984) found the opposite — a "forward premium puzzle" with an average coefficient across 75 published international estimates of **−0.88** (a meta-analysis of 75 separately published UIP regression estimates, compiled by Froot & Thaler, 1990, and cited in this paper). A general finding elsewhere in the literature (Bansal & Dahlquist, 2000; Frankel & Poonawala, 2010) is that **UIP's failure is typically milder in emerging markets** — coefficients closer to zero or even positive, not the sharply negative coefficients typical of developed-market tests.

Cieplinski, Braga and Summa test this directly on USD/BRL using monthly data from January 2000 to March 2014 (171 observations, all within Brazil's floating-exchange-rate/inflation-targeting era). Their headline result **contradicts the "milder in EM" generalization**: a **GARCH(1,1)** regression (a regression that also models how the *volatility* of the exchange-rate change itself evolves over time, instead of assuming it stays constant — useful here precisely because USD/BRL volatility swings so much across the sample) of the monthly BRL change on the interest-rate differential (Brazil's CDI rate over the US LIBOR) returns a coefficient of **−2.94** (statistically significant at the 1% level) — a larger, more sharply "wrong-signed" coefficient than the *international average* of −0.88, and well outside the range the EM literature had led researchers to expect. In other words, on this specific test, Brazil's UIP failure is not smaller than the developed-market norm — it's bigger.

But that single coefficient hides something the paper's second exercise reveals. Using **Markov-switching models** (formally: models that let the data itself decide when the economy has switched between a small number of discrete "regimes," each with its own coefficients — the same general technique behind Hauzenberger & Huber in Part II, applied here to a single country instead of across structural theories) applied to the same relationship, the authors find the interest-differential coefficient is **not one fixed number at all — it flips sign depending on how volatile the exchange rate currently is**:

- **In calm periods** (low exchange-rate volatility), the coefficient is negative — around **−1.49 to −1.59** across their two best-fitting specifications — consistent with ordinary carry-trade logic: a higher domestic rate attracts capital inflows and appreciates the currency.
- **In turbulent periods** (high exchange-rate volatility), the coefficient flips to **positive and greater than one** — **+6.79** in one specification, **+1.40** in another (both statistically significant) — the textbook-consistent UIP sign, but for the "wrong" reason. This is *not* carry-trade logic suddenly starting to work the way UIP predicts. It's reverse causality: Brazil's central bank *raises rates defensively* to stem capital flight and arrest a depreciation already underway, so a weakening currency and rising rates end up moving together in the data — coincidentally landing on UIP's textbook sign, without actually validating UIP's mechanism (higher rate → capital inflow → appreciation).

The model's own regime-dating exercise identifies the "turbulent" months as exactly the episodes anyone following USD/BRL would expect: May–September 2002 (the pre-election confidence crisis, contagion from the Argentine default), August–November 2008 (the global financial crisis), and September 2011 (the European sovereign-debt flare-up) — with the June 2013 "taper tantrum" the one high-volatility episode their model does *not* flag, an honestly-reported miss. Once this regime-switch is allowed, the country-risk variable (EMBI+ Brazil) itself stops being statistically significant — the volatility-regime switch in the interest-differential coefficient alone is enough to explain the dynamics, without needing country risk as a separate driver.

The authors' own interpretation leans on a heterodox "exogenous interest rate" framework (Lavoie; Serrano & Summa) rather than mainstream portfolio theory: they argue the central bank *sets* the policy rate as a matter of choice, constrained by Brazil's position in the international financial system (it cannot issue the world's reserve currency), rather than the rate being pinned down by an automatic no-arbitrage mechanism. Under that view, the negative coefficient in calm times reflects the "normal" channel (a rate hike draws in capital, appreciates the currency); the positive coefficient in crises reflects a policy *reaction* to a capital outflow already happening, not causation running the other way.

**What this adds to the country-comparison question:** the general EM-vs-DM finding noted above frames UIP failure as something that's typically *weaker* for emerging markets. Brazil, tested directly and in isolation, shows the opposite in its full-sample estimate — and the reason is not that Brazil is simply "a bigger anomaly," but that pooling all months together forces one fixed coefficient to stand in for two genuinely different, opposite-signed regimes (calm-period carry-trade logic vs. crisis-period defensive rate hikes). The full-sample GARCH(1,1) estimate is a different model specification from the regime-split one, not a simple average of the two regime coefficients — which is exactly why it can land outside the range either regime alone would suggest, rather than settling somewhere between them. That is the same lesson the wavelet-decomposition paper (Hacker, Karlsson & Månsson, §2) drew for Sweden using a completely different technique (splitting by *time scale* rather than by *volatility regime*) — a single fixed-weight coefficient estimated over one undifferentiated sample can misrepresent a relationship that is genuinely regime-dependent underneath.

### (b) The terms-of-trade/commodity-currency channel for USD/BRL: Souza, Mattos & de Lima (2019/2021)

The terms-of-trade/commodity channel is, per the paper reviewed below, the strongest evidence found anywhere in this review for a Brazil-specific fundamentals relationship — stronger than the textbook current-account-deficit channel described in §4 above. Souza, Mattos and de Lima's essay (the basis of the peer-reviewed 2021 *International Journal of Finance & Economics* paper) is the primary source behind that claim, and it is worth walking through in full because it does something none of the ten Part I/II papers do: it tests whether *structural* country characteristics (how open the economy is to trade) and *financial* country characteristics (how risky international investors currently perceive it to be) change the size of a fundamentals relationship — exactly the kind of country-specific explanatory-power question this section is about.

Using monthly data from January 1999 to January 2018 (228 observations, Brazil's entire floating-exchange-rate period), the authors first establish a long-run relationship (via DOLS, a cointegration-based estimator well suited to variables that drift together over time) between Brazil's real effective exchange rate and a real commodity-price index built specifically from Brazil's own export basket (soybean, iron ore, oil, sugar, coffee, beef, and chicken meat, weighted by their historical share of Brazilian exports). The headline elasticity: **a 1% rise in real commodity prices is associated with a 1.222% real appreciation of the BRL** (statistically significant at the 1% level, adjusted R² = 0.797) — an elasticity *greater than one*, meaning the BRL moves more than proportionally with the commodity cycle, not less. A version of the index excluding oil gives an almost identical result (1.308%, adjusted R² = 0.798).

The paper then asks exactly the country-differentiating question this section poses: does this elasticity depend on structural features of the Brazilian economy?

- **Trade openness dampens the relationship, as theory predicts.** The interaction term between commodity prices and Brazil's trade-openness measure (imports plus exports, over GDP) is negative and significant (coefficient −0.246) — in periods when Brazil trades more with the rest of the world relative to its GDP, the BRL's sensitivity to commodity swings is measurably smaller. The mechanism, worked out in Chen & Lee's (2014) theoretical model: a commodity boom raises a country's income, and if a large share of what people spend that extra income on is non-traded (services, housing, domestically-produced goods that can't simply be imported to compete the price rise away), those non-traded prices get bid up — and, via the same logic as the Balassa-Samuelson effect (§1 above), a higher relative price of non-tradables shows up as a real appreciation. Trade openness is used as a proxy for a *smaller* non-tradables share, on the theoretical premise that a more trade-open economy consumes more imported goods and produces more for export rather than for domestic-only consumption, so commodity income has less non-traded basket to bid up. **Worth flagging as a genuine caveat, not just theoretical color:** this is a proxy relationship the theory asserts, not something the paper measures directly. A rise in trade openness could in principle just mean *more volume of the same categories* of goods already being traded (Brazil exporting and importing more of what it already does, at greater scale) rather than a shift toward a more diversified, less non-tradables-heavy consumption basket — in which case the production would simply be exported rather than consumed domestically either way, and the "smaller non-tradables share" logic wouldn't actually be doing the work. The paper's own test only confirms that the interaction coefficient's *sign* matches the theory's prediction within Brazil's own time series; it doesn't independently verify that trade openness actually correlates with a smaller non-tradables consumption share for Brazil specifically.
- **Commodity export dependency does not**, contrary to the same theory's prediction — the interaction term for how large a share of Brazil's own exports are commodities is statistically insignificant. The authors' explanation: Brazil exports a genuinely diversified basket of seven different commodities (soybean, iron ore, oil, sugar, coffee, beef, and chicken meat) rather than depending on one dominant one. Contrast a single-commodity oil exporter, where a swing in that one commodity's price *is* essentially the entire terms-of-trade story for the country — the "export concentration" channel needs that kind of concentration to bite, and Brazil's spread-out export mix simply doesn't have it.

The short-run analysis adds the financial dimension. Using a smooth-transition regression (a model that allows the relationship to shift gradually between two "regimes" as some other variable crosses a threshold, rather than switching abruptly), the authors test two candidate triggers for non-linearity: global financial-market volatility (the VIX) and Brazil's own country-risk premium (the EMBI+ spread). **Only country risk matters — VIX does not.** In "calm" months (EMBI+ close to unchanged), commodity prices have **no significant short-run effect** on the BRL at all. In "crisis" months (EMBI+ swinging sharply), a 1% rise in commodity prices is associated with a **0.979% appreciation, significant at the 1% level** — the commodity-currency relationship essentially switches on only when country risk is moving a lot. In plain terms: day to day and month to month, commodity prices don't move the BRL in any statistically detectable way — the relationship only activates during episodes when Brazil's own country risk is itself swinging sharply, a meaningfully more conditional story than "commodity prices always matter for BRL." The model's own crisis-dating exercise correctly flags Brazil's best-known currency-stress episodes — April 1999, June–July 2002, October 2008, and March 2016 — with only the June 2013 taper tantrum as a partial miss, mirroring almost exactly the same regime-dating exercise (and the same near-miss) in the UIP paper above.

A robustness check adds the interest-rate differential and international reserves as controls, and turns up one more country-specific wrinkle — though it takes unpacking a sign-convention difference to see that it's not actually inconsistent with the Cieplinski/Braga/Summa paper above. This paper's dependent variable is the *level* of the real effective exchange rate (reer), defined so that a *higher* reer means a *stronger*, appreciated real; Cieplinski, Braga and Summa's dependent variable, by contrast, is the *change* in the nominal BRL/USD rate, where a *higher* value means the BRL is *weakening*. That's the opposite convention, so "negative coefficient" means opposite things in the two papers: negative in Cieplinski/Braga/Summa signaled appreciation (the Fama-puzzle "wrong sign," §2 above); negative here signals depreciation. With that in mind: the interest-differential coefficient in this robustness check comes out **negative** — in the authors' own words, "contrary to the expected, the coefficient... has a negative sign, indicating that an increase in the real interest differential is associated with a real exchange rate depreciation" — the opposite of the textbook UIP-consistent sign (theory expects a *positive* coefficient here: higher rates should attract capital and appreciate the reer). The authors attribute this to reverse causality: in emerging markets, interest-rate hikes are often a defensive *response* to capital outflows and depreciation pressure already under way (Shousha, 2016), not an independent driver of appreciation. That reverse-causality explanation is the authors' own; the parallel to Cieplinski, Braga and Summa's *crisis-regime* finding is this review's own bridge between the two papers, not a comparison either paper draws itself — but once the sign-convention difference described above is accounted for, it is essentially the same underlying story: higher rates and a weaker currency end up moving together in the data, for the same reverse-causality reason in both papers.

**What this adds to the country-comparison question:** the terms-of-trade channel is far more central to Brazil's currency story than the modest, low-frequency current-account effect §4 above describes for the general DM/EM literature — and its strength is itself conditional on two country-specific factors (trade openness structurally, country risk cyclically), not a fixed number. That an elasticity above one, dampened by trade openness and switched on by country risk, is the single strongest and most country-specific result in this whole review is itself direct evidence that "how well a theory explains the data" is not a universal constant — it depends on the country you ask the question about.

### Part III takeaway

- **Explanatory power genuinely differs by country, in both directions.** Brazil's full-sample UIP failure (−2.94) is *larger*, not smaller, than the international average (−0.88) — directly contradicting the general "UIP failure is milder in EM" finding reported elsewhere in the literature (§2). Meanwhile, the terms-of-trade/commodity channel — a modest, low-frequency effect in the developed-market-focused current-account literature of §4 — is, per the paper reviewed here, the single strongest and most robust fundamentals relationship found anywhere in this review for Brazil specifically (elasticity above 1).
- **In both cases, the reason a single country-specific coefficient can mislead is the same reason a single cross-country-pooled coefficient can mislead: regime-dependence.** Brazil's UIP coefficient is really two coefficients (calm vs. crisis) with opposite signs; Brazil's commodity elasticity is really "no effect" in calm months and "large effect" in crisis months. Averaging over the regime, as a single full-sample coefficient necessarily does, produces a number that describes neither regime accurately.
- **The trigger variable in both Brazil papers is the same: country risk (EMBI+), not global volatility (VIX).** This is a genuinely Brazil-specific (or at least EM-specific) finding — it says the switch is driven by how risky *Brazil itself* currently looks to international investors, not by generic global risk-off sentiment. That is a sharper, more actionable signal than "watch the VIX."
- **Practical implication for USD/BRL:** don't apply a single textbook coefficient — for UIP or for the commodity channel — uniformly across calm and crisis periods. Both papers say, independently, that the right question during any given month is which regime Brazil is currently in, with the EMBI+ spread as the most informative single gauge of that regime shift.

## Overall synthesis

- **Theory (Part I) explains why no fixed-weight fundamentals model should be expected to track the exchange rate consistently.** The scapegoat mechanism shows that a fully rational market, honestly trying to learn from imperfect information, will *necessarily* produce a wildly unstable relationship between fundamentals and the exchange rate in the data — even when the true underlying relationship barely moves month to month — purely because (a) each theory's own weight on the fundamentals it cares about could plausibly shift a great deal over long horizons, and (b) part of the exchange rate's movement is genuinely unobservable.
- **Practice (Part II) shows what happens when forecasting machinery takes that instability seriously.** Time-varying-parameter shrinkage and policy-rate-driven Markov-switching both reliably *detect* the instability the theory predicts — but neither converts that detection into a durable edge on point forecasts, the same result Meese and Rogoff reported in 1983 (§3 above).
- **Country matters, not just theory and practice.** The two Brazil-specific papers in Part III show that a single global-average coefficient can misrepresent USD/BRL in either direction — Brazil's UIP failure is *larger* than the international average, not smaller as the general EM literature suggests, while the terms-of-trade/commodity channel is *stronger* for Brazil than the modest, low-frequency current-account effect described for the general DM/EM literature in §4. Both country-specific results are themselves regime-dependent, switching on and off with Brazil's own country-risk premium (EMBI+) rather than with global risk sentiment (VIX) — the same instability theme from Parts I and II, one level more granular.
- **The practical implication: build a dashboard, not a point forecast.** Together, the two literatures are the strongest argument available for tracking fundamentals, flows, and positioning side by side rather than betting on a single model's next-month number — exactly the shape `analytics/exchange_rate/` (the Panorama Cambial) already takes.
